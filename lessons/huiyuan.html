生平 Life and World

An Era of Collapse

Huiyuan grew up in an era of collapse. The Han dynasty had fallen in 220 CE, and China was fractured into competing kingdoms, many ruled by non-Chinese dynasties in the north. The old Confucian order, which tied scholarship, governance, and ritual propriety into a single system, was shattered. In this vacuum, Buddhism offered something Confucianism could not: a systematic account of suffering, a path to transcendence, and institutions (monasteries) that could survive the fall of any particular state.

From Confucianism and Daoism to Buddhism

He came to Buddhism through Confucianism and Daoism. Before encountering the Dharma, Huiyuan studied the Confucian classics and was deeply versed in Daoist texts, specifically the Laozi and the Zhuangzi. This dual training was not unusual for his era, but it shaped his distinctive approach: he could argue for Buddhist principles in language that Confucian and Daoist intellectuals understood. When his teacher Dao'an lectured on Buddhist prajna (wisdom), Huiyuan reportedly used analogies from the Zhuangzi to help the audience grasp the concepts. This made him a bridge figure, not just translating Indian ideas but rethinking them in Chinese philosophical terms.

Dao'an: The Master Who Shaped Him

Dao'an was the master who shaped him. As the most prominent Buddhist monk in fourth-century China, Dao'an had organized the first systematic catalog of Chinese Buddhist texts and standardized monastic rules. Anticipating the political turmoil of the advancing Former Qin state, Dao'an dispersed his community to ensure the survival of the Dharma. He instructed Huiyuan to go south. Huiyuan obeyed, eventually reaching Mount Lu (Lushan) in 381 CE. He would remain there for the rest of his life, formally establishing Donglin Temple (East Grove Temple) on the mountain in 386.

Mount Lu as Fortress

Mount Lu became his fortress. From Donglin Temple, Huiyuan created the most important Buddhist center in southern China. He famously never crossed the stream at the base of the mountain, a self-imposed boundary that became legendary. This was not a withdrawal from the world, however. From his secluded perch, Huiyuan corresponded with the most powerful figures in China, mediated between northern and southern Buddhist traditions, and organized the White Lotus Society (Bailian she), a devotional community focused on meditation and the aspiration for rebirth in Amitabha's Pure Land.

The Bowing Controversy

The bowing controversy was the crisis that defined him. In 403 CE, the warlord Huan Xuan (who would briefly usurp the Jin dynasty throne) demanded that Buddhist monks bow before the ruler, just as all other subjects did. This was not a trivial request. In Confucian ritual, the bow (bai) was the fundamental act of political submission. For monks, who had symbolically "left the family" (chujia) and renounced worldly attachments, bowing to the emperor meant re-entering the hierarchical world they had abandoned. Huiyuan wrote On Why Buddhist Monks Do Not Bow Down before Kings (Shamen bu jing wangzhe lun) as a systematic defense of monastic exemption. The treatise was persuasive enough that Huan Xuan withdrew the demand.

Death on the Mountain

He died in 416 on his mountain. Huiyuan never left Mount Lu, but his influence radiated outward. The Pure Land tradition that traces its origins to his White Lotus Society would become the most widely practiced form of Buddhism in East Asia. His arguments for monastic independence set the terms for every subsequent debate about the relationship between Buddhism and the Chinese state, including Han Yu's famous anti-Buddhist memorial four centuries later.

概要 OVERVIEW
經文 The Reading Tiwald & Van Norden, pp. 69–80

Tiwald & Van Norden, Readings in Later Chinese Philosophy (Hackett, 2014), pp. 69–80. Part II: Chinese Buddhism. The introduction surveys Buddhism's arrival in China and the philosophical tensions it provoked. Selection 13 is Huiyuan's On Why Buddhist Monks Do Not Bow Down before Kings, a treatise defending monastic independence from state ritual authority.

Historical Moment

The Six Dynasties period (220–581 CE): civil war, political fragmentation, and intellectual upheaval after the fall of the Han. Without a centralized power enforcing Confucian orthodoxy, "unorthodox" teachings like Buddhism had a chance to flourish. By Huiyuan's time, Buddhism was the de facto state religion of several competing dynasties, heavily patronized by most emperors.

核心 The Core Problem

Should Buddhist monks bow to the emperor? This sounds like a question of etiquette. It is actually a question about the limits of state power. Confucian ritual demands that all subjects pay formal obeisance to the ruler. Buddhist monks claim exemption: they have left the "temporal world" and answer to a higher authority. Huiyuan's treatise is the most sophisticated defense of this exemption.

Why It Matters

  • First major Chinese Buddhist philosophical defense of monastic autonomy
  • Directly addresses the Confucian critique that monks undermine filial piety and social order
  • Sets up the confrontation that explodes 400 years later in Han Yu's "Memorial on the Bone of Buddha"
  • Raises questions about religion, state power, and individual conscience that remain alive today

背景 BUDDHISM IN CHINA: THE COLLISION
傳播 Why Buddhism Spread

Buddhism arrived during the Han dynasty but only gained mass following after the Han's collapse (220 CE). In an era of civil war and political fragmentation, Buddhism offered what the shattered Confucian order could not: a universal explanation of suffering, a path to individual salvation that did not depend on a functioning state, and institutions (monasteries, ordination lineages) that could survive the fall of any particular dynasty.

批評 Why Confucians Objected

Two flashpoints dominated public discourse. First, Buddhist monks refused to participate in the Confucian ritual order: they would not bow to rulers, honor ancestors with family sacrifices, or produce heirs. For Confucians, this undermined filial piety (孝 xiao) and the root of social order. Second, monasteries accumulated enormous wealth, land, and tax-exempt labor. Monks left the tax rolls. In Confucian eyes, Buddhism was parasitic on the state it refused to serve.

空 Emptiness and No-Self

The core Buddhist philosophical claims were radical. There is no permanent self (anatman). All things are "empty" (空 kong) of independent existence, meaning everything that exists is conditionally produced by causes and relationships. Mahayana Buddhism emphasized the "emptiness of all things, not just the emptiness of the self, but the emptiness of all concrete things, like trees and wagons, as well as abstract things, like time or evil" (Tiwald, p. 70). This was philosophically challenging for both Confucians (who grounded ethics in stable human nature) and Daoists (who posited a stable "Way").

緣起 Interdependence

The Buddhist argument from interdependence was particularly influential in Chinese Mahayana. If everything is conditionally produced, then your mental state right now exists because of an unbroken chain of causes extending through the editors of this textbook, Siddhartha Gautama, and every being who ever influenced them. "Some Buddhists would argue that this web of relationships is really what defines any one element in it" (Tiwald, p. 72). This reasoning justified universal compassion: if all things are interconnected, harming any being harms the whole web.

The Two Confucian Flashpoints

  • Monks leave their families: abandoning filial piety and the obligation to produce heirs
  • Monks refuse to bow to kings: rejecting the ritual hierarchy that binds ruler and subject
  • Both views "ran strongly counter to largely Confucian values of loyalty and filial piety that had become thoroughly interwoven with Chinese culture by the time that Buddhism began to attract large numbers of followers" (Tiwald, p. 74)
詞彙 KEY CONCEPTS FOR THE READING
出家 chujia "leaving the family"
The act of becoming a Buddhist monk or nun: shaving the head, casting off hairpins, donning monastic robes. For Confucians, this was a direct assault on 孝 xiao (filial piety), since it meant abandoning one's parents, producing no heirs, and severing the ancestral line.
化 hua "transformations"
Throughout the essay, "comply with the transformations" means participating in the cyclical changes of the temporal world: birth, death, political cycles, seasonal rhythms. Huiyuan's argument hinges on whether monks are obligated to "comply" with these worldly processes or are exempt because they seek something beyond them.
生 sheng "life" / "generating life"
Translated as "life" by Tiwald. Later Confucians insisted that 生生 "generating life" is the core value of the cosmos. Huiyuan argues that Buddhist monks, by understanding that "generating life depends on being subject to the transformations of the temporal world," seek to escape the cycle entirely through enlightenment.
宗 zong "ancestral source"
Translated ambiguously as "the First Principle." Originally Daoist, adopted by Chinese Buddhists to describe the universal ground or source of all things, from which enlightenment and ultimately salvation arise. Huiyuan uses this term to bridge Buddhist and Daoist frameworks.
本 ben "fundamental state"
Also translated as "root," "origin," or "foundation." In Huiyuan's era, this was equated with "Buddha-nature": one's original state as a fully formed capacity for Buddhahood. "We don't have full access to our own Buddha-nature" (Tiwald, p. 79, n. 14), which is why practice is necessary.
方便 fangbian "expedient means"
Sanskrit upaya. A technique of instruction based on adapting the teaching to the audience's level of understanding. The Lotus Sutra's parable of the burning house illustrates this: a father lures his children from danger with promises of toys, not explanations of fire. Different Buddhist "levels" (Hinayana, Mahayana) may be expedient means aimed at different capacities.
經文 ON WHY BUDDHIST MONKS DO NOT BOW DOWN BEFORE KINGS
導言 Introduction to the Treatise Selection 13 · pp. 75–80

Huiyuan's treatise responds to the political demand that monks show ritual obeisance to the emperor. He builds his defense in three stages, moving from the least controversial claim (Buddhist laypeople should obey the state) to the most radical (monks who have left the world need not comply with worldly authority at all). Translation by Justin Tiwald.

第一 Section 1: Buddhist Laypersons p. 76

Huiyuan begins by conceding ground. Buddhist laypersons (those who practice Buddhism without becoming monks) "are subjects who comply with the transformations of the temporal world." Their "basic feelings remain conventional and their outward behavior conforms with what's within the bounds of the temporal world" (p. 76). They maintain family obligations, perform ritual obeisance, and fit within the Confucian social order.

This is strategically important. Huiyuan is not arguing that all Buddhists are exempt from state authority. Only monks are. By making this concession first, he narrows the field of battle.

"Therefore they cannot withhold proper ritual deference [for a ruler] while receiving the benefits of his virtue, nor can they discard due reverence for him while soaking up the benefits of his kindness."Tiwald, p. 76
第二 Section 2: Buddhists Who Leave the Family pp. 76–77

Here is where the argument becomes radical. Monks have "left the family" (出家 chujia). They are "guests beyond the temporal world." Their entire purpose is to recognize that "they have a body, and that they can put an end to their suffering by not preserving the body" (p. 76). In other words, monks have rejected the value system in which bowing to a king makes sense. Their goal is to escape the cycle of birth and death (轮回 lunhui, samsara) entirely.

The symbolic markers are deliberate: shaving the head, casting off hairpins, donning monastic robes. These are visible rejections of the Confucian ritual code. "Because they change from ordinary customs, their clothing and insignia of rank cannot conform to the same ritual standards specified by secular codes" (p. 77).

"The Pattern [that they adhere to] is contrary to the needs of the physical body, and their Way is opposed to common practice."Tiwald, p. 77

Huiyuan argues that monks, by leaving the world, actually serve a higher purpose: they "rescue ordinary people from drowning in the flow of the temporal world and pull out the hidden roots from many eons [of past lives]" (p. 77). The monk's withdrawal is not selfishness. It is a form of service that operates on a different plane.

第三 Section 3: Seeking the Ancestral Source Without Complying with the Transformations pp. 78–80

The final and most philosophically complex section. Huiyuan now tackles the question: if monks don't comply with the temporal world, what do they seek? His answer draws on Laozi's concept of "oneness" to argue that kings themselves become great by attaining harmony with the ancestral source (宗 zong). But the monk seeks that same source by a different path: not through participation in worldly governance, but through transcending the cycle of transformations entirely.

The key move is the argument about sentience and suffering. "Those with sentience have feelings about the transformations [of things in the world]. Those without sentience have no feelings about the transformations" (p. 79). Living beings are trapped by their attachments: "the feelings become ever more profound. Words cannot describe suffering such as this." The monk's task is to sever these attachments, reach the "fundamental state" (本 ben), and thereby escape the cycle.

"Those who transcend this sinking and filthy enclosure do not hinder their life with feelings. As they do not hinder their life with feelings, their lives can be extinguished. As they do not hinder their mental faculties with life, their mental capacities can reach a state of profundity."Tiwald, p. 79

Huiyuan's conclusion is that the monk, by achieving this transcendence, actually performs a greater service than any temporal ruler could. "They convey themselves a great distance across the ford of the Three Vehicles, opening a path between Heaven and humankind" (p. 78). The monk who refuses to bow is not defying the king. The monk is operating in a realm where bowing and not bowing are equally irrelevant.

問題 DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
Principle vs. Compromise
  • Huiyuan concedes that Buddhist laypersons should obey the state but claims monks are exempt. Is this a principled distinction or a political compromise? What would happen to his argument if he demanded exemption for all Buddhists?
  • The text uses the phrase "comply with the transformations" (顺化 shunhua) repeatedly. For Confucians, complying with the social order IS the path to moral cultivation. Huiyuan argues that monks seek something beyond the social order. Is this a genuine philosophical disagreement, or are they talking past each other?
Translation and Transcendence
  • Huiyuan bridges Buddhist and Daoist frameworks by using the term 宗 zong ("ancestral source") to describe the ultimate reality that monks seek. Is this a legitimate philosophical translation, or does it distort the Buddhist concept of nirvana by dressing it in Daoist clothing?
  • Huiyuan argues that monks, by transcending the temporal world, actually perform a higher service to humanity. Is this a convincing response to the Confucian charge that monks are parasites on society? Or does it beg the question by assuming that transcendence is real and valuable?
State, Power, and Legacy
  • Han Yu's "Memorial on the Bone of Buddha" (written ~400 years later) calls for Buddhism to be "destroyed root and branch for ever." Compare his arguments to the Confucian objections Huiyuan was responding to. Has the debate changed in four centuries, or is Han Yu making the same arguments the Six Dynasties critics made?
  • The bowing controversy was ultimately about the limits of state power. Should any institution or individual be exempt from the authority of the ruler? Where do you draw the line? Compare to modern debates about religious exemptions from secular law.
  • The reading's introduction notes that Buddhist monasteries were "tax-exempt, and often became extremely wealthy through donations from the faithful, and by running their own businesses" (Tiwald, p. 73). Huiyuan's treatise makes a philosophical argument. But how much of the anti-Buddhist backlash was actually about money and institutional power rather than ideas?
