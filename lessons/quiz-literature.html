Question 1 Qu Yuan
Qu Yuan's Li Sao (離騷) is primarily a poem about:
The Li Sao ("Encountering Sorrow") is the founding poem of the Chinese lyric tradition. Qu Yuan, a minister of the state of Chu, was slandered and banished. The poem stages his grief as a visionary journey through the heavens, seeking a ruler who will recognize his loyalty and virtue. It established the template of the wronged loyal minister that echoed through Chinese literature for two millennia.
Question 2 Ruan Ji
Ruan Ji's Yonghuai (詠懷) poems use obscure, indirect imagery rather than direct statement primarily because:
Ruan Ji lived under the Sima family's consolidation of power, when political speech was lethal. His friend Ji Kang was executed for it. Ruan Ji survived by retreating into wine and writing poems so layered in allusion and indirection that no one could pin a specific political critique on him. The obscurity is a survival strategy. "Sleepless at midnight, I rise and play the lute" is not vague for aesthetic reasons; it is vague because clarity meant death.
Question 3 Jia Yi
Jia Yi's "Faults of Qin" (過秦論) argues that the Qin dynasty collapsed because:
Jia Yi wrote this essay early in the Han dynasty as a warning to his own rulers. His argument: Qin conquered all six states through military force but failed to transition from "attacking" to "preserving." It never adopted humaneness (ren) and rightness (yi) as governing principles. The same methods that won the empire could not hold it. The essay is both political analysis and rhetorical masterpiece.
Question 4 Han Fu
Sima Xiangru's Shanglin Fu (上林賦) depicts the emperor's hunting park primarily to:
The Shanglin Fu is the supreme example of Han epideictic fu. It catalogues the imperial park's rivers, mountains, animals, hunts, and entertainments in dizzying abundance. At the end, the emperor renounces excess and turns to virtue. This structure (spectacular display + moral correction) is the signature move of court fu. Whether the moral turn is genuine critique or ritual cover for the spectacle itself is a question the genre never resolves.
Question 5 Ji Kang
Ji Kang's "Letter to Shan Tao" (與山巨源絕交書) is a paradigmatic text in the Chinese tradition because it:
Shan Tao recommended Ji Kang for an official post. Ji Kang responded with this letter declaring their friendship over and listing "seven things I cannot stand and two things I cannot do" about office life. It became the defining statement of the recluse ethic: that personal integrity and freedom of spirit are more valuable than political success. Ji Kang was later executed, which gave the letter an even sharper edge.
Question 6 Kong Zhigui
"Proclamation on North Mountain" (北山移文) by Kong Zhigui satirizes:
The text imagines the mountain spirits themselves issuing a proclamation against a certain "recluse" (likely Zhou Yong) who made a grand show of retreating to the mountains, then promptly accepted an official post when offered. The mountain is humiliated: its mists and peaks were used as props for a career strategy. The satire cuts at the gap between performed withdrawal and genuine conviction.
Question 7 Tao Yuanming
In "Peach Blossom Spring" (桃花源記), the hidden community is composed of people who:
The fisherman discovers a community whose ancestors entered the valley during the turmoil of the Qin dynasty. They have lived there for generations in self-sufficient simplicity. They do not know the Qin fell, let alone that the Han and Wei followed. When the fisherman leaves and tries to find the place again, he cannot. The story is Tao Yuanming's vision of a world that chose to exit history, a quiet refusal of the entire political order.
Question 8 Bao Zhao
Bao Zhao's "Tedious Ways" (拟行路难) poems are driven by the poet's frustration that:
Bao Zhao was born into a humble family during the Liu Song dynasty, when the Nine Ranks system reserved high office for aristocratic clans. Despite being recognized as one of the finest poets of his generation (one of the "Three Masters of Yuanjia"), his low birth blocked real advancement. "The road is hard" is a metaphor for a system where talent alone cannot secure its deserved place.
Question 9 Bao Zhao
"The Ruined City" (蕪城賦) by Bao Zhao meditates on the ruins of Guangling to express:
Guangling (modern Yangzhou) was once a thriving city, then devastated by the rebellion of Liu Jun. Bao Zhao contrasts its former grandeur (walls, markets, music, wealth) with its present desolation (thorns, foxes, ghosts, silence). The fu works on multiple levels: a lament for a specific place, a meditation on the vanity of political ambition, and a reflection on how quickly power and beauty dissolve into nothing.
Question 10 Seclusion Tradition
The three texts in the "On Seclusion" unit (Yang Yun's letter, Ji Kang's letter, Kong Zhigui's proclamation) collectively explore:
Read together, the three texts map the full spectrum of withdrawal. Yang Yun defends his retreat as forced upon him by political persecution. Ji Kang declares withdrawal as a principled ethical choice, breaking with a friend over it. Kong Zhigui exposes the cynical version, where "reclusion" is performed for social capital and then cashed in for an appointment. The unit asks: when is retreat authentic, and when is it theater?
