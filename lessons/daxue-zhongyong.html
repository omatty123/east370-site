概覽 Overview
大學 The Great Learning Daxue

The Great Learning (Daxue 大學) is a beginner's handbook for ethical self-perfection and political transformation. It lays out a program of Three Guidelines and Eight Stages that connect personal moral cultivation to the ordering of the state and the world. It presents what Confucianism does best: a practical roadmap from inner virtue to social order.

Core Message

You already know how to be moral. All you need to learn is how to turn that knowledge into action. The text builds on the Mencian claim that all people share an innate moral sense, then provides a step-by-step training regimen to activate it.

Scale

Only about 1,750 Chinese characters. Short enough to memorize. Dense enough to spend a lifetime unpacking.

中庸 The Doctrine of the Mean Zhongyong

The Doctrine of the Mean (Zhongyong 中庸) is significantly longer and more philosophically ambitious. Where the Daxue is a practical how-to manual, the Zhongyong builds toward a cosmic vision: the perfectly genuine person who forms a triad with heaven and earth.

Core Message

The title may mean "the central ordinary-practice" or "bring centered in ordinary practice." Morality is not esoteric. It is a characteristic feature of everyday spontaneity. Our ordinary experience is the raw substance of the Dao.

Scale

About 3,500 Chinese characters, divided by Zhu Xi into 33 chapters. Eno organizes it into seven sections plus a coda, tracing its argument from Confucius's quotes on the mean, through governance, to the cosmic power of perfect genuineness (cheng 誠).

背景 Historical Context
禮記 Origins in the Liji Book of Rites

Both texts were originally anonymous chapters in the Liji 禮記 (Book of Rites), a sprawling anthology of texts about ritual assembled during the second century BCE. The Daxue was chapter 42; the Zhongyong was chapter 31. Neither was initially singled out for special note.

Traditional Authorship

Over time, the Daxue came to be attributed to Zengzi 曾參 (Zeng Shen, c. 505–436 BCE), a prominent junior disciple of Confucius. The Zhongyong was attributed to Zisi 子思 (c. 483–402 BCE), Confucius's grandson. Modern scholars generally view these attributions as later inventions devised to justify the texts' prestige.

Actual Dating

Both texts reflect the mature development of Confucian thought through the third century BCE. Internal evidence points to composition after the Qin unification (221 BCE): the Daxue quotes the "Oath of Qin" and celebrates radical renewal; the Zhongyong references standardized carts, writing, and conduct, all Qin-era policies.

大學 The Great Learning
三綱領 The Three Guidelines Section I.A
"The Dao of Great Learning lies in making bright virtue brilliant; in making the people new; in coming to rest at the limit of the good."— Eno, p. 11

These three phrases are cryptic on their own. The Commentary (Section II.A) fleshes them out through citations from authoritative texts: the Book of Documents, the Book of Poetry, and sayings of Confucius.

明 Making bright virtue brilliant
Activating the innate moral brilliance endowed by Tian. "In all of these, brilliance was spontaneous."
新 Making the people new
"Truly new each day. New each and every day. Again, new each day." Constant moral renewal, drawn from the Basin Inscription of Tang (Shang founder).
止 Coming to rest in the highest good
Confucius asks: orioles know where to rest, "Can we believe that human beings are not so good as birds?"
八條目 The Eight Stages Section I.B

The Eight Stages describe the path from inner cultivation to world transformation. They are presented in a sorites (chain syllogism): "If A then B; if B then C..." Supremely well suited for memorization.

一
Aligning affairs (格物 ge wu)
The most contested phrase in the text. Zhu Xi read it as "investigating things." Wang Yangming read it as "aligning the world through moral will." Eno's translation follows the latter.
二
Extending understanding (致知)
Developing one's moral knowledge through engagement with affairs.
三
Making intentions genuine (誠意)
Being without self-deceit. "The same as when we hate a bad odor or like a beautiful color" (Eno, p. 14).
四
Balancing the mind (正心)
When anger, fear, delight, or anxiety take hold, the mind loses balance. "When the mind is not focused one does not see what one is looking at."
五
Refining one's person (修身)
Overcoming partiality. "It is rare to find in the world one who can love, but know the bad points of those he loves."
六
Aligning one's household (齊家)
Family mastery as the foundation of public leadership. "There are none who cannot instruct their households but can instruct others."
七
Ordering the state (治國)
The reciprocity principle applied to governance. "The Dao of the carpenter's square": do not impose on others what you detest.
八
Setting the world at peace (平天下)
"A state does not take profit as profit; it takes righteousness as profit." Virtue, not wealth, is the root of political power.
結構 Structure of the Text

Zhu Xi divided the Daxue into two parts: a brief "Text" (jing 經, literally "classic") followed by ten sections of "Commentary" (zhuan 傳). The "Text" (Sections I.A and I.B) introduces the Three Guidelines and Eight Stages using the sorites form. The brilliance of the work emerges in the Commentary, where each Stage receives a practical, often vivid interpretation.

The commentary on Stages 1–2 is textually corrupt, with fragments and possible duplications. Stages 3–6 form the powerful core: concrete, psychologically acute, grounded in everyday experience. Stages 7–8 shift to political formulas and authoritative citations. Eno notes that the closing sections "lack the focus of the core sections of the text, and there are reasons to believe that the closing portions of the original text have been lost."

中庸 The Doctrine of the Mean
主題 Theme: The Source of the Center Chapter 1
"That which is ordained by Tian is called our nature; to lead by our nature is called the Dao; to cultivate the Dao is called the teaching."— Eno, p. 26

The opening chapter links human morality to its cosmic source. Tian ordains our nature (xing 性), which provides direct knowledge of the Dao. Key terms introduced here: Tian 天 (the cosmic force), xing 性 (human nature), Dao 道 (the Way), junzi 君子 (the moral exemplar), and zhong 中 (centrality, the mean).

"Pleasure and anger, sorrow and joy: before they emerge they are called centered; emerging by the proper rhythms they are called harmonious. Centered: this is the great root of the world. Harmonious: this is the ultimate Dao of the world."— Eno, p. 26
結構 Structure of the Text 33 Chapters

Eno's translation organizes the Zhongyong into seven sections plus a coda, each marking a shift in topic and tone. The text builds from Confucius's sayings toward a cosmic crescendo.

I
Cleaving to the central mean
Chapters 2–11
Quotes from Confucius on the difficulty of staying on the Dao. The obstacle is not knowing it, but persisting.
II
The common basis of the Dao
Chapters 12–16
The Dao is present in ordinary practice, accessible to every person. "Any husband or wife may partake in knowledge of it."
III
Confucius on the sages and filial perfection
Chapters 17–19
The sage kings Shun, King Wen, and King Wu are reframed as ultimate filial sons. Political greatness is an extension of family devotion.
IV
On government
Chapter 20 (a)–(b)
Duke Ai asks about governance. Confucius outlines nine canons for ruling, rooted in self-refinement.
V
Perfect genuineness (cheng 誠)
Chapters 20 (c)–26
The climax. "Perfect genuineness is the Dao of Tian. Making things perfectly genuine is the Dao of humankind." The sage who exhausts his nature can form a triad with heaven and earth.
VI
Ruling by the Dao of Nature and li
Chapters 27–29
The sage's ritual innovations as expressions of the cosmic order. "How great is the Dao of the sage! Brimming, it nurtures the things of the world."
VII
The sage's power of virtue
Chapters 30–32
"As arching vastness, as depthless springs." The sage as cosmic actor whose fame reaches "wherever boats or carts travel or human labor reaches."
終
Coda: The invisibility of ultimate greatness
Chapter 33
A poetic string of Poetry citations closing on silence. "The revolutions of heaven above / Possess neither sound nor smell." Virtue beyond perception.
術語 Key Concepts
誠 Genuineness (cheng) Central to both texts

Cheng 誠 is perhaps the central concept of the Zhongyong, and some scholars believe it is equally the pivot of the Daxue. It describes "a process of perfect inner correspondence": the alignment between our innate moral dispositions, our awareness of them, our judgments about affairs, and our expression of those judgments in action.

"Perfect genuineness is the Dao of Tian. Making things perfectly genuine is the Dao of humankind. One who is perfectly genuine hits the mark without effort, succeeds without forethought. To keep to the center of the Dao at perfect ease is to be a sage."— Eno, p. 34 (Zhongyong Ch. 20e)

The person who achieves cheng responds to the world with spontaneous moral clarity, liking and disliking in perfect alignment with what is good. This became a foundational concept in Neo-Confucian thought from the eleventh century onward.

天 Tian and Human Nature Shared framework

Tian 天

Tian was the high deity of Zhou state religion. In both texts it functions as the cosmic source that endows human beings with moral dispositions. "That which is ordained by Tian is called our nature." Tian is not quite a personal god here, nor quite an impersonal force. It is the origin of our moral capacity and the standard against which the sage is measured.

Xing 性 (Human Nature)

Both texts adopt the Mencian position that human nature is innately endowed with moral dispositions and is thus the seat of goodness. This is axiomatic, not argued. The Daxue builds its training program on this assumption; the Zhongyong traces its cosmic implications.

Junzi 君子

Originally "ruler's son" or "prince," the term came to denote an ideal of human moral excellence. In these texts, the junzi can serve as an equivalent for "sage." Both texts stress that the junzi is "cautious of his solitude" (shen qi du 慎其獨), a phrase also found in the Wuxing (Five Forms of Conduct).

Connections to Other Course Readings

  • Analects: Both texts quote Confucius extensively. The Zhongyong Ch. 13 echoes Analects 4.15 (devotion and reciprocity) and 15.24 (the Golden Rule).
  • Mencius: The Mencian theory of innate goodness is the foundation of both texts. Zhongyong Ch. 20(d)–(e) closely parallels Mencius 4A.12.
  • Xunzi: The Zhongyong draws on Xunzian themes too, particularly the cosmic triad of heaven, earth, and humanity, and the role of ritual (li) in Sections VI–VII.
  • Zhuangzi: Eno notes a "significant area of convergence" between the Daoist Zhuangzi and the Zhongyong on the topic of ordinary practice and union with the creative flow of Nature.
  • Laozi: The phrase "taking no action" (wuwei) appears in Zhongyong Ch. 26, a targeted co-optation of Daoist language.
  • Neo-Confucianism: Zhu Xi's elevation of these texts into the Four Books made them the foundation of Chinese education for centuries. Compare the Li (Pattern) lesson on Neo-Confucian metaphysics.
名句 Key Passages
大學 From the Daxue

The Chain Syllogism (I.B)

"In ancient times, those who wished to make bright virtue brilliant in the world first ordered their states; those who wished to order their states first aligned their households; those who wished to align their households first refined their persons; those who wished to refine their persons first balanced their minds..."— Eno, p. 11

On Self-Deception (II.B.3)

"Making the intentions perfectly genuine means being without self-deceit. It is the same as when we hate a bad odor or like a beautiful color. It describes a process of perfect inner correspondence."— Eno, p. 14

On Partiality (II.B.5)

"When people come to those for whom they hold kinlike affection they are partial. When they come to those whom they view as base and evil they are partial... Thus it is rare to find in the world one who can love, but know the bad points of those he loves, hate, but know the good points of those he hates."— Eno, p. 15

Righteousness Over Profit (II.B.7–8)

"If one has virtue, one has men. If one has men, one has land. If one has land, one has goods... Virtue is the root, goods are the branches."— Eno, p. 18
中庸 From the Zhongyong

Cleaving to the Mean (Ch. 9)

"One may level all the states of the world, decline high rank and salary, tread upon naked blades, and yet be unable to cleave to the mean in ordinary practice."— Eno, p. 27

The Dao in Ordinary Life (Ch. 12)

"The Dao of the junzi is broad yet hidden. Any husband or wife may partake in knowledge of it, yet reaching to its ultimate nature, there is that which even the sage does not know of it."— Eno, p. 28

Exhausting One's Nature (Ch. 22)

"Only those in the world who are perfectly genuine to the utmost can exhaust their natures. Those who can exhaust their natures can exhaust others' natures. Those who can exhaust others' natures can exhaust the natures of things. Those who can exhaust the natures of things can participate in the nurturing transformations of Tian. Those who can participate in the nurturing transformations of Tian can form a triad with heaven and earth."— Eno, p. 34

The Invisibility of Virtue (Ch. 33, Coda)

"The Dao of the junzi is hidden dark and grows brighter every day. The Dao of the small man strikes the eye but fades every day... Virtue light as a hair. Yet there remain things comparable to a hair. The revolutions of heaven above / Possess neither sound nor smell. That is the ultimate!"— Eno, pp. 39–40
討論 Discussion Questions
一 Self-Cultivation and Politics

The Daxue's Eight Stages link personal moral refinement directly to ordering the state and setting the world at peace. Is this chain plausible? Where might it break? Consider the Commentary on Stage 5 ("Refining one's person"), which focuses on overcoming bias. Is overcoming personal bias really the key to political competence?

二 Genuineness vs. Effort

The Zhongyong says: "One who is perfectly genuine hits the mark without effort, succeeds without forethought" (Ch. 20e). But it also says: "When others can do one, demand that you do one hundred; when others can do ten, demand that you do one thousand" (Ch. 20f). How do these reconcile? Is cheng a natural state or the product of grinding effort?

三 The Ordinary and the Cosmic

The Zhongyong starts with common morality ("any husband or wife may partake") and ends with the sage as a cosmic force equal to heaven and earth. Trace the argument through Sections II and V. How does the text get from everyday practice to cosmic co-creation? Is this a leap or a logical progression?

四 The Daxue as Self-Help

Eno calls the Daxue "a self-help manual for the aspiring ethical actor." Its Commentary on Stage 3 uses the examples of hating a bad smell and loving a beautiful color to explain "making intentions genuine." Compare this to the Mencian sprouts of virtue (Mencius 2A.6). Is the Daxue making a psychological claim about moral intuition, or a prescriptive claim about what we should do?

五 Ge wu 格物: The Great Debate

The phrase ge wu (Stage 1) has been translated as "investigating things" (Zhu Xi) and "aligning affairs" (Eno, following Wang Yangming). Zhu Xi's reading launched a tradition of empirical inquiry: study everything to grasp the principles of the cosmos. Wang Yangming countered that moral knowledge is innate and the phrase means perfecting your moral will through action. What are the practical consequences of each reading? Which produces a more viable path of self-cultivation?
