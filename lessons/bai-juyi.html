傳 BIOGRAPHY
Bai Juyi (白居易, 772–846). Historical portrait from the Wan hsiao tang collection. Public domain.

Bai Juyi (白居易, 772–846), courtesy name Letian (樂天), was the most popular poet of the Tang dynasty in his own lifetime and arguably the most widely read Chinese poet across East Asia. He later adopted the literary name Xiangshan Jushi (香山居士, "Lay Buddhist of Fragrant Mountain"). In Birch's anthology, he appears as Po Chü-i (Wade-Giles).

Born in Xinzheng, Henan, his family fled south during the aftermath of the An Lushan Rebellion. His father died in 794, leaving the family in financial difficulty. Bai showed literary talent early: the famous anecdote has him composing "Grass on an Ancient Plain" at around age sixteen, which so impressed the established poet Gu Kuang that he recommended the young man pursue the imperial examinations.

He passed the jinshi exam in 800 at age twenty-eight, a remarkable achievement given that only about 2–3% of candidates typically passed. He entered the civil service, and by 807 had been appointed "Reminder of the Left" (Zuoshiyi), a censorial post that allowed him to submit frank memorials to the throne. It was during this period that he met Yuan Zhen (元稹, 779–831), beginning the most celebrated literary friendship of the mid-Tang.

Bai championed a radical poetics of accessibility. He reportedly tested his poems on an illiterate old servant woman and rewrote anything she could not follow. He believed "articles should be created for the times, and poetry for current affairs." He organized his own work into four categories: satirical poems, leisure poems, sentimental poems, and unregulated verses. This "plain style" made his work enormously popular. His poems were copied on inn walls and sung by performers across the empire.

The turning point came in 815. Chancellor Wu Yuanheng was assassinated outside his home. Bai immediately submitted a memorial demanding justice, but his rank did not entitle him to be the first to memorialize on such matters. Political enemies seized the pretext. He was demoted and exiled to Jiangzhou (modern Jiujiang) as Sub-Prefect. During this exile he wrote the Pipa Xing (Song of the Pipa Player), one of his masterpieces.

After his recall, he served as governor of Hangzhou (822–824), where he built a causeway at West Lake still called the "Bai Causeway" today. He spent his final decades in semi-retirement at Luoyang, increasingly devoted to Chan Buddhism. He died in 846 at age seventy-four, leaving approximately 2,800 surviving poems, the largest corpus of any Tang poet.

概覽 TODAY'S READING
讀 The Reading Birch, pp. 266–278

Our selection from Birch covers Bai Juyi's major works within the "Poets of a Generation: Two" section (pp. 260–287). Bai's portion runs from page 266 through 278, containing one epic narrative (120 lines), one extended pilgrimage poem (seven pages), and a cluster of shorter lyrics. The range demonstrates what Birch calls moods "from the sublimity of 'The Temple' to the contemplation of baldness" (p. 260).

時 Historical Moment Mid-Tang, c. 806–846

Bai Juyi writes in the aftermath of the An Lushan Rebellion (755). The High Tang golden age of Wang Wei, Li Bai, and Du Fu is over. The empire survived but lost its confidence. Regional military governors hold real power. Eunuchs control the palace armies. The literati class feels both responsible for the empire and excluded from it.

Bai's response: poetry as civic duty. His "New Music Bureau Ballads" (新樂府) deliberately imitated folk song forms to reach ordinary people. "A Song of Unending Sorrow" retells the rebellion as a love story between the emperor and his consort. "The Temple" records a personal escape into Buddhism. The shorter poems practice what Birch calls Bai's "quiet whimsy" (p. 260).

Core Tensions

  • Accessibility vs. depth: Can plain language carry real thought?
  • Duty vs. withdrawal: Service leads to exile; Buddhism offers peace
  • History as love story: Does the Xuanzong-Yang Guifei romance obscure politics?
  • The exiled poet: How does demotion transform the writing?
長恨歌 A SONG OF UNENDING SORROW
長恨歌 A Song of Unending Sorrow tr. Witter Bynner · pp. 266–269
"China's Emperor, craving beauty that might shake an empire,
Was on the throne, for many years, searching, never finding,
Till a little child of the Yang clan, hardly even grown,
Bred in an inner chamber, with no one knowing her,
But with graces granted by heaven and not to be concealed,
At last one day was chosen for the imperial household." Birch, p. 266; tr. Bynner

The Historical Story

Emperor Xuanzong (r. 712–756) fell in love with Yang Guifei (Yang Yuhuan), a consort of extraordinary beauty. He lavished the empire's resources on her, neglected governance, and elevated her family to enormous power. In 755, the general An Lushan launched a devastating rebellion. As Xuanzong fled the capital, his own soldiers demanded Yang Guifei's execution as the cause of the disaster. The emperor was forced to watch her strangled. He spent the rest of his life in grief.

The Poem's Three Movements

Part 1: The Infatuation (pp. 266–267). Bai catalogs the emperor's obsession: "If she but turned her head and smiled, there were cast a hundred spells, / And the powder and paint of the Six Palaces faded into nothing." The Yang family rises ("And, because she so illumined and glorified her clan, / She brought to every father, every mother through the empire, / Happiness when a girl was born rather than a boy"). Then the decay: "The Forbidden City, the nine-tiered palace, loomed in the dust / From thousands of horses and chariots headed southwest."

Part 2: The Catastrophe and Mourning (pp. 267–268). The pivot is brutal: "Till war-drums, booming from Yü-yang, shocked the whole earth / And broke the tunes of 'The Rainbow Skirt and the Feathered Coat.'" The soldiers demand Yang Guifei's death. "Flowery hairpins fell on the ground, no one picked them up, / And a green and white jade hair-tassel and a golden hair-bird." The emperor can do nothing. The second half tracks his desolation: "He stared at the desolate moon from his temporary palace, / He heard bell-notes in the evening rain, cutting at his breast."

Part 3: The Supernatural Reunion (pp. 268–269). A Daoist priest finds Yang Guifei's spirit on an enchanted isle. She sends back a broken hairpin and a private vow: "On the seventh day of the Seventh-month, in the Palace of Long Life, / We told each other secretly in the quiet midnight world / That we wished to fly in heaven, two birds with the wings of one, / And to grow together on the earth, two branches of one tree." The final couplet: "Earth endures, heaven endures; sometime both shall end, / While this unending sorrow goes on and on for ever."

Why It Matters

Written c. 806, this is probably the most famous narrative poem in Chinese. Bai Juyi takes a political catastrophe and recasts it as a love story. The question for discussion: is this sympathy or evasion? By giving the emperor human grief, does Bai let him off the hook for the rebellion? Or does the poem's structure (indulgence leads to war leads to death leads to fantasy) constitute its own kind of criticism? Notice that the "reunion" happens only through a medium. The emperor never actually gets Yang Guifei back. The sorrow is unending precisely because the Daoist ending is a consolation that does not console.

蓮 ON THE LOTUSES
移根蓮 On the Lotuses tr. Arthur Waley · pp. 269–270

Lotuses are transplanted from their natural habitat to a government ditch. They survive, but they lose their sweetness.

"In human matters it also must be so. / It is better for a man to be utterly cast away / Than torn from his roots and put where he is not in place." Birch, pp. 269–270; tr. Waley

Analysis

A parable of exile and displacement. The lotuses are Bai Juyi himself, uprooted from Chang'an and planted in a provincial post where he does not belong. "Torn from his roots" applies equally to the flower and the poet. The poem's argument is radical: better to be destroyed entirely than to survive in the wrong place. This is the voice of a man who has experienced demotion and knows that institutional survival is not the same as being alive. Compare to Tao Yuanming's decision to quit office entirely rather than bow to a petty superior. Both poets choose integrity over position, but Bai Juyi frames it as botanical fact.

遊悟真寺詩 THE TEMPLE
遊悟真寺詩 The Temple tr. Arthur Waley · pp. 270–277
"Autumn: the ninth year of Yüan-ho;
The eighth month, and the moon swelling her arc.
It was then I travelled to the Temple of Wu-chen,
A temple terraced on Wang Shun's Hill." Birch, p. 270; tr. Waley

Structure: A Five-Day Journey Inward

At seven pages, this is the longest poem in the selection. It records a five-day pilgrimage to Wu-chen Temple (Temple of Awakened Truth) on Wang Shun's Hill. The poem moves through three layers:

1. The Physical Climb. Wading streams, gripping ivy, tracking monkeys. The mountain resists the visitor. The natural world is not decorative. It is an obstacle course that strips away the traveler's urban identity.

2. The Architectural Tour. Treasure Towers, Jade Image Hall, Painted Dragon Hall. Buddhist relics, a jade flute "borne as a gift to the Garden of Jetavan," the Chamber of Guanyin. The temple is both real building and spiritual diagram.

3. The Autobiographical Turn. The poem pivots to confession:

"I, that a man of the wild hills was born, / Floundering fell into the web of the World's net." Birch, p. 276; tr. Waley

He recalls his career as a hollow compromise. Then declares freedom: "Like a wild deer that has torn the hunter's net / I range abroad by no halters barred." He sits with Zhuangzi's book and announces he will never return to the "marble basin" of office.

Why It Matters

Bai Juyi was the friend and ally of Han Yu, the arch-enemy of Buddhism. Yet here he writes a poem that finds genuine transcendence in a Buddhist temple. The contradiction is the point. Mid-Tang intellectuals could not be easily sorted into "pro-Buddhist" or "anti-Buddhist" camps. Bai's Buddhism was practical and personal: not doctrinal philosophy but a way of surviving political disappointment. The temple climb is a metaphor for what Buddhism offered the exiled literatus: a path that goes up and away from the court. Compare to Wang Wei's Chan Buddhism in the Wang River poems. Both poets find in Buddhism what Confucianism cannot provide: permission to stop striving.

短詩 THE SHORTER POEMS
紅鸚鵡 The Red Cockatoo tr. Waley · p. 277

"Sent as a present from Annam — / A red cockatoo. / Coloured like the peach-tree blossom, / Speaking with the speech of men."

They caged it. Six lines. A beautiful bird that can talk, sent from the frontier as a gift, locked in a cage for its talents. The parallel is hard to miss: the eloquent get punished for their eloquence. Bai Juyi was exiled for speaking up about a political assassination. The cockatoo is Bai Juyi. The cage is Jiangzhou.

Analysis

This is Bai's "plain style" at its sharpest. No allusion, no obscurity. A child could follow the surface story. But the political meaning is devastating. The bird's crime is "speaking with the speech of men." Bai's crime was the same. In six lines, he transforms a pet-poem into an indictment of how the Tang state treats its critics.

沐浴 On His Baldness tr. Waley · p. 278
"On my bald pate I trickle a ladle full.
Like one baptized with the Water of Buddha's Law,
I sit and receive this cool, cleansing joy." Birch, p. 278; tr. Waley

Analysis

Bai loses his hair and decides he loves it. No more comb, no more topknot, no more fuss. He pours cold water on his bare head and compares himself to a Buddhist monk receiving baptism. What Birch calls Bai's "quiet whimsy" (p. 260) is actually a serious move: he turns a sign of aging into a sign of spiritual freedom. The topknot was a mark of the Confucian gentleman. Losing it means losing the social performance. Bai is delighted. This is retirement poetry at its most radical: the body's decay becomes liberation.

贈客 To a Talkative Guest tr. Waley · p. 278
"I beg you, Sir, do not tell me about things at Ch'ang-an; / For you entered just when my lute was tuned and lying balanced on my knees." Birch, p. 278; tr. Waley

Analysis

Five lines of perfect refusal. The retired poet does not want news from the capital. The lute is tuned. The visitor brings politics. Bai chooses music over information. This is the recluse ideal in a social situation: the guest has already arrived, so Bai cannot retreat physically. Instead, he retreats mentally, using the lute as a boundary marker. Compare to Wang Wei's "To the Assistant Prefect Chang," where Wang refuses the question of success with fishermen's songs. Same move, same genre, different century. The Tang literatus had a script for saying "I don't care anymore," and Bai Juyi performs it with comic precision.

題壁 The Poem on the Wall tr. Waley · p. 278
"My clumsy poem on the inn-wall none cared to see;
With bird-droppings and moss's growth the letters were blotched away.
There came a guest with heart so full, that though a page to the Throne,
He did not grudge with his broidered coat to wipe off the dust, and read." Birch, p. 278; tr. Waley

Analysis

Birch notes that "Yüan Chen wrote that on his way to exile he had discovered a poem inscribed by Po Chü-yi on the wall of the Lo-k'ou Inn" (p. 278). A forgotten poem on an inn wall, covered in bird droppings, and a friend in exile who stops to clean it and read. The "broidered coat" marks Yuan Zhen as an official, yet he stoops. This is the Yuan-Bai friendship in miniature: two poets reading each other across distance and disgrace. The poem also says something about what happens to literature after the writer moves on. Words on walls get covered in moss. It takes an act of love to recover them.

陶淵明 THE TAO YUANMING CONNECTION
羈鳥 Caged Birds and Free Deer: Bai Juyi's Tao Yuanming

Bai Juyi was a lifelong admirer of Tao Yuanming (陶淵明, 365–427). He wrote an entire series of poems imitating Tao's style. The connection runs deep through today's reading, and it centers on a single metaphor: the animal that is either caged or free.

Tao Yuanming's most famous poem, "Returning Home" (歸去來兮辭), contains the line:

"羈鳥戀舊林，池魚思故淵"
"The caged bird longs for the old forest; the pond fish yearns for the deep pool." Tao Yuanming, "Returning Home"

This is the foundational metaphor of the recluse tradition: the talented creature, trapped in service, longing for its natural habitat. Bai Juyi knew this line cold. In today's reading, he gives us both outcomes of the metaphor.

The Deer That Escapes: "The Temple" (p. 276)

"Like a wild deer that has torn the hunter's net / I range abroad by no halters barred." Birch, p. 276; tr. Waley

After the five-day climb to Wu-chen Temple, Bai casts himself as Tao Yuanming's caged bird, finally free. But he changes the animal: not a bird, a deer. And the deer does not simply "long for" the forest. It tears the net. This is a more violent version of the recluse escape. Tao Yuanming resigned quietly. Bai Juyi was exiled by force, and the poem's metaphor reflects that. The net was torn, not opened. The freedom was not chosen so much as seized from wreckage.

The Bird That Doesn't Escape: "The Red Cockatoo" (p. 277)

"Sent as a present from Annam. / A red cockatoo. / Coloured like the peach-tree blossom, / Speaking with the speech of men." They caged it. End of poem.

This is Tao Yuanming's caged bird, but without the longing. The cockatoo does not dream of the old forest. It just sits in the cage. Its talent (human speech) is the reason it was captured. Bai Juyi was exiled for the same reason: he spoke up about Wu Yuanheng's assassination. The cockatoo is a darker version of the metaphor because there is no escape, no torn net, no temple on a hill. Just a beautiful, articulate creature in a box.

The Classroom Pairing

Read these two poems together and you get the full range of what Tao Yuanming's metaphor can do. The Temple gives you the deer that breaks free, the exhilaration of escape, the Buddhist mountaintop. The Red Cockatoo gives you the bird that stays trapped, the political reality that not everyone gets a temple. Bai Juyi lived both versions. He was exiled (caged), found Buddhism (escaped), returned to semi-retirement in Luoyang (free), but never forgot that the cage exists. The two poems are the same metaphor seen from opposite ends of a career.

討論 DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
The Plain Style
  • Bai Juyi tested his poems on an illiterate servant woman and rewrote anything she could not follow. Li He (who we will read alongside him) wrote "Dew on the secret orchid / Like screaming eyes." Are these opposite poetics, or different responses to the same broken world? What is gained and lost by radical accessibility?
  • The Red Cockatoo is six lines long and uses no allusions. It is also an indictment of political persecution. Can plain language carry the same weight as Du Fu's "encrusted jewels of compressed esoteric allusion"?
Love, Power, History
  • "A Song of Unending Sorrow" retells the An Lushan Rebellion as a love story. Is Bai Juyi sympathizing with Emperor Xuanzong, criticizing him, or both? Does the Daoist ending (the spirit on the enchanted isle) offer real consolation, or does it expose the emperor's fantasy of reunion as a failure to face consequences?
  • Yang Guifei is strangled on the emperor's orders. The poem mourns her death but never questions the soldiers' demand that she die. Whose side is Bai Juyi on?
Buddhism and Withdrawal
  • "The Temple" is a seven-page pilgrimage poem that ends with Bai declaring he will never return to office. He was a friend of Han Yu, the most famous anti-Buddhist polemicist of the Tang. How do you reconcile these positions? Is Bai's Buddhism genuine or strategic?
  • "On His Baldness" turns aging into Buddhist liberation. "On the Lotuses" turns transplantation into a parable of exile. How does Bai Juyi use natural imagery differently from Wang Wei? (Hint: Wang Wei dissolves into nature. Bai Juyi argues with it.)
