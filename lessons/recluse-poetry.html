The Recluse Tradition
Historical Context

Centuries of division and turmoil between the close of the Han dynasty (219) and the foundation of the Sui (589). The T'ang (618) would bring stability, but these poets lived in the "years of darkness" in between.

Political chaos drove many intellectuals to retreat, whether physical (rural withdrawal) or spiritual (Daoist/Buddhist mystique). The court was dangerous; serving the wrong faction could mean death.

This poetry offered "spiritual sustenance to all for whom the times were out of joint."

The Three Voices

Chang Heng (78-139 CE)

Earliest voice. His "Bones of Chuang Tzu" is a dialogue with death itself, a noble ode inspired by Daoist mystique.

Juan Chi (210-263 CE)

Two centuries between their lifetimes. Juan Chi and Pao Chao (414-466) share "the profound melancholy of the years of darkness, and a taste for the macabre."

T'ao Ch'ien (365-427 CE)

The defining recluse. Also known as Tao Yüan-ming. Served briefly in the Chin (Tsin) bureaucracy, withdrew at 33, never returned. His poetry of "shadow-play" has come to seem most characteristically Chinese to many Western readers. Translated by William Acker.

Chang Heng · "The Bones of Chuang Tzu" (pp. 176-178)
Context

Chang Heng encounters bones by the roadside and addresses the dead man, offering to restore him to life through divine intervention. The dead man refuses, preferring death's peace to life's suffering.

The poem echoes the famous skull passage in the Zhuangzi, where Zhuang Zhou encounters a skull that likewise refuses resurrection. Chang Heng turns this philosophical parable into a sustained lyric meditation.

The Traveler's Discovery
I, Chang P'ing-tzu, had traversed the Nine Wilds and seen their wonders, In the eight continents and their outlying islands And in all the corners of the inhabited earth, I had admired all that was beautiful and had tasted all that was savorous. I had done, indeed, everything that heart could wish; And wherever I had been, I had noted what was said. Suddenly I looked and by the road-side I saw a man's bones lying in the squelchy earth, Black rime-Loss of putrefying flesh still hung upon him; His bald ribs stared like rafters of a roof. I took a twig of plain-tree and struck the bones, saying: "Dead man, in life you set your cap at careerism and greedily pursued fame and power, Or else, perhaps, you were a fellow of no importance, Who hugged your hoe and rod and held the furrow-Loss?"
Translated by Arthur Waley
The Dead Man's Response
The dead man speaks back. The heart of the poem.
O Friend, how strange and unacceptable your words! In death I rest and am at peace; in life I toiled and strove. Is the hardness of the winter stream Better than the melting of spring? All pride that the body knew, Was it not lighter than dust? What Ch'ao and Hsu despised, What Po-ch'eng gave up and left, Where Pien-sui drowned himself, All was in vain. In death there are no morning bells To rouse me to cross my hands on breast. In death there are no friends and no enemies, No High Court and no thieves. By the preparation of Yin and Yang I was shaped and given my span of years; Formless and without substance, Earth was the bed on which I lay.
Translated by Arthur Waley
The Dead Man's Conclusion
Where neither Yao nor Shun can praise me, Nor wolf nor tiger harm me, Lance prick me nor sword wound me? Of the Primal Spirit is my substance; I am a wave In the river of Darkness and Light. The Maker of All Things is my Father and Mother, Heaven is my bed and earth my cushion, The thunder and lightning are my drum and fan, The sun and moon my candle and my torch, The Milky Way my moat, the stars my jewels. With Nature my substance is joined. I have no passion, no desire, Wash me and I shall be no whiter, Foul me and I shall yet be clean. I come not, yet am here; Hasten not, yet am swift.
Translated by Arthur Waley
The Traveler's Compassion
The voice stopped, there was silence. A ghostly light Flickered and soon went out. Stiff stems of thorn In endless lines Stretch to the empty horizon. I gazed upon the dead, stared in sorrow and compassion. Then I called my servant and together we went on our way.
Translated by Arthur Waley
"In death I rest and am at peace; in life I toiled and strove. Is the hardness of the winter stream better than the melting of spring?" — Chang Heng, "The Bones of Chuang Tzu" (tr. Waley)
Juan Chi · "Poems of My Heart" (pp. 179-181)
Context

Juan Chi (Ruan Ji, 210-263) lived during the violent transition from Wei to Jin. The Ssu-ma clan was consolidating power through assassination and intimidation. Speaking freely could cost your life.

Six poems selected, showing escalating withdrawal from political life, from sleepless loneliness to the final plea for immortals to help him survive "this long and fearful night."

Poem 1 · The Sleepless Night
Being sleepless at midnight, I rise and play upon my lute. Thin curtains reflect the bright moon And a light breeze fans my robe. A wild goose cries among the clouds on the moor, The birds of passage twitter in the woods of the southern marsh. As I pace to and fro, what is it in my mind? — My solitary sadness makes me wretched.
Translated by C.J. Chen and Michael Bullock
Poem 2 · Wasted Youth
In my youth, I too was fond of singing and dancing And went west to stay in the capital of Hsien-yang. But all the people I knew there were shallow With nothing constant but their love of wine. Before I knew it my money was gone, And this year I am thinking of the return journey. My beautiful green coat with the red satin lining— I cannot get it clean again, for long is the road and deep the dust upon it.
Translated by C.J. Chen and Michael Bullock
Poem 3 · The Dark Stork
The stork vanishes upward; the crows circle the court
Inscribe on your heart Every inch of the time at sunset. When the cock has crowed at the top of the mulberry tree, When the dog has barked at the rising moon, Then the dark stork Vanishes into the sky, Flies swiftly and without return. When you look for it, You cannot even find its shadow. The glorious sky is cold and stern. It is not like The cuckoos and the crows That circle round the Court, Fly in and fly out of the rooms, And peck for grain in the inner halls.
Translated by C.J. Chen and Michael Bullock
Poem 4 · Treading on Thin Ice
Day and night Revolve Like a turning wheel. Ten thousand generations Succeed one another Like morning and evening. The spring winds blow And the fragrant plants begin to grow. Trees are full of flowers, There are crickets in the garden. The cold dew freezes them. The autumn cicada utters a long cry. Swallows and sparrows Fly in flocks to the south, The soaring hawk swiftly pursues them. How can there be righteousness In a world like this? In constant fear I have trodden on thin ice.
Translated by C.J. Chen and Michael Bullock
Poem 5 · The Wise Recluse
His influence— the scorching sun or a torrential river, His servants numerous as clouds, His power shakes the four seas. But in the eyes of a wise recluse, Size is of the least importance. Not that one covets a rare jewel Or clings to jade. But is it not better To retire in time Than to cover the whole earth with one's fame?
Translated by C.J. Chen and Michael Bullock
Poem 6 · The Long and Fearful Night
The final poem. A plea for survival through withdrawal.
I will not learn to ride a winged horse In order to travel for ten thousand miles; It is better to forget in a river or a lake Than to wet one another with bubbles on stony dry land. The ancient immortals Will help me To survive this long and fearful night.
Translated by C.J. Chen and Michael Bullock
"It is better to forget in a river or a lake / Than to wet one another with bubbles on stony dry land." — A direct echo of the Zhuangzi: fish stranded on dry ground keep each other alive with spittle, but would they not prefer to be swimming free in the rivers and lakes, having forgotten each other entirely? — Juan Chi, Poem 6 (tr. Chen & Bullock)
T'ao Ch'ien · The Master Recluse (pp. 182-188)
Context

T'ao Ch'ien (Tao Yüan-ming, 365-427) is the most celebrated recluse poet in Chinese literature. After serving briefly as a bureaucrat under the Eastern Jin, he withdrew at age 33 to farm and write. He never returned to official life.

His poetry extols rustic joys and reflects on the "shadow-play" of life, the interplay of substance and illusion that runs through all human experience. "The life of man is like a shadow-play / Which must in the end return to nothingness."

Returning to Dwell in the Country — I · pp. 182-183
The Dusty Net: escape from official life
In youth I had nothing that matched the vulgar tone, My nature of itself hankered for the hills and mountains. Inadvertently I fell into the Dusty Net, Once having gone it was more than thirteen years. The tame bird longs for its old forest— The fish in the house-Loss pond thinks of its ancient pool. I too will break the soil at the edge of the southern moor, I will guard simplicity and return to my fields and garden. My house-Loss plot amounts to some ten acres, My thatched dwelling has eight or nine rooms. Elms and willows shade my back veranda, Peach and plum trees in rows before the hall. Hazy and dim the distant hamlet of men, Drifting the smoke above the houses. A dog barks in the deep lanes, A cock is crowing atop a mulberry tree. No dust and confusion within my doors and courtyard, In my empty rooms, more than sufficient leisure. Too long I was held within the barred cage. Now I am able to return again to Nature.
Translated by William Acker
Returning to Dwell in the Country — II · pp. 182-183
Shadow-play: the ruined dwellings
Long have I loved to stroll among the hills and marshes, And take my pleasure roaming the woods and fields. Now I hold hands with a troop of nieces and nephews, Parting the hazel growth we tread the untilled wastes— Wandering to and fro amidst the hills and mounds Everywhere around us we see dwellings of ancient men. Here are vestiges of their wells and hearthstones, There the rotted stumps of bamboo and mulberry groves. I stop and ask a firewood-gatherer: "These men—what has become of them?" The firewood-gatherer turns to me and says: "Once they were dead that was the end of them." In one generation the friends of court and market Are utterly gone—nothing is left! In very truth the life of man is like a shadow-play Which must in the end return to nothingness.
Translated by William Acker
Written While Drunk · p. 184
Perhaps the most famous recluse poem in Chinese literature
I built my house near where others dwell, And yet there is no clamour of carriages and horses. You ask of me "How can this be so?" "When the heart is far the place of itself is distant." I pluck chrysanthemums under the eastern hedge, And gaze afar towards the southern mountains. The mountain air is fine at evening of the day And flying birds return together homewards. Within these things there is a hint of Truth, But when I start to tell it, I cannot find the words.
Translated by William Acker
採菊東籬下，悠然見南山
"I pluck chrysanthemums under the eastern hedge, and gaze afar towards the southern mountains."
T'ao Ch'ien. The iconic image of the Chinese recluse.
On Reading the Classic of the Hills and Seas · pp. 184-185
Rural contentment: ploughing done, time for books
In the month of June the grass grows high And round my cottage thick-leaved branches sway. There is not a bird but delights in its place of rest And I too love my thatched cottage. I have done my ploughing; I have sown my seed. Again I have time to sit and read my books. The narrow lane— deep ruts on either side— Rather deters the carriages of friends! In high content I pour the spring wine And pick the lettuce in my garden. A fine rain comes from the east And a fresh wind bears it company. My thoughts float idly over the story of the King of Chou, My eyes wander over the pictures of Hills and Seas. At a single glance I survey the whole Universe. He will never be happy, whom such pleasures fail to please.
Translated by William Acker
A Song of Poor Scholars · p. 185
The "real pain" is that the Way does not prevail
They had not trodden the paths of power, Nor had the world flocked to their doors. A bed of straw was always warm enough, And fresh-gathered yams were good enough for breakfast. What they suffered was the real pain, Hunger and cold they did not feel at all.
Translated by William Acker
Written in Imitation of an Ancient Bearers' Song · p. 186
A funeral meditation
We try to speak but cannot utter words, We try to see but our eyes are dim. Once he used to sleep in a high-roofed house; Now he sleeps in the wilds among the grasses. Each morning he steps forth from the house gate— But the day of his return— when will it be?
Translated by William Acker
Putting the Blame on His Sons · p. 187
Humorous: the great recluse's lazy children
White hair shrouds both my temples, My skin and flesh have lost their fullness. Although I have five sons, Not a single one of them loves brush and paper. A-shu is twice times eight— In laziness he has no equal. A-hsuan is just fifteen, But cares for nothing in the world. Yung and Tuan are thirteen, And cannot add six to seven. T'ung-tzu is nearly nine, But only hunts for pear-trees and chestnut-trees. If such was Heaven's decree In spite of all that I could do, Bring on, bring on "the thing within the cup."
Translated by William Acker
Written on the Ninth Day of the Ninth Month of the Year Yi-yu · pp. 187-188
Autumn elegy: the Double Ninth Festival
Slowly, slowly, the autumn draws to its close, Cruelly cold the wind congeals the dew. Vines and grasses will not be green again— The trees in my garden are withering. The pure air is cleansed of lingering lees And mysteriously, Heaven's realms are high. Nothing is left of the spent cicada's song, A flock of geese goes crying down the sky. The Myriad Transformations open and shut by turns, And Man's life, how should it not be hard? From ancient times there was none but had to die, Remembering this scorches my very heart.
Translated by William Acker
"The life of man is like a shadow-play / Which must in the end return to nothingness." — T'ao Ch'ien, "Returning to Dwell in the Country" (tr. Acker)
Discussion Questions
Question 1
Chang Heng's dead man prefers death to life. Compare this with the Zhuangzi's "skull" passage (which it directly echoes). Is this Daoist philosophy or something darker, a poetry of despair born from political chaos?
Question 2
Juan Chi's Poem 3 contrasts the dark stork (which "vanishes into the sky") with "the cuckoos and the crows that circle round the Court." What political allegory is at work? Why does the stork's disappearance feel like triumph?
Question 3
T'ao Ch'ien calls official life "the Dusty Net" and "the barred cage." Is his withdrawal an act of freedom or privilege? Could a farmer or soldier make the same choice?
Question 4
"Written While Drunk" says "When the heart is far the place of itself is distant." What does this mean? Is seclusion a physical state or a mental one?
Question 5
The recluse tradition runs directly counter to the Confucian mandate of public service. How would Kongzi or Mengzi respond to T'ao Ch'ien? Is withdrawal ever justified in Confucian thought?
Question 6
These poets write during centuries of political collapse. Compare the recluse response to chaos with Han Feizi's Legalist response. Both diagnose the same problem (disorder), but the solutions are opposite (withdraw vs. control). Which is more honest about human nature?
