作者 THE AUTHOR
陸機 Lu Ji Portrait
陸機 Lu Ji 261–303 CE · Huating, Wu

Family and Origins

Lu Ji was born in 261 CE into one of the most powerful families in the Wu kingdom. His grandfather Lu Xun was Wu's most celebrated general and chancellor; his father Lu Kang served as commander of Wu's armed forces. The Lu clan of Huating (modern Songjiang, Shanghai) represented the southern aristocratic elite.

Fall of Wu

In 280 CE, the Jin dynasty conquered Wu, ending the Three Kingdoms period. Lu Ji was nineteen. He spent the next decade in retirement, studying and writing at the family estate in Huating. This period of enforced seclusion shaped his literary sensibility.

Journey to Luoyang

Around 289 CE, Lu Ji and his brother Lu Yun traveled to the Jin capital of Luoyang, where they quickly won literary fame. The literary critic Zhang Hua praised their talents, and Lu Ji was eventually appointed president of the national university. At Luoyang he composed the Wen fu, among other major works.

亂世 Political Catastrophe

War of the Eight Princes

Lu Ji became entangled in the Jin dynasty's internal power struggles. The War of the Eight Princes (291–306 CE) saw imperial relatives battling for control. Lu Ji was given military command by Sima Ying, Prince of Chengdu, but suffered a devastating defeat at the Battle of Heqiao in 303 CE.

Execution

Rivals at court used the military failure to bring false charges of treason. Lu Ji was executed in 303 CE, along with his two sons and two brothers. He was forty-three years old. Before his death, he reportedly sighed: "I shall never again hear the cranes cry at Huating." The Birch introduction notes he was "victim of a cutthroat struggle for power at the Chin court" (Birch, p. 203).

背景 HISTORICAL CONTEXT
六朝 The Period of Division 220–589 CE

A Fractured World

Lu Ji wrote during the Western Jin dynasty (266–316 CE), a brief reunification sandwiched between centuries of division. The fall of the Han in 220 CE had shattered the old imperial order. Three kingdoms competed for legitimacy, then Jin unified them by conquest, only to collapse within decades into civil war and northern invasions.

Literature in an Age of Instability

Paradoxically, political chaos produced literary brilliance. Writers no longer assumed a stable Confucian order where literature served the state. The period saw the rise of personal lyric poetry, literary criticism as a distinct pursuit, and intense reflection on what writing was for. Lu Ji's Wen fu belongs to this moment: an age when literature had to justify itself on its own terms, not simply as a tool of governance.

文賦 Why Write About Writing? The Wen Fu's Innovation

A Fu About Making Fu

The Wen fu is a rhyme-prose (fu) about the art of literary composition. Britannica calls it "a subtle and important work of literary criticism that defines and demonstrates the principles of composition." The choice of form is deliberate: Lu Ji writes a fu about writing, so the essay itself is a demonstration of the craft it describes. The medium embodies the message.

First Systematic Literary Theory

Encyclopedia.com notes that the Wen fu is "the most brilliant disquisition on literary creation by a Chinese up to its time." Earlier thinkers (Kongzi, Mengzi) made scattered remarks about literature. Cao Pi's "Discourse on Literature" (c. 217 CE) offered brief evaluations of contemporaries. Lu Ji went further: he analyzed the entire creative process from initial inspiration to the finished work, classified literary genres, diagnosed common failures, and reflected on the mysterious nature of artistic inspiration.

結構 STRUCTURE OF THE ESSAY
序 Prefatory Remarks Birch, p. 204

Lu Ji opens with a prose preface explaining his purpose. He has studied the great writers and gained insight into "their minds at work." But he confronts a fundamental problem: words fail to capture meaning, and meaning fails to capture reality.

"Constantly present is the feeling of regret that the meaning apprehended does not represent the objects observed; and, furthermore, words fail to convey the meaning." Birch, p. 204; trans. Shih-hsiang Chen

This gap between experience, understanding, and expression is the essay's central problem. Everything that follows is Lu Ji's attempt to bridge it.

十體 THE TEN GENRES (SECTION 5)
文類 Lu Ji's Taxonomy of Literary Forms Birch, p. 208

In Section 5, Lu Ji classifies literature into ten genres, giving each a one-line characterization. This is one of the earliest systematic genre taxonomies in Chinese literary criticism. Note the Birch text uses Wade-Giles romanization for these terms.

詩 Lyric shi (shih in Birch)
"born of pure emotion, is gossamer fibre woven into the finest fabric"
賦 Exhibitory Essay fu
"being true to the objects, is vividness incarnate"
碑 Inscription bei (pei in Birch)
"rhetoric must be a foil to facts"
誄 Elegy lei
"tenderly spins out ceaseless heartfelt grief"
銘 Mnemonic ming
"a smooth flow of genial phrases, succinct but pregnant"
箴 Epigram zhen (chen in Birch)
"staccato cadences... all transparent force"
頌 Eulogy song (sung in Birch)
"enjoys the full abandon of grand style"
論 Expository lun
"must in exactitude and clarity excel"
奏 Memorial zou (tsou in Birch)
"balanced and lucid, must be worthy of the dignity of its royal audience"
說 Argument shuo
"with glowing words and cunning parables persuades"

Why This Matters

  • Genre determines style: Each form demands a different relationship between emotion, truth, and ornament
  • No single standard: "The forms of things differ in a myriad ways, / For them there is no common measure" (p. 208)
  • Meaning governs form: "It is the all-harmonizing force of Meaning that integrates and bodies forth features supreme" (p. 208)
  • Contrast with the fu: The fu prizes vividness; the shi prizes pure emotion. Different tools, different purposes
名句 KEY PASSAGES
冥想 The Creative Process

Meditation (Section 2)

"All external vision and sound are suspended,
Perpetual thought itself gropes in time and space;
Then, the spirit at full gallop reaches the eight limits of the cosmos,
And the mind, self-buoyant, will ever soar to new insurmountable heights." Birch, p. 205; trans. Shih-hsiang Chen

Writing begins not with pen on paper but with an interior journey. The poet must first empty the senses, then let the mind range freely across all of time and space. Only after this cosmic meditation can the work of selecting and shaping begin.

Working Process (Section 3)

"But not until the heart attains calm transparency does thought crystallize
Into such words as no man before fancied or pronounced." Birch, p. 207; trans. Shih-hsiang Chen

Lu Ji describes composition as alternating between flow and obstruction. Ideas sometimes come with "sure touches and smooth rhythm"; at other moments, "as if beset by mountainous obstacles." The breakthrough requires calm transparency, not effort.

靈感 Inspiration and Purpose

Joy of Writing (Section 4)

"Writing is in itself a joy,
Yet saints and sages have long since held it in awe.
For it is Being, created by tasking the Great Void,
And 'tis sound rung out of Profound Silence." Birch, p. 207; trans. Shih-hsiang Chen

Writing is simultaneously pleasure and cosmic act. It brings Being from Void, sound from silence. The Daoist resonance is deliberate: Lu Ji draws on the Daodejing's language of emptiness and creation to frame literary work as participation in the generative force of the universe.

Of Inspiration (Section 10)

"Such moments when Mind and Matter hold perfect communion,
And wide vistas open to regions hitherto entirely barred,
Will come with irresistible force,
And go, their departure none can hinder." Birch, p. 213; trans. Shih-hsiang Chen

Inspiration cannot be commanded. Lu Ji compares it to flashes of light, to sounds arising in mid-air. Even when "the Secret may be held within oneself, / It is none the less beyond one's power to sway." This honest admission of the writer's helplessness before inspiration remains one of the most striking passages in world literary criticism.

The Use of Literature (Section 11)

"The use of literature
Lies in its conveyance of every truth.
It expands the horizon to make space infinite,
And serves as a bridge that spans a myriad years." Birch, p. 214; trans. Shih-hsiang Chen

The closing section elevates literature from craft to civilizational mission. Writing preserves the ancients, maps roads for posterity, and "manifests all the powers of transformation in which gods and spirits share." This is not entertainment. It is how a civilization speaks across time.

問題 DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
Form and Self-Reference
  • Lu Ji writes a fu about writing. How does the form of the essay demonstrate its own argument? Does the Wen fu succeed by its own criteria?
  • Compare Lu Ji's treatment of the fu genre ("being true to the objects, is vividness incarnate") with the fu we read from Sima Xiangru. Does the Shanglin fu fit Lu Ji's definition?
Inspiration and Control
  • Lu Ji says inspiration "will come with irresistible force, / And go, their departure none can hinder." If the writer cannot control inspiration, what can the writer control? What is the relationship between discipline and spontaneity in the essay?
  • How does Lu Ji's description of the creative process compare to your own experience of writing? What resonates? What doesn't?
Literature's Purpose
  • Lu Ji claims literature "serves as a bridge that spans a myriad years." How does this compare to the Confucian view that literature should serve moral instruction? Is Lu Ji's vision broader?
  • Lu Ji was executed three years after composing this essay on the eternal power of literature. Does knowing his fate change how you read Section 11?
  • How does the Wen fu relate to earlier texts we've read about the purpose of writing (e.g., the "Great Preface" to the Book of Songs)?
