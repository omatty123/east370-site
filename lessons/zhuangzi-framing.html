The Apophatic Tradition and the Zhuangzi

You pick up the Zhuangzi. It tells you there is no fixed right or wrong. It tells you to "just go along with things." It tells you a useless tree is the happiest tree. It tells you a man dreamed he was a butterfly and couldn't tell the difference.
It sounds like freedom. It sounds easy. It sounds like permission to stop worrying.
It is none of those things.

A Word You Need

What Is the Apophatic?

The word apophatic comes from the Greek apophasis: "to say away," "to un-say." It names a way of approaching the deepest truths by saying what they are not, rather than what they are.

The opposite is cataphatic ("to say toward"): positive statement. "God is good." "The Dao is the origin of all things." "Benevolence is the highest virtue." These are cataphatic claims. They say what something is.

The apophatic turn happens when a thinker concludes that the most important reality cannot be captured in positive statements. Not because we haven't found the right words yet, but because language itself—the act of defining, categorizing, asserting—distorts the thing it tries to describe. To say what it is would be to make it smaller than it is.

So you work by negation. You strip away. You say what the thing is not, and what's left—the silence after you've stopped talking—is closer to the truth than anything you could have said.

The Dao that can be spoken is not the enduring Dao.
The name that can be named is not the enduring name.

— Daodejing, Chapter 1

If you've already read the Laozi, you've encountered this. The opening line of the Daodejing is one of the most famous apophatic statements in world literature. But the Zhuangzi takes this impulse much further—and in a direction that is easy to misread.

This Is Not Only Daoist

The Apophatic Across Traditions

The apophatic impulse shows up independently in many traditions. Recognizing it elsewhere helps us see what Zhuangzi is and is not doing. In every case, notice the pattern: the thing that cannot be said is treated as the most important thing, and getting to it requires discipline, not relaxation.

Christian Mysticism

Pseudo-Dionysius, Meister Eckhart, The Cloud of Unknowing

God is beyond all categories. To say "God is good" limits God to our concept of goodness. True knowledge of God comes through unknowing—systematically stripping away every attribute until you are left in "divine darkness."

"The most godly knowledge of God is that which is known by unknowing." — Pseudo-Dionysius, 5th c. CE

This is not casual. Apophatic Christian mystics underwent extreme ascetic discipline. The "unknowing" was the result of rigorous practice, not its absence.

Madhyamaka Buddhism

Nagarjuna, 2nd c. CE

All phenomena are empty (sunyata) of inherent existence. Nothing has a fixed self-nature. Every concept we use to grasp reality—including "emptiness" itself—must be negated.

"Emptiness wrongly grasped is like picking up a poisonous snake by the wrong end." — Nagarjuna

Nagarjuna's point is that if you turn emptiness into a position—"I believe everything is empty"—you've missed it entirely. The negation must negate itself. This requires ferocious intellectual and meditative discipline.

Daoist Tradition

Laozi, Zhuangzi, 4th c. BCE

The Dao cannot be named, defined, or grasped through deliberate effort. The highest virtue (de) doesn't know itself as virtuous. The sage acts through non-action (wu wei)—not passivity, but action so aligned with the natural order that it leaves no trace.

"The Way is obscured by small completions. Words are obscured by glory and show." — Zhuangzi, Ch. 2

Zhuangzi's version is unique: he uses humor, paradox, and literary absurdity as apophatic tools. The jokes aren't decoration. They're the method.

In every case, the pattern is the same: positive assertion about ultimate reality is treated as a trap. And in every case, the alternative to positive assertion is not "anything goes"—it is a demanding practice of self-discipline, self-emptying, or self-transformation.

The question for us today: what does Zhuangzi's version of this demand?

Reading Today

莊子 Zhuangzi's Apophatic Method

Zhuangzi doesn't just argue that language can't capture the Dao. He performs it. His entire literary strategy is apophatic. Look at the tools he uses in today's reading:

1. Stories that refuse to deliver a moral. The Kun/Peng fable opens Chapter 1 with cosmic grandeur—and then the text immediately retells the same story from a different source, slightly differently. Which version is right? The question is the point.

2. Self-undermining arguments. Chapter 2 argues that all distinctions between "right" and "wrong" are perspectival. But this argument is itself a distinction. Zhuangzi knows this: "If we're already one, can I say it? But since I've just said we're one, can I not say it? The unity and my saying it make two." The argument eats itself—on purpose.

3. Humor as negation. The monkey trainer gives 3 in the morning and 4 at night; the monkeys are furious. He gives 4 in the morning and 3 at night; they're delighted. Nothing changed. You laugh. And in laughing, you've already felt—before you could articulate it—that our distinctions might be equally empty. The joke does what the argument can't.

4. Reductio on rival positions. Confucians say X is right, Mohists say X is wrong. Zhuangzi doesn't pick a side. He asks: "if you want to right their wrongs and wrong their rights, it's better to throw them open to the light." This isn't neutrality. It's a refusal of the entire game.

The Method Is the Message

A cataphatic philosopher gives you a thesis and defends it. Zhuangzi gives you a fish that becomes a bird, a man who becomes a butterfly, a tree that wins by losing. None of these can be reduced to a propositional claim. That irreducibility is the point.

The text isn't failing to be clear. It is succeeding at being apophatic.

Read Carefully

The Trap for Western Readers

Western students read the Zhuangzi and immediately think they recognize it. Within minutes they'll reach for one of these labels:

The Label What Students Think What Zhuangzi Actually Does
Relativism There's no right or wrong, so anything goes. There IS a Way. There ARE perfected people. But the Way cannot be reached through the activity of asserting right and wrong.
Libertarianism / Anarchism The individual should be free from social constraint. The self must be dissolved, not liberated. "Perfect people have no self." Freedom comes from having no one left to be free.
Skepticism We can't know anything, so why bother? Rational argument is limited, but intuition, skill, and experience give real access to the Way. The cook's knife knows the ox.
Postmodernism / Deconstruction All meaning is constructed and unstable. Language is limited, but there is a reality beyond it. The Dao is real. The problem is our tools, not the world.
"Chill Out" Philosophy Stop worrying, go with the flow, be cool. "Going along with things" requires the total dissolution of ego, preference, and self-identity. This is closer to monastic discipline than to relaxation.

Why This Matters

Every one of these misreadings turns Zhuangzi into a version of a familiar Western idea—usually one that flatters the reader. "Everything is relative? Great, then my opinions are as good as anyone's." "No right or wrong? Awesome, no one can tell me what to do."

But Zhuangzi isn't telling you that your opinions are fine. He's telling you that having opinions—the very structure of opinionating—is the problem. And the solution is not more comfortable than the problem. It is harder.

The Hard Part

無己 What the Text Actually Demands

Look at the hierarchy in Chapter 1. It is a hierarchy—not a leveling. Zhuangzi ranks different ways of being, and judges most of them insufficient:

Petty Officials

"People who know how to do one job, handle a small town, or impress a ruler to get put in charge of a state see themselves like this."

Trapped in ego and worldly ambition

宋子 Songzi

"The whole world could praise him and he would not be encouraged. The whole world could condemn him and he would not be upset."

Impressive—but "he stops there"

列子 Liezi

"Liezi rides about on the wind. It's wonderful! He's gone two weeks at a time."

Transcends ordinary limits—but "he still relies on something"

至人 The Perfected Person

"If he could chariot the norms of Heaven and earth and ride the changes in the six mists to wander the inexhaustible, then what would there be to rely on?"

No self. No accomplishment. No name.

Each level is judged not good enough. Songzi is indifferent to praise and blame—but he still draws a line between inner and outer. Liezi rides the wind—but the wind is still something he depends on. The standard is total non-dependence.

And the thesis statement of the chapter: "Perfect people have no self (wu ji 無己), spiritual people have no accomplishment (wu gong 無功), sagely people have no name (wu ming 無名)."

Read that carefully. This is not "be yourself." This is ego annihilation. The word is wu 無—"without," "non-," "nothingness." No self. No achievement. No reputation. Everything that makes you you—your preferences, your judgments, your identity—is what must be dissolved.

The "free and easy wandering" of the chapter title? That's the name for what it looks like from the outside once you've done this impossible work. From the inside, there's no one left to experience it as freedom.

The Paradox at the Heart

The apophatic pattern holds: the highest attainment is described by negation. Not by what the perfected person has, but by what they lack. No self. No accomplishment. No name.

This is not "anything goes." This is the most demanding standard imaginable—a standard defined by what you must give up, not what you get to keep.

Chapter 2 shows the same pattern at the level of thought. Master Dapple doesn't just have an interesting idea. He loses himself. His student says: "The one leaning on the armrest now is not the one who leaned on it before." His body becomes "dried wood." His heart becomes "dead ashes." This is the language of radical meditative transformation, not of casual open-mindedness.

And the butterfly dream at the end? Zhuangzi does NOT say there's no difference between Zhuangzi and the butterfly. He says: "there must be some difference between them!" The point isn't nihilism. It's that identity is far more porous than we assume—and experiencing that porosity is called hua 化, "the transformation of things." Not a concept. An event.

The Crucial Distinction

Apophatic vs. Permissive

Here is the distinction to hold in your mind as you read today:

Permissive Reading Apophatic Reading
"No right or wrong" Your views are as good as anyone's. Do what feels right to you. The activity of judging right/wrong is itself the obstacle. The sage doesn't pick a better side; the sage steps off the see-saw.
"Go along with things" Relax. Don't overthink it. Be chill. Dissolve the self that would impose its preferences on reality. This requires total transformation, not a change of attitude.
"Uselessness" Being lazy is fine. Achievement is overrated. Refusing to be defined by human categories of value is how you align with Heaven/Nature (Tian). The tree isn't lazy; it's free of the system that would cut it down.
"The Butterfly Dream" Nothing is real, man. "There must be some difference!" The boundary between identities is real but permeable. This is about transformation (hua), not illusion.
"Wandering" Do whatever you want. Freedom! Wandering without dependence requires first having nothing to depend on: no self, no achievement, no name. This is the end of a path, not its beginning.

The permissive readings are seductive because they make Zhuangzi into a philosopher who affirms what you already wanted to do. The apophatic reading is uncomfortable because it asks you to give up the very thing doing the wanting.

For Discussion

討論 Today's Questions
  1. The Ladder: Chapter 1 presents Songzi and Liezi as admirable but incomplete. What is each one still "relying on"? Can you imagine what it would mean to rely on nothing at all? Is that even humanly possible?
  2. Humor as Method: Why does Zhuangzi use jokes, fables, and absurd stories instead of arguments? Think about the monkey trainer: could you state its "lesson" in a sentence? If you could, would that sentence do the same work as the story?
  3. Self-Undermining: Chapter 2 argues that all positions are perspectival—but that's itself a position. Zhuangzi knows this ("since I've just said we're one, can I not say it?"). Is this a flaw in his thinking or part of his method?
  4. Against Mengzi: Zhuangzi says "the sprouts of benevolence and righteousness and the pathways of right and wrong are all snarled and jumbled." Compare this to Mengzi's confidence that humans have innate moral sprouts. Who is more persuasive, and why?
  5. The Big One: If "perfect people have no self," then who is doing the wandering? Is the goal of the Zhuangzi liberation or dissolution? Is there a difference?

Further Reading

Scholars on These Questions

On Zhuangzi's Philosophy

Brook Ziporyn, Zhuangzi: The Essential Writings (2009)—the best annotated English translation, with an introduction that takes the apophatic dimension seriously.

A.C. Graham, Chuang-Tzu: The Inner Chapters (1981)—the landmark scholarly translation. Graham's introduction remains one of the best short essays on Zhuangzi's thought.

On Wu Wei & Cultivation

Edward Slingerland, Effortless Action (2003)—on the "paradox of wu wei": effortless action requires tremendous effort to achieve.

Michael Puett, The Ambivalence of Creation (2001)—on self-cultivation as discipline in Warring States thought, including Zhuangzi.

On the Apophatic

Denys Turner, The Darkness of God (1995)—the clearest introduction to apophatic theology in the Christian tradition. Useful for comparison.

Jay Garfield, The Fundamental Wisdom of the Middle Way (1995)—Nagarjuna's Mulamadhyamakakarika translated with philosophical commentary.
