Knowing by Unknowing

The Apophatic Tradition and the Zhuangzi
Course Portal Apophatic? Traditions Zhuangzi The Trap Demands Discussion

You pick up the Zhuangzi. It tells you there is no fixed right or wrong. It tells you to "just go along with things." A man dreamed he was a butterfly and couldn't tell the difference.

It sounds like freedom. It sounds easy. It sounds like permission to stop worrying.

It is none of those things.

A Word You Need

What Is the Apophatic?

Apophatic comes from the Greek apophasis: "to say away," "to un-say." It names a way of approaching the deepest truths by saying what they are not rather than what they are.

The opposite is cataphatic ("to say toward"): positive statement. "God is good." "The Dao is the origin of all things." These are cataphatic claims. They say what something is.

The apophatic turn happens when a thinker concludes that the most important reality cannot be captured in positive statements. Not because we haven't found the right words yet, but because language itself, the act of defining and categorizing, distorts the thing it tries to describe. To say what it is would be to make it smaller than it is.

So you work by negation. You strip away. You say what the thing is not, and what remains, the silence after you've stopped talking, is closer to the truth than anything you could have said.

The Dao that can be spoken is not the enduring Dao.
The name that can be named is not the enduring name.

Daodejing, Chapter 1

If you've already read the Laozi, you've encountered this. The opening line of the Daodejing is one of the most famous apophatic statements in world literature. The Zhuangzi takes this impulse much further, and in a direction that is easy to misread.

This Is Not Only Daoist

The Apophatic Across Traditions

The apophatic impulse shows up independently in many traditions. In every case, notice the pattern: the thing that cannot be said is treated as the most important thing, and getting to it requires discipline, not relaxation.

Christian Via Negativa

Desert Fathers, Pseudo-Dionysius, 3rd-6th c. CE

The via negativa ("negative way") holds that God cannot be grasped by the mind. Every concept, image, or name we apply to God falls short. Even calling God "good" or "powerful" limits the limitless. The only adequate response is silence.

"The most godly knowledge of God is that which is known by unknowing." (Pseudo-Dionysius, 5th c.)

The Desert Fathers practiced this as radical silence. Pseudo-Dionysius systematized it: God is beyond being, beyond goodness, beyond all categories. You approach the divine by stripping away every attribute until you enter what he called "the divine darkness."

Madhyamaka Buddhism

Nagarjuna, 2nd c. CE

All phenomena are empty (sunyata) of inherent existence. Every concept we use to grasp reality, including "emptiness" itself, must be negated.

"Emptiness wrongly grasped is like picking up a poisonous snake by the wrong end." (Nagarjuna)

If you turn emptiness into a position, saying "I believe everything is empty," you've missed it entirely. The negation must negate itself.

Daoist Tradition

Laozi, Zhuangzi, 4th c. BCE

The Dao cannot be named, defined, or grasped through deliberate effort. The sage acts through non-action (wu wei): not passivity, but action so aligned with the natural order that it leaves no trace.

"The Way is obscured by small completions. Words are obscured by glory and show." (Zhuangzi, Ch. 2)

Zhuangzi's version is unique: he uses humor, paradox, and literary absurdity as apophatic tools. The jokes aren't decoration. They're the method.

Reading Today

莊子 Zhuangzi's Apophatic Method

Zhuangzi doesn't just argue that language can't capture the Dao. He performs it. His entire literary strategy is apophatic:

1. Stories that refuse to deliver a moral. The Kun/Peng fable opens Chapter 1 with cosmic grandeur, then the text immediately retells the same story from a different source, slightly differently. Which version is right? The question is the point.

2. Self-undermining arguments. Chapter 2 argues that all distinctions between "right" and "wrong" are perspectival. But this argument is itself a distinction. Zhuangzi knows this: "If we're already one, can I say it?" The argument eats itself. On purpose.

3. Humor as negation. The monkey trainer gives 3 in the morning and 4 at night; the monkeys are furious. He switches to 4 and 3; they're delighted. Nothing changed. You laugh, and in laughing you've already felt, before you could articulate it, that our distinctions might be equally empty.

4. Reductio on rival positions. Confucians say X is right, Mohists say X is wrong. Zhuangzi doesn't pick a side: "if you want to right their wrongs and wrong their rights, it's better to throw them open to the light." Not neutrality. A refusal of the entire game.

The Method Is the Message

A cataphatic philosopher gives you a thesis and defends it. Zhuangzi gives you a fish that becomes a bird, a man who becomes a butterfly, a tree that wins by losing. None of these can be reduced to a propositional claim. That irreducibility is the point.

Read Carefully

The Trap for Western Readers

Western students read the Zhuangzi and immediately think they recognize it:

The Label What Students Think What Zhuangzi Actually Does
Relativism No right or wrong, so anything goes. There IS a Way. There ARE perfected people. But the Way cannot be reached through the activity of asserting right and wrong.
Anarchism The individual should be free from social constraint. The self must be dissolved, not liberated. "Perfect people have no self." Freedom comes from having no one left to be free.
Skepticism We can't know anything, so why bother? Rational argument is limited, but intuition, skill, and experience give real access to the Way. The cook's knife knows the ox.
Postmodernism All meaning is constructed and unstable. Language is limited, but there is a reality beyond it. The Dao is real. The problem is our tools, not the world.
"Chill Out" Stop worrying, go with the flow. "Going along with things" requires the total dissolution of ego, preference, and self-identity. Closer to monastic discipline than to relaxation.

Every one of these misreadings turns Zhuangzi into a version of a familiar Western idea, usually one that flatters the reader. But Zhuangzi isn't telling you that your opinions are fine. He's telling you that having opinions, the very structure of opinionating, is the problem. The solution is not more comfortable than the problem. It is harder.

The Hard Part

無己 What the Text Actually Demands

Look at the hierarchy in Chapter 1. It is a hierarchy, not a leveling. Zhuangzi ranks different ways of being and judges most of them insufficient:

Petty Officials

"People who know how to do one job, handle a small town, or impress a ruler."

Trapped in ego and worldly ambition

宋子 Songzi

"The whole world could praise him and he would not be encouraged."

Impressive, but "he stops there"

列子 Liezi

"Liezi rides about on the wind. It's wonderful!"

Transcends ordinary limits, but "he still relies on something"

至人 The Perfected Person

"What would there be to rely on?"

No self. No accomplishment. No name.

Each level is judged not good enough. Songzi is indifferent to praise and blame, yet he still draws a line between inner and outer. Liezi rides the wind, yet the wind is still something he depends on. The standard is total non-dependence.

The thesis statement: "Perfect people have no self (wu ji 無己), spiritual people have no accomplishment (wu gong 無功), sagely people have no name (wu ming 無名)."

This is not "be yourself." This is ego annihilation. The word is wu 無, meaning "without," "non-," "nothingness." No self. No achievement. No reputation. Everything that makes you you, your preferences, your judgments, your identity, is what must be dissolved.

The Paradox at the Heart

The highest attainment is described by negation. Not by what the perfected person has, but by what they lack. No self. No accomplishment. No name. This is not "anything goes." It is the most demanding standard imaginable: a standard defined by what you must give up, not what you get to keep.

The "free and easy wandering" of Chapter 1? That's the name for what it looks like from the outside once you've done this impossible work. From the inside, there's no one left to experience it as freedom.

For Discussion

討論 Today's Questions
  1. The Ladder: What is Songzi still "relying on"? What about Liezi? Can you imagine what it would mean to rely on nothing at all?
  2. Humor as Method: Could you state the monkey trainer's "lesson" in a sentence? If you could, would that sentence do the same work as the story?
  3. Self-Undermining: Chapter 2 argues that all positions are perspectival, but that's itself a position. Flaw or method?
  4. Against Mengzi: Zhuangzi says "the sprouts of benevolence and righteousness are all snarled and jumbled." Compare this to Mengzi's confidence in innate moral sprouts. Who is more persuasive?
  5. The Big One: If "perfect people have no self," then who is doing the wandering?
