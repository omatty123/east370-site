Mozi founded Mohism, arguably China's first systematically organized philosophical movement. A strong argument: Mozi, not Confucius, is China's first philosopher—the first to construct explicit arguments rather than relying on received wisdom.

Part One
兼愛·非攻

Ethics: Inclusive Care

Key Terms

Chinese Pinyin Meaning
兼愛 jiān ài Inclusive care; impartial concern for all
非攻 fēi gōng Condemning aggression; anti-war doctrine
義 yì Righteousness; objective moral standard
利 lì Benefit; the ultimate criterion

Inclusive Care (兼愛)

The Core Problem: Theft, assault, exploitation, war—all stem from "partiality": people caring only for themselves and their families at others' expense.

The Solution: Care for all equally. "If people regarded others' states as they regard their own, who would attack?" Universal benefit replaces partial interest.

Against Confucian Graded Love: Mozi rejects the idea that we should love family more than strangers—this partiality causes conflict.

兼相愛，交相利
"Inclusive mutual care, reciprocal mutual benefit"
The Mohist ethical formula

Condemning Aggression (非攻)

Logical Extension: If we should care for all equally, aggressive warfare—harming the innocent—is the greatest crime.

Practical Commitment: Mohists were famous for defending besieged cities against unjust attacks, developing defensive military technology.

Consistency Test: If killing one person is murder, why is killing thousands in war "glorious"? The logic is identical—only the scale differs.

Moderation Doctrines

節用 Moderation in Use: Resources spent on luxury could feed the hungry. Condemns waste.

節葬 Moderation in Burial: Elaborate Confucian funerals impoverish the living without helping the dead. "Like seeking harvest by burning seeds."

非樂 Against Music: Court ceremonies with bells and drums waste resources. What does ritual music produce? Nothing useful.

"If people regarded others' states as they regard their own, who would attack others' states? ... If people regarded others' families as they regard their own, who would bring disorder to others' families?" — Mozi, "Inclusive Care"
Part Two
尚賢·尚同

Politics: Order and Authority

Key Terms

Chinese Pinyin Meaning
尚賢 shàng xián Promoting the worthy; meritocracy
尚同 shàng tóng Conforming upward; unified standards
天志 tiān zhì Heaven's intent; ultimate moral source
鬼神 guǐ shén Ghosts and spirits; moral enforcers

Promoting the Worthy (尚賢)

Against Hereditary Rule: Officials should be chosen by merit, not birth. "If capable, promote him. If incapable, demote him."

Radical Meritocracy: Even farmers and artisans can rise to office if worthy. Talent determines status, not family background.

Conforming Upward (尚同)

The Problem: "With one person, one sense of right; with ten, ten senses." Moral chaos from conflicting opinions.

The Solution: Unified standards. Align judgments with superiors, ultimately with Heaven. Creates moral consensus.

Heaven and Spirits

天志 Heaven's Intent: Heaven loves all impartially and wants their welfare. Provides the ultimate moral standard.

明鬼 Understanding Ghosts: Spirits enforce morality when humans fail. Even if you doubt their existence, belief is socially useful—spirits deter crime.

"In ancient times, when people were first born... everyone had their own sense of right and wrong. The more people, the more different senses. So the world was in chaos." — Mozi, "Conforming Upward"

Part Three
三表

Method: The Three Standards

Key Terms

Chinese Pinyin Meaning
法 fǎ Model, standard—like carpenter's square
三表 sān biǎo Three standards; epistemological test
類 lèi Kind, category—basis of analogy
非命 fēi mìng Condemning fatalism

Why Standards Matter

Against Intuition: Confucians rely on cultivated moral sense. Mozi objects: customs vary between cultures. We need objective criteria.

The Craftsman's Model: A carpenter uses a square and compass, not feelings. Ethics needs the same precision—explicit standards (法 fa) for judgment.

本

Root / Precedent

Does it accord with the sage-kings' practice? Historical evidence a policy can work.

原

Source / Evidence

Can ordinary people verify it through observation? Empirical grounding.

用

Use / Consequences

Does it benefit state and people in practice? Results are the ultimate test.

Against Fatalism (非命)

Applying the Standards:

Root: Sage-kings didn't believe in fate—they worked hard and achieved order.

Source: We observe hard workers prosper while the lazy fail.

Use: Fatalism makes people passive; rejecting it encourages effort.

Therefore: fatalism fails all three standards.

Mohist Legacy

Organization: Followers formed a disciplined movement led by "Grand Masters" (巨子 juzi), willing to die for their principles.

Later Mohists: Developed sophisticated logic, optics, geometry, and mechanics—China's closest equivalent to Greek natural philosophy.

Decline: Mohism faded after Han dynasty but influenced Legalism and Chinese consequentialist thinking.

言必有三表
"Every doctrine must have three standards"
Mozi's epistemological principle

Discussion Questions

Question 1

Is Mencius right that "inclusive care" undermines family bonds? Can you care for your parents AND care equally for strangers? What's the difference between treating everyone fairly and loving everyone identically?

Question 2

Mozi judges actions by their consequences (benefit to society). What are the strengths and weaknesses of this approach? Can good intentions matter even if outcomes are bad?

Question 3

Mozi's "three standards" combine precedent, observation, and practical results. Is this a good epistemology? What's missing? How does it compare to modern scientific method?

Question 4

Is Mozi's argument against music convincing? Should resources spent on arts and entertainment be redirected to material needs? What value does music have that Mozi misses?

Question 5

Mozi argues belief in spirits is useful even if spirits don't exist. Is this a good argument? Is it ethical to promote beliefs for their social effects rather than their truth?

Question 6

"Promoting the worthy" sounds good—but who decides who's worthy? Could Mozi's meritocracy create its own kind of elite? How does this compare to modern meritocracy debates?
