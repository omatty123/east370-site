墨子

The Mozi

Author: Mo Di (Master Mo) Period: ~470–391 BCE Reading: Ivanhoe pp. 57–108
Course Portal Interactive Map Overview Key Terms Ten Doctrines Three Standards vs. Confucius Discussion

Mozi (墨子, "Master Mo") founded Mohism, arguably the first systematically organized philosophical movement in Chinese history. A strong argument can be made that it is Mozi, not Confucius, who deserves the title of China's first philosopher—the first to construct explicit arguments for his positions rather than relying on received wisdom.

"The benevolent person's planning on behalf of the world is in no respect different from the filial son's planning on behalf of his parents."

Mozi likely emerged from the artisan class and may have been a carpenter. His followers formed a highly organized movement with military discipline, led by a series of "Grand Masters" (巨子 jùzǐ). The Mohists were famous for their austerity, their opposition to aggressive warfare, and their willingness to die defending besieged cities against unjust attacks.

Unlike Confucius, who relied on cultivated moral intuition and ritual propriety, Mozi demanded explicit standards (fǎ) for evaluating claims and policies. His consequentialist ethics judged actions by their effects on social order, material welfare, and population growth.

Heaven. For Mozi, an active moral agent that rewards the good and punishes the wicked. The ultimate standard of righteousness.

Core Teaching

十論 The Ten Doctrines

The Mohists presented their philosophy through ten doctrines organized in five complementary pairs.

1

Inclusive Care & Condemning Aggression

兼愛 · 非攻

Inclusive Care (兼愛 Jiān Ài) is the heart of Mohist ethics. Mozi argued that the world's problems—theft, assault, exploitation, war—all stem from "partiality": people caring only for themselves and their own families at others' expense.

"Suppose there were two men... One holds to partiality and the other holds to inclusiveness. The one who holds to partiality would say, 'How could I care for my friend's parents as I care for my own?' But the one who holds to inclusiveness would say, 'I will care for my friend's parents as I care for my own.'"

Condemning Aggression (非攻 Fēi Gōng) follows logically: if we should care for all equally, then aggressive warfare—which harms the innocent—is the greatest crime. The Mohists were famous for defending besieged cities and developing defensive military technology.

2

Promoting the Worthy & Conforming Upward

尚賢 · 尚同

Promoting the Worthy (尚賢 Shàng Xián) attacks hereditary aristocracy. Officials should be chosen by merit, not birth. "If a person is capable, promote him. If incapable, demote him."

Conforming Upward (尚同 Shàng Tóng) establishes a moral hierarchy: people should align their judgments with their superiors, ultimately with Heaven itself. This creates unified moral standards rather than conflicting opinions.

"In ancient times, when people were first born... everyone had their own sense of right and wrong (yi). With one person there was one sense of right and wrong; with two people, two senses; with ten, ten senses. The more people, the more different senses."
3

Moderation in Use & Moderation in Burial

節用 · 節葬

Moderation in Use (節用 Jié Yòng) condemns waste. Resources spent on luxury could feed the hungry and clothe the cold.

Moderation in Burial (節葬 Jié Zàng) directly attacks Confucian funeral practices. Elaborate coffins, long mourning periods, and burial goods impoverish the living without helping the dead.

"If we try to enrich the state by means of lavish burials and prolonged mourning, it would be like seeking a full harvest by burning the seeds."
4

Heaven's Intent & Understanding Ghosts

天志 · 明鬼

Heaven's Intent (天志 Tiān Zhì) provides the ultimate moral standard. Heaven loves all people impartially and wants their welfare. Rulers who follow Heaven's will bring prosperity; those who violate it are punished.

Understanding Ghosts (明鬼 Míng Guǐ) argues that spirits exist and enforce morality. Even if you doubt their existence, Mozi argues it's socially beneficial to believe—spirits deter crime when human authorities fail.

5

Condemning Music & Condemning Fatalism

非樂 · 非命

Condemning Music (非樂 Fēi Yuè) targets Confucian emphasis on ritual music. The bells, drums, and performances of court ceremonies waste resources and distract from productive labor.

Condemning Fatalism (非命 Fēi Mìng) attacks the belief that fate determines outcomes. If people believe their lot is fixed, they won't strive to improve. Mozi insists human effort matters.

"If we establish the doctrine of fatalism and thereby instruct the common people, they will become lazy and neglect their work. This is not the way to govern the state."

Mohist Method

三表 The Three Standards

Mozi's epistemological test: any claim must pass three criteria to be accepted.

本

Root / Precedent

Does it accord with the practice of the ancient sage-kings? Historical precedent provides evidence that a policy can work.

原

Source / Evidence

Can it be verified by what ordinary people see and hear? Empirical observation grounds claims in reality.

用

Use / Consequences

Does it benefit the state and the people when put into practice? Practical results are the ultimate test.

辯 Example: Argument Against Fatalism

  1. Root: The sage-kings Yao, Shun, and Yu did not believe in fate—they worked hard and achieved order.
  2. Source: We observe that people who work hard prosper, while the lazy fail.
  3. Use: Believing in fate makes people passive; rejecting fate encourages effort and improves society.

Therefore, fatalism fails all three standards and should be rejected.

非儒 Mozi vs. Confucius

The Mozi contains explicit chapters titled "Against the Confucians" (非儒). While Mozi may have originally studied Confucianism, he came to reject its core assumptions.

Against Ritual Propriety (禮 Lǐ): Confucians treat traditional ritual as the foundation of morality. Mozi objects: customs vary between cultures. If ritual is just "what we've always done," it can't provide objective moral guidance.
Against Graded Love: Confucians say we should love family more than strangers. Mozi argues this "partiality" causes conflict—I favor my family at your family's expense. Only impartial care creates harmony.
Against Lavish Funerals: Confucian filial piety demands elaborate burials and three-year mourning. Mozi calculates: this wastes resources, weakens the living, and reduces the population. It's economically disastrous.
Against Music: Confucians see ritual music as morally cultivating. Mozi asks: what does it produce? Nothing. The resources for bells and drums should feed the hungry.

Mencius later responded that Mohist "inclusive care" amounts to "having no father"—a devastating insult implying Mozi's ethics would destroy the family itself.

Discussion Questions

討論 For Class
  1. Impartiality vs. Special Relationships: Is Mencius right that "inclusive care" undermines family bonds? Can you care for your parents AND care equally for strangers? What's the difference between treating everyone fairly and loving everyone identically?
  2. Consequentialism: Mozi judges actions by their consequences (benefit to society). What are the strengths and weaknesses of this approach? Can good intentions matter even if outcomes are bad?
  3. Standards for Truth: Mozi's "three standards" combine precedent, observation, and practical results. Is this a good epistemology? What's missing? How does it compare to modern scientific method?
  4. Against Music: Is Mozi's argument against music convincing? Should resources spent on arts and entertainment be redirected to material needs? What value does music have that Mozi misses?
  5. Religion as Social Tool: Mozi argues belief in spirits is useful even if spirits don't exist. Is this a good argument? Is it ethical to promote beliefs for their social effects rather than their truth?
  6. Meritocracy: "Promoting the worthy" sounds good—but who decides who's worthy? Could Mozi's system create its own kind of elite? How is it different from modern meritocracy debates?
