概要 OVERVIEW
慧能 Huineng 638–713 CE

The Sixth Patriarch

Huineng (慧能, 638–713) is widely regarded as the founder of the Southern School of Chan Buddhism, better known in the modern West by its Japanese name, Zen. Perhaps more than any other spiritual leader, Huineng put a distinctively Chinese stamp on the Buddhist tradition, combining Chinese and especially Daoist views about agency and mental discipline with originally Indian Buddhist ideas about enlightenment and salvation.

An Illiterate Woodcutter

The Platform Sutra emphasizes that Huineng was uneducated and illiterate. This is not incidental. It is a philosophical claim: insight is wholly innate, not acquired through learning. A woodcutter from the southern frontier can surpass the head monk of an entire monastery, not despite his lack of education but because enlightenment has nothing to do with book-learning.

壇經 The Platform Sutra Selection 16

What Is It?

The Platform Sutra of the Sixth Patriarch (Liuzu Tanjing 六祖壇經) purports to be a written transcription of Huineng's lectures. It is one of the classics of Chinese Buddhism. The translation here by Philip J. Ivanhoe is based on the Dunhuang manuscript version.

What Makes It Radical

The Platform Sutra begins with Huineng's rise from menial laborer to standard-bearer for the Chan tradition. It then proceeds to elucidate several of his core views on Buddhist wisdom and practice. The text draws from both the biographical and expositional portions of the sutra.

Three claims make this text revolutionary:

  • Insight is innate: Every person already possesses perfect understanding and Buddha-nature. Enlightenment is not gained; it is uncovered.
  • Sudden, not gradual: Enlightenment happens in a flash of recognition, not through incremental stages of cultivation.
  • Practice is life itself: "Sitting meditation" does not mean sitting motionless. It means maintaining a straightforward mind in all activities, at all times.
偈頌 THE POEM CONTEST (SECTIONS 4, 6, 8)
傳法 The Fifth Patriarch's Challenge pp. 92–93

Write a Poem, Win the Dharma

The Fifth Patriarch Hongren gathers his disciples and issues a challenge: each should compose a poem demonstrating their understanding of enlightenment. Whoever shows the deepest insight will receive the robe and the dharma, becoming the Sixth Patriarch.

"I say to you that birth and death are great affairs for the people of the world. You disciples practice self-cultivation all day long, but all you seek is [rebirth in] blessed realms. You do not seek to escape from the bitter sea of birth and death." p. 92; trans. Philip J. Ivanhoe

The head monk Shenxiu writes his poem secretly on the wall at midnight. The illiterate Huineng hears it read aloud, recognizes its limitation, and composes his own counter-poem.

對照 Two Poems, Two Philosophies
Shenxiu (Head Monk)
The body is the tree of insight (prajnā);
The mind is like a clear mirror.
Always clean and polish it;
Never allow dirt or dust!
p. 93
Huineng (Illiterate Laborer)
Insight fundamentally has no tree;
The bright mirror has no stand.
Buddha-nature is always pure and clean;
How could there ever be dirt or dust?
p. 93

What's the Difference?

  • Shenxiu: The mind exists and must be actively maintained. Enlightenment requires constant effort, like polishing a mirror.
  • Huineng: There is no mirror to polish. Buddha-nature is already perfectly pure. The very idea that you need to "clean" it is the delusion.
  • Gradual vs. sudden: Shenxiu implies gradual cultivation. Huineng negates the framework entirely.
  • The deeper claim: If insight "has no tree," then the body/mind duality itself is an illusion. There is nothing to clean, nothing to be cleaned.

The Fifth Patriarch publicly dismisses Huineng's poem but secretly summons him at night, confirms him as the true heir, and gives him the robe and dharma. Huineng flees south to avoid the jealousy of the other monks.

教義 CORE TEACHINGS (SECTIONS 12–19)
定慧 Stability and Insight Sections 13–15

One Substance, Not Two

Huineng insists that stability (ding 定) and insight (hui 慧) are not two separate things. Stability is the Substance of insight; insight is the Function of stability. They relate like a lamp and its light: where there is a lamp, there is light, and without the lamp there is no light. You cannot have one without the other.

"Stability and insight are one Substance, not two. Stability is the Substance of insight; insight is the Function of stability. Where there is insight, stability is within the insight. Where there is stability, insight is within the stability." p. 94; trans. Philip J. Ivanhoe

Against False Meditation

This unity has a sharp practical consequence. Do not say that stability precedes insight or insight precedes stability. Those who treat them as separate "profess a dualistic teaching" (p. 94). The practice of enlightenment "does not lie in verbal arguments."

坐禪 What Meditation Really Means Sections 14, 18–19

Not Sitting Motionless

Huineng launches a direct attack on a common form of Chan practice: sitting motionless, eliminating delusions, and not allowing thoughts to arise. He considers this a grave mistake that has driven practitioners insane.

"I have seen those who teach people to sit viewing the mind and viewing purity, to remain motionless and to not allow thoughts to arise. Exerting themselves in this manner, deluded individuals fail to become enlightened, and clinging to this [method] can even cause them to go insane." p. 95; trans. Philip J. Ivanhoe

A Straightforward Mind

"Universal stability is to act with a straightforward mind at all times: in motion or at rest, sitting or lying down" (p. 95). The Vimalakirti Sutra says: "A straightforward mind is the field of the Way." Meditation is not a special posture. It is how you carry yourself through every moment of life. If the mind does not abide in phenomena, the Way flows freely.

What Real Sitting Meditation Is

In Section 19, Huineng defines his terms. Externally, "not to allow thoughts to go out to any environment is 'to sit.'" Internally, "to see one's fundamental nature and maintain one's composure is 'to meditate.'" Externally, to separate from phenomena is meditation. Internally, to maintain one's composure is stability. If externally there is some phenomenon, internally one's nature remains composed (p. 98).

無念 The Three Cardinal Doctrines Section 17

Huineng names three principles that define his teaching. These are not separate doctrines but three angles on one insight.

無念 No-thought (wunian)
The cardinal doctrine. "To be among phenomena yet separate from them, to have thoughts yet not think them." Not the absence of thought, but non-attachment to thought.
無相 No-phenomena (wuxiang)
The Substance. "To be able to separate from all phenomena and the Substance of your nature will remain clean and pure." Separate from the phenomenal world while being in it.
無住 Non-abiding (wuzhu)
The foundation. "Successive thoughts should not abide." Past, present, and future thoughts should succeed one another without being cut off and without clinging to any one dharma.
"If even a single thought is cut off, the Dharma Body separates from the physical body. If a single thought abides, then successive thoughts will abide. This is called being fettered." p. 96; trans. Philip J. Ivanhoe
頓漸 Sudden vs. Gradual Section 16

No Difference in the Dharma

Huineng addresses the central debate of Chinese Buddhism directly: is enlightenment sudden or gradual?

"In the dharma there is no such thing as sudden or gradual. [However,] among people there are those with sharp and those with dull spiritual capacity. Deluded individuals pursue the gradual [method]. Enlightened individuals follow sudden cultivation." p. 96; trans. Philip J. Ivanhoe

The dharma itself is neither sudden nor gradual. Those are descriptions of people, not of truth. To realize one's fundamental mind is to see one's fundamental nature. "Those who are enlightened realize that from the very start there is not the slightest difference" (p. 96).

要義 KEY CONCEPTS
佛性 Buddha-Nature Throughout

The Platform Sutra's most fundamental claim: every sentient being already possesses perfect Buddha-nature. It has "always remained pure, shining without shadow, always completely knowing" (p. 104). Misguided thoughts have covered it, so people only recognize the qualities of the everyday world and become entangled in karmic consequences.

This is a radical democratization of enlightenment. The illiterate woodcutter and the learned head monk have identical Buddha-nature. Education, status, and years of study count for nothing if the fundamental insight is missing.

禪 Chan as Chinese Buddhism Connections

Daoist Echoes

Huineng's language borrows heavily from Daoism. "The Way must be allowed to flow freely" echoes the Daodejing. "A straightforward mind is the field of the Way" resonates with Daoist wu wei (non-action). Chan is what happens when Indian Buddhism meets Chinese spontaneity.

Against the Indian Tradition

Traditional Indian Buddhism emphasized meditation as a seated, controlled practice. The Platform Sutra redefines meditation as a quality of mind available in any posture and any activity. Huineng even mocks the idea that Vimalakirti should have scolded Shariputra for sitting in the forest (p. 95), referencing an Indian sutra to undermine the Indian meditation tradition.

Connections to Other Readings

  • Fazang: Both use Substance/Function (ti/yong). For Fazang, gold is Substance, lion is Function. For Huineng, stability is Substance, insight is Function.
  • Laozi: "The Way flows freely" and non-attachment echo Daodejing themes. But Huineng locates freedom in the mind, not in nature.
  • Zhuangzi: The "fundamental mind" (benxin) echoes Mengzi 6A10, which Chan Buddhists borrowed. Spontaneity links to Zhuangzi's skill stories.
  • Mengzi: "Fundamental mind" (benxin) was first used in Mengzi 6A10 for innate moral sensibilities. Huineng repurposes it for innate Buddha-nature.
問題 DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
The Poems
  • Shenxiu says the mind is "like a clear mirror" that must be constantly polished. Huineng says the mirror "has no stand." Who is right? Can you identify a modern analogy for each position?
  • Is Huineng's poem actually better, or is it only better given Chan assumptions? If you were a student of Shenxiu's, how would you defend his poem?
Practice and Paradox
  • Huineng says that sitting motionless and eliminating thoughts is a "grave mistake" that can drive people insane (p. 95). What does he propose instead? Is his alternative practical, or is it a paradox (how do you practice non-practice)?
  • "No-thought" is not the absence of thought but non-attachment to thought. How is this different from ordinary thinking? Can you actually do this?
Tradition and Authority
  • The Platform Sutra insists Huineng was illiterate. Why does the text make this claim so prominently? What philosophical work does Huineng's illiteracy do?
  • How does the Platform Sutra's concept of Buddha-nature compare to Mengzi's innate moral sprouts? Both say goodness/wisdom is innate. Are they making the same argument?
  • Huineng's teaching "put a distinctively Chinese stamp on the Buddhist tradition" (editorial intro, p. 91). What specifically is Chinese about it, as opposed to Indian Buddhist?
