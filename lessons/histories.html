Collection Overview

Three texts that shaped the Chinese historical tradition

Zuo Commentary

左傳
Zuo zhuan

Narrative history arranged as commentary on the Spring and Autumn Annals. The richest source of idioms in classical Chinese.

Covers 722-468 BCE

Conversations from the States

國語
Guo yu

Speeches and dialogues from eight states, organized geographically. Sometimes called the "outer commentary" to the Spring and Autumn Annals.

Covers ~1000-453 BCE

Intrigues of the Warring States

戰國策
Zhanguo ce

Rhetorical set-pieces showcasing persuasion, cunning, and political manipulation. Less "history" than a handbook of statecraft.

Covers ~490-221 BCE
Reading One
晉景公之死

The Death of Duke Jing of Jin

from the Zuo Commentary (左傳) - Translated by Burton Watson

Key Terms

Chinese Pinyin Meaning
晉景公 Jin Jing Gong Ruler of Jin, r. 599-581 BCE
巫 wu Shaman / diviner
膏肓 gao huang Region between heart and diaphragm (medically inaccessible)
病入膏肓 bing ru gao huang Idiom: "disease has entered the vitals" = beyond cure

Synopsis

The First Dream

Duke Jing has a prophetic nightmare: a vengeful ogre with disheveled hair - the spirit of someone the duke had killed - breaks through the palace gates, demanding retribution promised by Heaven.

The Prophecy

A sorcerer from Mulberry Field describes the dream without being told, then delivers his verdict: "You will not live to eat the new grain."

The Second Dream

The duke falls gravely ill. Before the physician Huan arrives from Qin, the duke dreams his illness appears as two little boys who plan to hide "above the diaphragm and below the heart" where medicine cannot reach.

The Ironic Death

When the new grain arrives, the duke summons the sorcerer, shows him the wheat as proof his prophecy failed, and has him executed. The duke then begins to eat - his stomach swells, he rushes to the privy, falls into the pit, and dies.

病入膏肓
bing ru gao huang
"The disease has entered the vitals"
This story is the origin of this famous Chinese idiom, meaning "beyond cure" or "a hopeless situation."

Major Themes

1

Fate vs. Human Agency

The prophecy is fulfilled precisely through attempts to defy it.

2

Ironic Justice

The duke dies exactly as predicted - before eating the new grain - but not in any way he could have anticipated.

3

Dreams as Omens

Three dreams structure the narrative: the vengeful ogre, the cunning illness-boys, and the servant's ascent to heaven.

Reading Two
申生之難

The Downfall of Prince Shensheng

from the Conversations from the States (國語) - Translated by Burton Watson

Key Terms

Chinese Pinyin Meaning
申生 Shen Sheng Crown prince of Jin, d. 655 BCE
驪姬 Li Ji Concubine from the Rong tribes; the schemer
孝 xiao Filial piety; the virtue that paralyzes Shensheng
優 you Court entertainer; moves between factions

Synopsis

Part 1: The Omen

Duke Xian divines whether to attack the Li Rong barbarians. The historian predicts: "Victory, but bad luck." The duke attacks anyway, wins, and brings back Lady Li as a captive-turned-consort.

Part 2: The Ministers' Debate

Lady Li bears a son. The duke considers replacing Crown Prince Shensheng. Three ministers respond: blind obedience, principled resistance, and strategic silence.

Part 3: The Poison Plot

Lady Li orders Shensheng to sacrifice to his dead mother and bring the offerings to the duke. While the duke is hunting, she poisons the food. When tested, the wine makes the ground boil; the meat kills a dog.

Part 4: The Suicide

Advisors urge Shensheng to flee. He refuses on grounds of filial piety: if he flees, blame falls on his father. "I cannot sacrifice my father to curry favor with others." Shensheng hangs himself in the ancestral temple.

Historical Aftermath

Li Ji's machinations - the Li Ji zhi luan (驪姬之亂) - destabilized Jin for decades. Eventually, Shensheng's brother Chong'er returned from nineteen years of exile to become the famous Duke Wen of Jin, one of the Five Hegemons.

Major Themes

1

Filial Piety as Tragic Flaw

Shensheng's virtue (孝 xiao) paralyzes him. He chooses death over exposing his father's manipulation.

2

The Femme Fatale Trope

Li Ji is the archetypal "wicked consort" who destroys a state through seduction and cunning.

3

Political Loyalty Dilemmas

The three ministers represent three models: blind obedience, principled resistance, and strategic silence.

Reading Three
三說

Three Persuasions

from the Intrigues of the Warring States (戰國策) - Translated by J.I. Crump

About the Zhanguoce

The Zhanguo ce differs fundamentally from the previous texts: it is less "history" than a collection of rhetorical set-pieces, showcasing the arts of persuasion practiced by itinerant advisors (shuike 說客) in the cutthroat world of Warring States diplomacy.

Persuasion I

Ying-hou Warns King Zhao of Qin

Fan Sui (speaking as Ying-hou) warns King Zhao that power has slipped from the throne to the Queen Mother, Lord Hua-yang, and Jang-hou. He uses three parables:

The Spirit of the Grove: A rash youth gambled with a sacred spirit and won its genie for three days. When the spirit tried to reclaim it, the youth refused. The grove rotted and died. Moral: Don't lend out your power.
The Heavy Fruit: "When the fruit is heavy, the bough is strained; when the bough is strained, the trunk is harmed." Moral: Overmighty subjects endanger the ruler.

Themes

  • Centralization of Power - Legalist argument against feudal fragmentation
  • The Persuader's Art - Indirect criticism through parable

Persuasion II

Sable Po and Tian Dan

Sable Po repeatedly slanders Tian Dan at court. Tian Tan invites him to dinner and asks why. Sable Po responds with a famous parable:

"When robber Chih's dog barked at the sage Yao, it wasn't judging character - a dog barks at whoever isn't its master."

Sable Po then saves Tian Tan from court intrigue, dramatically intervening when the king disrespects his faithful minister. The king executes all nine slanderous ministers.

Historical Background: The Fire Oxen of Jimo (279 BCE)

Tian Tan's famous defense of Jimo is one of the most celebrated military actions in Chinese history. He collected 1,000+ oxen, tied daggers to their horns and burning straw to their tails, and drove them into the enemy camp at night.

Persuasion III

The Queen of Zhao and Prince Chang-an

Qin attacks Zhao. Zhao seeks military aid from Qi, but Qi demands Prince Chang-an as a hostage (質子 zhizi) before sending troops. The Queen Dowager refuses and threatens:

"Whosoever again urges that Prince Chang-an be a hostage, I will spit in his face!"

The elderly commander Chu Che approaches her indirectly - commiserating about aging and aching feet. Then he asks about her daughter, the Queen of Yen. When she left for Yen, the Queen wept but prayed "let her not return!" Why? Because she hoped her sons would succeed as kings.

"Looking back three generations in Zhao, and in all the other states - have the sons and grandsons of kings continued to succeed to the throne? Your majesty has given Prince Chang-an rich lands and great wealth, but you have not ordered him to show mettle for his country. When you die, what will he have done to prove himself worthy?" - Chu Che to the Queen Dowager

The queen relents. Prince Chang-an goes to Qi as hostage. The troops of Qi march forth.

Themes

1

Indirect Persuasion

Chu Che never directly argues; he leads the queen to conclude it herself.

2

True vs. False Love

"Loving" a child by spoiling them vs. preparing them for challenges.

Comparative Analysis

Three texts, three approaches to power and virtue

Discussion Questions

  1. In the Duke Jing story, is the sorcerer's prophecy truly fulfilled? How does the narrative play with the idea of fate?

  2. Compare the three ministers' responses to the Li Ji crisis (obedience, resistance, silence). Which position do you find most defensible?

  3. Shensheng's choice to die rather than expose his father's manipulation is presented sympathetically. How should we evaluate "filial piety" when it enables injustice?

  4. What makes Chu Che's persuasion of the Queen of Zhao successful where direct arguments failed? Identify his specific rhetorical techniques.

  5. Lady Li and the Queen of Zhao are both powerful women in these texts. How do the narratives treat female political agency differently?
