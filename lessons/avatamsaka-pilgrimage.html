概 Overview
入法界品 What Is the Gandavyuha?

Book 39, "Entry into the Realm of Reality," is the longest single book in the Avatamsaka at over 400 pages. It tells the story of Sudhana (善財, Shancai), a wealthy youth sent south by the bodhisattva Manjushri to find 53 spiritual teachers. Each encounter is a self-contained episode with its own setting, visual language, and personality.

The text was composed as a standalone work (known as the Gandavyuha) before being incorporated into the larger Avatamsaka Sutra. Its narrative form, a chain of encounters with increasingly strange and powerful teachers, had no real precedent in Buddhist literature. The diversity of the teachers is the structural argument: monks, laywomen, night goddesses, earth spirits, celestial beings, a ship captain, a perfumer, children, and a future Buddha all possess authentic spiritual authority.

Companion page: The Flower Ornament Scripture: Cosmic Numbers, Infinite Teachers covers the number poetry (Book 30), interpenetration passages, Indra's Net, and Maitreya's Tower vision.
鏈 The Chain Pattern

Every encounter follows the same structure: Sudhana arrives, describes what he sees, and the teacher reveals their "liberation" (解脫, jieTuo). Then each teacher says the same thing: "I only know this one liberation. How can I know the practice of the great enlightening beings?" And sends him to the next teacher.

The refusal to claim total authority is the structural argument. No single teacher holds the whole truth. The text distributes wisdom across a network of 53 voices, and the journey between them is the education.

The Range of Teachers

  • Monks and nuns in deep concentration
  • Laywomen on golden thrones, practicing engaged compassion
  • Night goddesses who teach through darkness and light
  • Earth spirits who stamp their feet and reveal hidden treasures
  • Celestial beings who rain material blessings at crossroads
  • A ship captain, a perfumer, children, a future Buddha

名 Bodhisattva Names as Poetry
名即教 The Names Tell You Everything pp. 1139-1143

In the Avatamsaka, a bodhisattva's name IS their teaching compressed into a title. The names are not labels. They are poems. Each name tells you what the bodhisattva does, how they do it, and what their vow is. When ten great bodhisattvas arrive at the Jeta grove from ten directions at the opening of Book 39, each one's name is a complete philosophical statement.

The Pattern in the Names

Notice the pattern: every name contains an active element (light, fire, knowledge, vows, energy) combined with a cosmic scope (cosmos, all barriers, all bands of demons, illumination). These are not passive titles. They describe action at infinite scale.

The buddha-land names are equally poetic: "Diamond Ocean Womb," "Banner of Arrays of Clouds of Fragrance," "Filled with Jewel Sunlight." Each bodhisattva comes from a world whose name matches their character.

In Chinese translation, these names become even more compressed. The translation carries the structure: [quality] + [scope] + [function]. The name is a job description, a vow, and a poem simultaneously.

善知識 Teacher Names in the Gandavyuha

The same principle applies to Sudhana's 53 teachers. Their names encode their teachings:

Saradhvaja
"Arrow Banner": the name suggests directional precision, a meditation that hits its target
Prashantarutasagaravati
"She Whose Voice Is an Ocean of Tranquility": the longest name, the most detailed teaching. Total responsiveness to every type of person.
Sthavara
"The Steadfast": the earth goddess, stability itself
Vasanti
"Spring / The Flourishing One": the night goddess who works through darkness
Avalokiteshvara 觀世音
"Observer of the World's Sounds": compassion that listens before it acts
Maitreya 彌勒
"The Loving One": the future Buddha, from Sanskrit maitri meaning "loving-kindness"
Samantabhadra 普賢
"Universally Good / Worthy": universal goodness in practice, the 53rd teacher

The convention of encoding teaching in naming is not decorative. It is epistemological. To know the name is to know the teaching. To hear the name is to receive the first instruction.

五 The Five Great Bodhisattvas
Manjushri 文殊師利 (Wenshu) Wisdom
Name meaning: "Gentle Glory" or "Wonderful Virtue"

The bodhisattva of wisdom. Sends Sudhana on his journey. Appears "in the form of a youth" at the opening (p. 1169).

The Commission: Manjushri tells the monks that "A man or woman setting out on the Great Vehicle imbued with ten indefatigable determinations will enter the stage of realization of Thusness" (p. 1171). He then goes south. Shariputra, seeing Manjushri leave, sends sixty monks to follow him. They meet Sudhana.

At the end (p. 1502): Manjushri extends his hand "over a hundred and ten leagues" and lays it on Sudhana's head, saying "Good, good!" This gesture from a hundred leagues away is the Avatamsaka in miniature: distance is no obstacle to intimacy.

Samantabhadra 普賢 (Puxian) Practice & Vows
Name meaning: "Universally Good" or "Universal Worthy"

The bodhisattva of practice and vows. The 53rd and final teacher. Sudhana sees him "sat on a lotus seat of jewels facing the lion throne of the Buddha on the enlightenment site" (p. 1503).

His body emanates "clouds of light rays as numerous as atoms in all buddha-lands" from every pore (p. 1505).

"In each atom of all worlds the network of all buddhas shone."— Cleary, p. 1504

The Vow of Practice of Universal Good (pp. 1503+): Samantabhadra does not deliver a final revelation. He teaches that enlightened practice IS enlightenment. The journey was the destination all along.

Avalokiteshvara 觀世音 (Guanyin) Compassion
Name meaning: "Observer of the World's Sounds" (觀 observe + 世 world + 音 sounds)

The bodhisattva of compassion. In the Gandavyuha, stands on a mountainous cliff radiating protection in all directions.

Liberation: "Great Compassion Without Delay." Compassion that acts instantly, without hesitation.

In Chinese Buddhism, becomes Guanyin (觀音), the most widely worshipped figure in all of East Asian Buddhism.

The name encodes the method: first you LISTEN (觀音), then you act. Compassion begins with attention.

Maitreya 彌勒 (Mile) Future Buddha
Name meaning: "The Loving One" (from Sanskrit maitri, loving-kindness)

The future Buddha, the 52nd teacher, guardian of the Tower. Greets Sudhana with a 15-page speech of welcome, then opens the Tower by snapping his fingers.

After the Tower vision, sends Sudhana back to Manjushri:

"Now go back to Manjushri and ask him how an enlightening being is to learn and carry out the practice of enlightening beings."— Cleary, p. 1501

The circular structure: Maitreya sends Sudhana back to where he began (Manjushri), and Manjushri sends him to Samantabhadra. The quest loops.

善知識 A Gallery of the Strange and Holy

Each encounter follows the same pattern but no two look alike. From Saradhvaja's knees, "warrior sages, priestly sages, worldly sages, scholars versed in various crafts" emanate and go throughout the ten directions (p. 1198). From his navel, "various forms of wizards, as many as there are living beings." From his shoulders, "demigods and supernatural beings as numerous as atoms in countless buddha-fields."

The body of the meditating monk becomes a universe producing beings from every surface. This is the sutra's signature move: turning a quiet scene into a vision of infinite activity.

Saradhvaja Monk
Liberation: Concentration of Ultimate Nonobstruction

Sitting in deep concentration, beings emanate from every pore of his body, appearing to sentient beings in all worlds to teach according to their capacities. He does not move, yet he is everywhere. The classic image of meditative power as cosmic activity rather than withdrawal. (pp. 1197-1207)

Asha Laywoman
Liberation: "Sorrowless Well-Being"

A laywoman seated on a golden throne in a great hall, surrounded by attendants. Her liberation is not the monk's withdrawal but a householder's engaged compassion. She attained realization while living in the world. (pp. 1208-1214)

Avalokiteshvara Bodhisattva
Liberation: "Great Compassion Without Delay"

Standing on a mountainous cliff, radiating protection in all directions. The famous bodhisattva of compassion (觀音, Guanyin in Chinese) appears not as a gentle icon but as a cosmic force of active, urgent aid. The name of the liberation says it: compassion that acts instantly, without hesitation. (pp. 1276-1280)

Mahadeva Celestial Being
Liberation: "Cloud Net"

A celestial standing at a crossroads, manifesting heaps of gold and jewels for anyone who needs them. His "cloud net" liberation rains material blessings as a skillful means to draw beings toward enlightenment. Liberation can look like generosity. (pp. 1281-1282)

Sthavara Earth Goddess
Liberation: Indestructible Adamantine Basis

When Sthavara stamps her foot, the earth reveals billions of jewel deposits beneath its surface. She is the ground itself made conscious, the stability on which everything rests. Her teaching: the earth is not inert matter but a living spiritual force. (pp. 1283-1284)

Vasanti Night Goddess
Liberation: "Light of Truth That Dispels the Darkness of All Beings"

A night goddess with a golden complexion, seated in a flower calyx in a circle of the Buddha. Emanations pour from her pores in all directions, each teaching according to the mentality of each being. She embodies the paradox: spiritual light that works through darkness, not against it. (pp. 1284-1300)

Prashantarutasagaravati Night Goddess
Liberation: "Supernal Manifestations Producing Immense Joy"

The most detailed teacher portrait. She teaches differently for every type of person: those obsessed with consumer goods get forbearance; those filled with anger get led toward transcendent tolerance; the lazy get purified vigor; those on wrong paths get the light of wisdom. Her method is total responsiveness. (pp. 1329-1335)

Maitreya Future Buddha
Liberation: The Great Tower of Vairocana

The 52nd teacher and the climax of the journey. Maitreya greets Sudhana with the most extraordinary welcome speech in Buddhist literature: 15 pages of prophecy, metaphor, and praise. Then he opens his Tower. See the companion page for the Tower vision. (pp. 1456-1495)

What the Diversity of Teachers Teaches

  • Wisdom is not monastic property. Laywomen, earth goddesses, celestial beings, and night spirits all possess authentic liberations.
  • Each liberation is unique. No two teachers have the same method. The dharma is infinitely adaptable.
  • The feminine is not peripheral. Night goddesses, earth goddesses, and laywomen are among the most powerful teachers in the text.
  • The journey itself is the teaching. Sudhana does not arrive at truth; he walks through 53 versions of it.
終 The Ending: The Loop
文殊 Manjushri's Hand p. 1502

After the Tower, Maitreya sends Sudhana back to Manjushri. Sudhana travels to the city of Sumanamukha.

"Then Manjushri extended his hand over a hundred and ten leagues and laid it on the head of Sudhana, who was standing in the city of Sumanamukha, and said, 'Good, good!'"— Cleary, p. 1502

This gesture from a hundred leagues away is vintage Avatamsaka: physical distance is not an obstacle. The hand that reaches across space to touch Sudhana's head is the interpenetration teaching made intimate. Manjushri sends Sudhana to his final teacher.

普賢 Samantabhadra: The End That Loops pp. 1503-1505

Sudhana's 53rd teacher is Samantabhadra (Universally Good). He does not deliver a final revelation. Sudhana sees him "sat on a lotus seat of jewels facing the lion throne of the Buddha" (p. 1503), his body emanating light from every pore.

"Then Sudhana, having seen these ten foreshadowing lights... In each atom of all worlds the network of all buddhas shone. The auras of all buddhas emanated from each atom of all worlds, with hundreds of thousands of various colors, and pervaded the whole cosmos."— Cleary, p. 1504

Samantabhadra teaches the Vow of Practice of Universal Good. The message: enlightened practice IS enlightenment. The journey does not arrive at a destination separate from itself. The last teacher points at where you already are.

The ending loops back to the beginning. This is not a narrative flaw. It is the narrative form of interpenetration: the end is in the beginning, and the beginning is in every step.

善財 The Gandavyuha as Narrative Invention
形式 A Form Without Precedent

The Gandavyuha (Book 39) is not just philosophy. It is a narrative form that had no precedent in Buddhist or Indian literature and that echoed through East Asian storytelling for a millennium. Its structure: a young seeker is sent south, meets a teacher, receives one teaching, and is told "go find the next one." Fifty-three times. The wisdom is never in one place. It is distributed across a network of encounters, and the journey between them is the education.

What the Gandavyuha Invented

  • The chain-of-encounters form: each episode self-contained, each teacher pointing to the next. This episodic-but-cumulative structure anticipates the great Chinese pilgrimage narratives.
  • Journey to the West (16th c.): Xuanzang's pilgrimage for sutras, with encounters at every stage, draws on the same structural DNA. The pilgrims meet teachers, demons, and bodhisattvas in disguise. The journey IS the teaching.
  • Chan encounter dialogues (語錄, yulu): the master-student meeting as literary form. Each Chan encounter is a compressed Gandavyuha episode: arrive, ask, receive a teaching that shatters your expectations, move on.
  • "Spiritual friend" networks (善知識, shanzhishi): the Gandavyuha established the concept that wisdom travels through a network of relationships, not a single institutional hierarchy.
  • Pilgrimage circuits: Japan's 88-temple Shikoku pilgrimage, the 33-temple Kannon circuits, and the Korean temple pilgrimage routes all echo the Sudhana structure. Walk. Arrive. Receive. Walk again.
  • Borobudur as walkable narrative: Pilgrims at Borobudur follow Sudhana's path physically, circumambulating upward through the relief panels. The architecture converts the Gandavyuha's literary form into a bodily practice. You don't read the story. You walk it.
影響 Literary & Philosophical Influence
詩 Chinese Poetry and Chan Literature

Wang Wei (699–761)

The clearest case of Avatamsaka influence on a major Chinese poet. Wang Wei's courtesy name was Mojie (摩詰), after the bodhisattva Vimalakirti (Weimojie 維摩詰), a figure associated with the nonduality of form and emptiness. His landscape poems enact the Huayan principle that observer and observed interpenetrate. In "Deer Park" (鹿柴), an empty mountain where no one is visible, yet sunlight enters the deep forest and shines on green moss, the poem performs what Huayan philosophy describes: phenomena that are simultaneously empty and full, absent and luminous.

Su Shi (1037–1101)

Su Shi's "First Prose-Poem on the Red Cliff" draws on Huayan logic. The changing water and unchanging moon become an image of the interpenetration of the mutable and the permanent. Scholars describe the resulting vision as "an empty, still and rounded view of the universe and life," a phrase that could serve as a gloss on Huayan metaphysics. Su Shi never names Huayan directly in the Red Cliff, but the philosophical architecture is unmistakable.

D.T. Suzuki's Formula

D.T. Suzuki (1870–1966), the most influential figure in introducing Zen to the West, stated directly: "Huayan is the philosophy of Zen and Zen is the practice of Huayan." The formula captures how Huayan's theoretical architecture (interpenetration, mutual identity of phenomena) provided the metaphysical framework that Chan/Zen enacted in meditation and koan practice. The "one in all, all in one" formula that permeates Chan literature derives directly from the Avatamsaka.

Of the sutra itself, Suzuki wrote that "no religious literature in the world can ever approach the grandeur of conception, the depth of feeling, and the gigantic scale of composition, as attained by the sutra."

流 Downstream Traditions

Neo-Confucianism: Borrowed Architecture

The Cheng brothers (Cheng Hao 程顥, 1032–1085; Cheng Yi 程頤, 1033–1107) built their philosophies on the concept of li (理, principle), "an idea they brought to Neo-Confucianism from Buddhist and Daoist writings." Zhu Xi (朱熹, 1130–1200) went further, appropriating the Huayan li/shi framework to construct his system of li (pattern-principle) and qi (material force). John Makeham's The Buddhist Roots of Zhu Xi's Philosophical Thought (2018) documents this debt in detail. The Neo-Confucianists publicly criticized Buddhism while privately absorbing its conceptual architecture, one of the most productive cases of intellectual borrowing-while-denying in Chinese history.

Korea: Uisang's Ocean Seal

Uisang (義湘, 625–702) studied under the Huayan patriarch Zhiyan in China alongside Fazang, then returned to Korea to found the Hwaeom (華嚴) school. His masterwork is the Ocean Seal Diagram (海印圖): the entire doctrine of dependent co-arising compressed into a poem of 210 characters arranged in a geometric seal pattern. His teacher Zhiyan reportedly said: "This one Ocean Seal of yours is equal to my seventy-two seals."

Japan: Todai-ji and the Kegon School

The Kegon school (華嚴宗) was transmitted to Japan in 736 CE. Its head temple, Todai-ji in Nara, houses the Great Buddha (大仏, Daibutsu): a 15-meter bronze Vairocana Buddha, the cosmic Buddha at the center of the Avatamsaka. Commissioned by Emperor Shomu (r. 724–749), the statue is the most monumental physical manifestation of the Avatamsaka vision anywhere in the world. The sheer scale is itself a statement about Huayan cosmology.

Borobudur: The Sutra in Stone

Borobudur (9th century, Java) is the largest Buddhist temple in the world. Its upper galleries contain 1,460 narrative relief panels depicting the Gandavyuha, Sudhana's pilgrimage to fifty-three teachers carved in stone. Twenty of the fifty-two advisors depicted are female. The reliefs are considered among the finest works of art in the Buddhist world.

討論 Discussion Questions
一 1

The bodhisattva names at the opening of Book 39 are not random labels. Pick two names from the assembly (e.g., "Standard of Knowledge Scattering All Bands of Demons" and "Light from the Origin of the Vows of the Illumination") and analyze what they tell you about each bodhisattva's function. How does the naming convention compare to naming practices in other religious traditions?

二 2

Sudhana's 53 teachers include monks, laywomen, night goddesses, earth spirits, and celestial beings. Why does the sutra insist on this diversity? What would be lost if all the teachers were monks?

三 3

The night goddess Vasanti teaches "by the light of truth that dispels the darkness for all sentient beings." How does the paradox of a night goddess who works through light reshape your understanding of Buddhist compassion? What does the darkness represent?

四 4

Manjushri extends his hand "over a hundred and ten leagues" to touch Sudhana's head. How does this gesture embody the interpenetration principle? Compare it to the cosmic distances described in Book 30's number poetry.

五 5

Sudhana's quest ends with Samantabhadra, who does not deliver a final answer but teaches that practice IS enlightenment. The ending loops back to the beginning. Is this a satisfying ending? What does the circular structure say about the Buddhist understanding of spiritual progress?

六 6

Compare the Gandavyuha's chain-of-encounters narrative to another pilgrimage narrative you know (Journey to the West, The Odyssey, The Canterbury Tales, the Shikoku 88-temple circuit). What is unique about the Gandavyuha's structure?
