概論 WHY GENRE MATTERS
文體 Seven Genres in Eight Weeks

Since January, we have read texts spanning roughly 1,500 years of Chinese literary production. Each major genre we encountered carried its own rules, its own relationship to truth, and its own answer to the question: What is writing for?

This page maps the seven broad genre categories we have studied so far. Some are older than Kongzi. The newest, chuanqi, was invented by poets we've already met. Together they form a literary system where form is never neutral: choosing to write a fu instead of a shi is a statement about what kind of truth you are after.

演變 Genre as Evolution

Chinese literary genres did not arrive all at once. They accumulated over centuries, each new form emerging because the existing ones could not say what someone needed to say.

c. 1000–600 BCE
Shi 詩 emerges as ritual song, folk lyric, political complaint. The oldest literary form in our course.
c. 500–200 BCE
Zi 子 (Masters lit.) and Chu Ci 楚辭 develop simultaneously. Philosophy and ecstatic poetry, both responses to political crisis.
c. 200 BCE–200 CE
Fu 賦 (Rhapsody) becomes the prestige genre of the Han court. Spectacle and verbal excess as imperial display.
c. 200–580 CE
Wen 文 (personal prose) flourishes as the empire fragments. Writers turn inward. Lu Ji theorizes the whole system.
c. 400–900 CE
Jing 經 (Buddhist scripture) enters Chinese literary culture. Radically different assumptions about language and truth.
c. 750–900 CE
Chuanqi 傳奇 (fiction) emerges. For the first time, writers consciously invent stories as literary art.
詩 POETRY (SHI)
詩 Lyric Poetry Zhou through Tang
詩
Shi: The Oldest Genre
shi (shih in Birch)

Poetry is where Chinese literature begins. Lu Ji defined it as "born of pure emotion, gossamer fibre woven into the finest fabric" (Birch, p. 208). From the ritual songs of the Book of Songs through Tang regulated verse, shi remained the core literary form for over 1,500 years.

What makes it shi?

  • Personal voice: the poet speaks from felt experience
  • Compression: meaning packed into tight lines, often four or five characters per line
  • Formal structure: evolves from folk song (irregular) to regulated verse (strict tonal patterns by the Tang)
  • The "Great Preface": "Poetry is where the heart-mind goes. In the heart it is intention; expressed in words it is poetry"

What we've read

  • Warring States Songs of Chu (Qu Yuan's Li Sao, Nine Songs)
  • Six Dyn. Recluse Poetry (Tao Yuanming, Ruan Ji)
  • Six Dyn. Bao Zhao
  • Tang Wang Wei, Li Bai, Du Fu
  • Tang Bai Juyi, Yuan Zhen, Li He
楚辭 Songs of Chu A Special Case
辭
Chu Ci: The Ecstatic Tradition
chu ci (ch'u tz'u in Birch)

The Songs of Chu stand apart from the northern shi tradition. They are longer, wilder, more rhythmically irregular, saturated with southern shamanic imagery. Where shi tends toward restraint, chu ci tends toward excess.

How it differs from shi

  • Longer lines: typically six or seven characters, with the exclamatory particle xi (兮) as a rhythmic marker
  • Shamanic roots: derived from southern ritual incantations, not northern court music
  • Cosmic scope: the poet journeys across heaven and earth, summons spirits, confronts gods
  • Political allegory: Qu Yuan's exile becomes the template for all unjustly dismissed ministers
"Long did I sigh and wipe away my tears,
To think how man's life is beset with sorrow." Qu Yuan, Li Sao; Birch, p. 54; trans. David Hawkes

Why it matters for the genre map

The chu ci tradition fed directly into the fu. The verbal extravagance, the long descriptive catalogues, the cosmic scale of ambition: all of these reappear in Han rhyme-prose. Qu Yuan's personal voice also anticipates the recluse poets a millennium later.

賦 RHAPSODY (FU)
賦 Rhyme-Prose / Rhapsody Warring States through Six Dynasties
賦
Fu: The World Made Vivid
fu

Lu Ji called the fu "being true to the objects, vividness incarnate" (Birch, p. 208). The fu is the display genre: long, elaborate, rhythmic prose-poetry that catalogues the world in overwhelming detail. It was the prestige literary form of the Han dynasty court.

What makes it fu?

  • Descriptive excess: the point is to name everything, to make the reader see the scene in total
  • Mixed form: alternates between prose and verse, rhymed and unrhymed passages
  • Spectacle: imperial hunts, grand parks, capital cities, cosmic journeys
  • Political subtext: the display of imperial wealth often conceals a moral warning about excess

What we've read

  • Warring States Song Yu's "Fu on the Wind"
  • Han Jia Yi's "Lament for Qu Yuan" and "Fu on the Owl"
  • Han Sima Xiangru's Shanglin Fu
  • Six Dyn. Lu Ji's Wen Fu (a fu about writing itself)
"Writing is in itself a joy,
Yet saints and sages have long since held it in awe.
For it is Being, created by tasking the Great Void,
And 'tis sound rung out of Profound Silence." Lu Ji, Wen Fu; Birch, p. 207; trans. Shih-hsiang Chen
變化 How the Fu Evolved

The arc of the fu

The fu has a fascinating trajectory in our course. It begins as a vehicle for spectacle (Sima Xiangru's vast imperial park), then transforms into a vehicle for ideas (Lu Ji's meditation on literary creation). The form stays recognizable, but the content shifts from external display to internal reflection.

Fu evolution in three texts

  • Song Yu: nature as political allegory (the wind serves the king but chills the commoner)
  • Sima Xiangru: the imperial park as total catalogue of the world's abundance
  • Lu Ji: the creative process itself as a worthy subject for literary display

Fu vs. shi: the core distinction

If shi is about feeling, the fu is about seeing. Shi compresses; fu expands. Shi says "I felt this"; fu says "look at all of this." This is Lu Ji's point: "The lyric (shi) is born of pure emotion... The exhibitory essay (fu), being true to the objects, is vividness incarnate" (Birch, p. 208). They are complementary tools for different kinds of truth.

子 MASTERS LITERATURE (ZI)
子 Philosophical Prose Warring States
子
Zi: Argument as Literature
zi

The great philosophical texts of the Warring States period are not just philosophy. They are literature: carefully crafted, stylistically distinct, and brilliantly varied in their use of narrative, dialogue, parable, and polemic. Each Master has a recognizable literary voice.

Seven voices, seven styles

孔 Kongzi
Compressed dialogue. The Master speaks in fragments that require interpretation. "The gentleman is not a utensil."
墨 Mozi
Logical argument, almost legalistic. Repetitive structure builds an airtight case. The anti-literary philosopher.
孟 Mengzi
Combative dialogue. Mengzi argues with kings, traps opponents in their own logic, uses vivid analogies (the child at the well).
老 Laozi
Paradox and aphorism. "The way that can be told is not the constant way." Language that undermines itself.
莊 Zhuangzi
Wild narrative. Parables, fables, absurdist dialogues, dreams within dreams. The most "literary" philosopher.
荀 Xunzi
Systematic essay. Extended arguments with clear structure, definitions, evidence. The most rigorous prose stylist.
韓 Han Feizi
Historical anecdote as weapon. Uses past examples to prove present points. Stories serve power, not beauty.
文學 Why the Masters Are Literature

The literary question

Students sometimes wonder: why are we reading philosophy in a literature course? The answer is that in Chinese tradition, these texts were never only philosophy. The Zhuangzi is one of the greatest works of narrative imagination in any language. The Analects invented a literary form (the compressed dialogue) that influenced all subsequent Chinese prose. The Mengzi is a masterclass in rhetorical combat.

Zhuangzi as proto-fiction

Zhuangzi matters especially for our genre map. His parables, his talking animals, his dream paradoxes, his fictional dialogues between real historical figures: these are among the earliest examples of deliberate narrative invention in Chinese literature. When Tang writers invented chuanqi a millennium later, they were doing something Zhuangzi had already pioneered in a different register.

"Once Zhuang Zhou dreamt he was a butterfly, a butterfly flitting and fluttering around, happy with himself and doing as he pleased. He didn't know he was Zhuang Zhou. Suddenly he woke up and there he was, solid and unmistakable Zhuang Zhou. But he didn't know if he was Zhuang Zhou who had dreamt he was a butterfly, or a butterfly dreaming he was Zhuang Zhou." Birch, p. 100; trans. Burton Watson
文 PROSE (WEN)
文 Prose Essays and Letters Six Dynasties through Tang
文
Wen: The Self on Paper
wen

Wen is the broadest term: it can mean "literature," "culture," even "civilization." But as a genre label, it refers to prose writing that is neither poetry nor philosophy nor history. Personal letters, literary essays, polemics, travel writing. The author speaks directly, in their own voice, about their own situation.

Two waves

  • Six Dynasties prose Six Dyn.: Letters defending the decision to withdraw from public life. Yang Yun, Xi Kang, Tao Yuanming's "Peach Blossom Spring," Kong Zhigui. The genre of reclusion.
    On Seclusion
  • Tang prose Tang: Han Yu and Liu Zongyuan revive "ancient-style prose" (guwen, 古文) against the ornate "parallel prose" of the Six Dynasties. Prose as moral argument, political intervention, philosophical statement.
    Tang Prose Essays
"My boat tipped and tossed in the wind and waves, and a feeling of vast loneliness overwhelmed me, as if I were about to make a voyage from which there is no return." Tao Yuanming, "The Peach Blossom Spring"; Birch, p. 167; trans. James Robert Hightower
經 Buddhist Scripture A Different Kind of Text
經
Jing: Words Beyond the Human
jing

Buddhist scripture enters Chinese literary culture as translation: Sanskrit and Pali texts rendered into Chinese by teams of translators over centuries. These texts operate under entirely different assumptions about what language can do.

What's different about scripture?

  • Scale: the Avatamsaka Sutra dwarfs anything in the Chinese literary tradition. Numbers like "ten to the power of ten to the power of ten" are routine.
  • Authorship: not attributed to a human author. These are understood as records of the Buddha's speech.
  • Purpose: not aesthetic pleasure or moral instruction but transformation of consciousness.
  • Logic: paradox, infinite recursion, simultaneous perspectives. "One is all; all is one."

What we've read

  • Six Dyn. Huiyuan's essays on why monks don't bow to the emperor
  • Tang Huayan Buddhism and the Avatamsaka Sutra
傳奇 FICTION (CHUANQI)
傳奇 Tales of the Marvelous Tang Dynasty, c. 750–900
傳奇
Chuanqi: Conscious Invention
chuanqi (ch'uan-ch'i in Birch)

Lu Xun considered Tang writers "the first consciously to practise the art of fiction" (Birch, p. 288). Before the Tang, narratives existed in histories, anecdotes, and wonder-tales. Nobody set out to write fiction as a literary craft. The chuanqi changed that.

What makes chuanqi new?

  • Deliberate invention: the author knowingly creates characters and events. Previous narrative was historical or supernatural. Chuanqi is made up and acknowledged as such.
  • Psychological depth: characters have complex motivations. Yingying is not a type; she is a person.
  • Scholar-authors: these writers are poets and officials, not professional storytellers. Yuan Zhen was Bai Juyi's closest friend. Bo Xingqian was Bai Juyi's brother.
  • Classical prose style: "reminiscent of biographical entries in the standard histories" (Birch, p. 288). Clean, economical, powerful.

The three stories

  • Yuan Zhen: "The Story of Ts'ui Ying-ying" (pp. 290–299). Romance and betrayal.
  • Bo Xingqian: "The Story of Miss Li" (pp. 300–313). Ruin and redemption.
  • Du Guangting: "The Curly-bearded Hero" (pp. 314–322). Destiny and yielding.
新 Why Fiction Was the Last Genre

Everything had to come first

Fiction requires all the other genres as foundation. It needs:

  • From shi: the ability to render inner emotional life. Yingying's poems are the heart of her story.
  • From fu: descriptive power. The scenes in "Miss Li" are built with a fu writer's eye for vivid detail.
  • From zi: narrative invention. Zhuangzi's parables proved that Chinese prose could sustain invented stories.
  • From wen: the personal voice. Chuanqi narrators comment, judge, and embed themselves in their tales.
  • From shi (history): the biographical prose style. Chuanqi mimics the form of dynastic biographies.

The key innovation

  • Before chuanqi: "this really happened" (history) or "this might have happened" (anecdote) or "the gods did this" (wonder-tale)
  • Chuanqi: "I made this up, and it reveals something true about human nature"

From Zhuangzi to Yuan Zhen

The distance from Zhuangzi's butterfly dream to Yuan Zhen's Yingying is about 1,000 years. Both are acts of literary imagination. But Zhuangzi embeds his inventions in philosophy; Yuan Zhen writes a standalone story whose purpose is the story itself. That leap from embedded narrative to autonomous fiction is what the chuanqi represents.

全貌 THE BIG PICTURE
系統 Lu Ji's Framework: Form Follows Purpose

Lu Ji's Wen Fu remains the best single statement of how Chinese genres relate to each other. Writing in the 290s CE, he classified ten literary forms (Birch, pp. 207–209). His core principle: genre determines style. You don't choose a form at random. Each form has its own relationship to truth, emotion, and ornament.

詩 Lyric
"born of pure emotion, is gossamer fibre woven into the finest fabric"
賦 Exhibitory Essay
"being true to the objects, is vividness incarnate"
論 Expository
"must in exactitude and clarity excel"
說 Argument
"with glowing words and cunning parables persuades"

Lu Ji wrote three centuries before chuanqi existed. He had no category for fiction. That absence is itself revealing: in the third century, deliberate prose fiction was simply not a recognized literary form. When Tang writers invented it 500 years later, they were adding something genuinely new to the system Lu Ji mapped.

問 Three Questions That Cut Across All Genres

1. What is the relationship between form and truth?

A shi poem and a fu and a chuanqi all claim to convey truth. But they mean different things by "truth." Poetry offers emotional truth. The fu offers perceptual truth (the world as it looks). The zi offer rational truth. The chuanqi offers truth through invented scenarios that never happened but illuminate how people actually behave.

2. Who speaks?

In shi, the poet speaks directly. In the fu, a persona describes a scene. In the zi, a Master teaches. In wen, a writer addresses a specific audience. In the chuanqi, a narrator tells someone else's story. The evolution of Chinese literature is partly an evolution of who gets to speak and how.

3. What is writing for?

The Confucian answer: moral instruction and political order. The Daoist answer: liberation from fixed categories. The Buddhist answer: transformation of consciousness. The chuanqi answer: to reveal the human heart through stories that never happened. Each genre carries its own answer, and the answer shapes the form.

問題 DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
Genre Boundaries
  • Lu Ji's Wen Fu is a fu about writing. The chuanqi "Story of Yingying" contains embedded shi poems. The Zhuangzi contains parables that read like short stories. Are genre boundaries real, or are the best works the ones that cross them?
  • Pick two genres we have studied. What can one say that the other cannot? Give a specific example from the readings.
The Fiction Question
  • Why did it take until the Tang dynasty (roughly the 8th century) for Chinese writers to produce deliberate fiction? What had to exist first? What changed?
  • Compare the chuanqi "Story of Yingying" to any passage in the Zhuangzi that tells an invented story. Both are fictional. What's different about how they use fiction?
Your Own Reading
  • Which genre have you found most compelling this term? Which has been most difficult to appreciate? What does that tell you about your own assumptions about literature?
  • If you had to explain one Chinese literary genre to someone who has never encountered Chinese literature, which would you choose and why?
