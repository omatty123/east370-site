作者 THE AUTHORS
宋玉 Song Yu fl. 298–263 BCE · Chu

Life

A Warring States poet from the city of Yan 鄢 (modern Yicheng, Hubei), Song Yu served as a minister at the court of King Xiang of Chu. Little reliable biographical information survives; most details come from anecdotal sources compiled centuries later.

Relationship to Qu Yuan

Traditionally regarded as Qu Yuan's disciple and literary successor, Song Yu's name is paired with Qu Yuan's as "Qu-Song" (屈宋). Sima Qian listed Song Yu among the great poets of Chu who lived after Qu Yuan, though the exact nature of their relationship remains uncertain.

Major Works

  • Jiu Bian 九辯 (Nine Changes)
  • Feng Fu 風賦 (Fu on the Wind)
  • Gaotang Fu 高唐賦 (Rhapsody on Gaotang Terrace)
  • Zhao Hun 招魂 (Summons of the Soul) — attribution disputed

Legacy

Considered the most outstanding Chu Ci writer after Qu Yuan. His Jiu Bian established "be grieved by autumn" (悲秋) as a lasting motif in Chinese literature. Many Chinese idioms still in use trace back to his poems.

賈誼 Jia Yi c. 200–168 BCE · Western Han

Life

Born in Luoyang, Jia Yi displayed exceptional literary talent from youth. In 179 BCE, Emperor Wen appointed him boshi (博士, "professor"), then rapidly promoted him to Grand Master of the Palace. His reform proposals threatened older officials, and in 176 BCE he was exiled to Changsha as Grand Tutor to its young king.

Later Career

Recalled to the capital c. 172 BCE as consultant on Daoist mysticism, he was then appointed Grand Tutor to Prince Liu Yi. When the prince died in a horse-riding accident (169 BCE), Jia Yi blamed himself and died grief-stricken approximately one year later, at about age 33.

Major Works

  • Xinshu 新書 (New Writings) — political treatise
  • Guo Qin Lun 過秦論 (Disquisition Finding Fault with Qin)
  • Diao Qu Yuan Fu 弔屈原賦 (Lament for Qu Yuan)
  • Funiao Fu 鵩鳥賦 (Fu on the Owl)

Legacy

One of the earliest known writers of fu rhapsody. His synthesis of Legalist, Daoist, and Confucian doctrines "significantly shaped the imperial institutions of the Han dynasty, which became the models for successive dynasties of imperial China" (Routledge Encyclopedia of Philosophy). His "people as root" (民本) concept influenced Chinese political thought for millennia.

作品 THE WORKS
風賦 Fu on the Wind Song Yu · 4th–3rd c. BCE

Historical Context

Framed as a dialogue between Song Yu and King Xiang of Chu at Magnolia Terrace (Langtai), the poem exemplifies the courtly setting in which early fu were composed and performed. While likely a literary convention rather than historical record, the dialogue format became characteristic of Song Yu's works in the Wen Xuan anthology.

Summary

The king, enjoying a pleasant breeze, asks Song Yu whether common people share this wind. Song Yu's response forms the heart of the work: the same wind produces entirely different experiences depending on one's social circumstances.

風賦 Fu on the Wind — Excerpts & Analysis

The King's Wind

"Born from the earth, rising through beautiful landscapes, it rambles in the inner courtyard, climbs gauzy curtains, passes into the inner chamber... It is so clean and pure, fresh and cool, it cures illness, dispels hangovers, soothes the body, comforts men." — Translation adapted from David R. Knechtges, Wen Xuan

The Commoner's Wind

"Gustily rises from a remote lane, scooping out grime, raising dust. Sullen and sad, fretting and fuming, it dashes through holes, invades doors, stirring up sand piles, blowing dead embers, throwing up filth and muck." — Translation adapted from David R. Knechtges, Wen Xuan

Key Themes

  • Social commentary through allegory: The contrast between "king's wind" (雄風) and "commoner's wind" explores class disparity
  • Environmental determinism: The same natural element produces vastly different lived experiences
  • Critique of privilege: The king naively assumes equality; Song Yu demonstrates structural inequality
  • Nature as vehicle for political observation: Pioneered using natural phenomena to comment on social conditions

Significance

The Fu on the Wind demonstrates how early fu used elaborate description and dialogue to explore social and philosophical themes. Its technique of using nature to critique political conditions became influential in Chinese literary tradition.

弔屈原賦 Lament for Qu Yuan Jia Yi · 176 BCE

Historical Context

When Jia Yi was exiled to Changsha in 176 BCE, he traveled south and crossed the Xiang River — the same region where Qu Yuan had drowned himself approximately a century earlier (c. 278 BCE). The geographical and circumstantial parallels moved Jia Yi to compose this lament, identifying his own situation with that of the great Chu poet-statesman.

Summary

The rhapsody mourns Qu Yuan's fate as a loyal minister who was slandered by rivals, exiled from his homeland, and driven to suicide. Jia Yi draws explicit parallels between Qu Yuan's situation and his own exile from Emperor Wen's court, reflecting on the injustice of wise counselors being rejected while sycophants prosper.

弔屈原賦 Lament — Themes & Significance

Key Themes

  • Identification with Qu Yuan: Jia Yi explicitly compares his fate to the unhappy Chu statesman
  • Political allegory: Explores the fate of loyal ministers who are slandered at court
  • Sacrificial offering: Written as a tribute to Qu Yuan, cast into the river during Jia Yi's journey
  • Virtue unrecognized: Contemplates talented individuals rejected by corrupt courts

Significance

The Lament inspired a minor literary tradition of exiled poets composing verses and casting them into rivers during their journeys. It established a model for later literati to identify with Qu Yuan when facing political exile or persecution. The work is preserved in Sima Qian's Records of the Grand Historian (Volume 84).

鵩鳥賦 Fu on the Owl Jia Yi · c. 170 BCE

Historical Context

During his three-year exile in the Changsha Kingdom, an owl flew into Jia Yi's room at sunset. In Han popular belief, owls were omens of death. The depressed Jia Yi interpreted this as a sign of his impending death and composed this rhapsody in response.

Summary

The rhapsody employs a question-and-answer format: frightened by the owl's appearance, the speaker receives philosophical consolation from the bird. Life is subject to constant change; fortune-telling has no real influence; human positions are never secure. The wise person must adapt to circumstances and remain flexible.

鵩鳥賦 Fu on the Owl — Themes & Significance

Key Themes

  • Daoist self-consolation: Described as "a beautiful example of Daoist self-consolation in a world of uncertainty and change"
  • Philosophy of change: Uses the owl to convey Daoist wisdom about the equality of life and death
  • Huang-Lao influence: Reflects the Daoist school of Huang-Lao thought — all things are created by dao (道) and de (德)
  • Spiritual freedom: The sage exemplifies the Daoist ideal of detachment from worldly concerns

Significance

The Fu on the Owl is the earliest preserved and definitely datable fu in Chinese literature. It established the fu as a vehicle for philosophical reflection and personal expression, becoming a key model for transposing Daoist ideas to poetry. Tragically, despite composing these therapeutic words, Jia Yi died just a few years later.
