01 Ru Kongzi · Analects 18.5–18.6
"I cannot herd with birds and beasts. If I do not associate with the people of this world, with whom shall I associate?" Kongzi is responding to:
A
Mozi's argument that concern for all people must replace family loyalty
B
Recluses who argue that withdrawal from a corrupt world is the only honest choice
C
Laozi's claim that the Dao that can be spoken is not the true Dao
D
Mengzi's four sprouts theory of innate moral goodness
Why

In Analects 18.5–18.6, the Madman of Chu and the hermit-farmers Changju and Jieni challenge Kongzi: why engage with a world this broken? His answer defines the Confucian position for the next two millennia. The world is broken, and that is precisely why you must stay in it. Every recluse poet from Zhang Heng to Tao Yuanming is answering this same challenge.

02 Mo Mozi · Mohism
What makes Mozi arguably China's first true philosopher, rather than Kongzi?
A
He founded a school with more students than Kongzi's
B
He rejected all earlier tradition and started from scratch
C
He was the first to construct explicit arguments rather than rely on received wisdom and personal cultivation
D
He wrote in classical Chinese rather than speaking in dialogue form
Why

Kongzi teaches through example, dialogue, and personal cultivation. He rarely argues. Mozi systematically constructs arguments with premises, evidence, and conclusions. His "three tests" (san biao) for evaluating claims represent the first explicit methodology for philosophical reasoning in the Chinese tradition. He doesn't say "the ancients did it this way." He says "here's why this is right, and here's how you can verify it."

03 Ru Mengzi · Four Sprouts
A child falls into a well. You feel alarm and distress. According to Mengzi, this reaction proves:
A
That humans are fully virtuous at birth and need no further cultivation
B
That humans have innate moral tendencies ("sprouts") which require cultivation to develop into full virtue
C
That compassion is a learned social behavior reinforced by Confucian ritual
D
That fear of social judgment drives all seemingly moral behavior
Why

The child-in-the-well is Mengzi's most famous argument. You don't feel alarm because you want the parents' gratitude, or village praise, or to avoid a bad reputation. The feeling is spontaneous, prior to calculation. But it is a sprout (duan 端), not a finished plant. Without cultivation, the sprout dies. This is RU 2.0: human nature is good, but goodness still requires effort.

04 Dao Laozi · Daodejing
Laozi and Kongzi diagnose the same crisis (the collapse of Zhou order). Their prescriptions are opposite. How?
A
Kongzi says restore ritual and culture; Laozi says ritual and culture are themselves the disease
B
Kongzi advocates military strength; Laozi advocates diplomacy
C
Both agree the solution is less government intervention, but differ on how to achieve it
D
Kongzi rejects the Zhou model entirely; Laozi wants to restore it
Why

"When the great Dao declined, benevolence and righteousness appeared" (Daodejing 18). For Laozi, Kongzi's virtues (ren, yi, li) are symptoms of the illness, not the cure. The very fact that you need to name benevolence proves it is no longer natural. A truly good society wouldn't need the word "good." Kongzi sees culture as the solution; Laozi sees it as the problem.

05 Dao Zhuangzi · Cook Ding
Cook Ding's knife has stayed sharp for nineteen years of butchering oxen. Why?
A
He sharpens it daily, demonstrating Xunzi's principle of accumulated effort
B
He uses it only on ceremonial occasions, preserving the blade through infrequent use
C
He follows the natural structure of the ox, guiding the blade through gaps rather than cutting through bone
D
He replaced the original blade with one forged from superior Chu metal
Why

"A good cook changes his knife once a year, because he cuts. An ordinary cook changes his every month, because he hacks." Cook Ding does neither. He perceives the natural spaces in the ox and follows them. The blade passes through gaps where there is no resistance. This is Zhuangzi's image of wuwei in action: mastery that looks effortless because it works with the grain of things, not against it. Lord Wen-hui gets the message: "I have learned how to nurture life."

06 Ru Xunzi · Human Nature
Xunzi uses the metaphor "blue dye comes from the indigo plant, yet it is bluer than indigo." What is he arguing?
A
That human nature is good and naturally develops beyond its origins
B
That only people born with natural talent can become virtuous
C
That law is superior to ritual for transforming behavior
D
That learning and deliberate effort can transform raw human nature into something better than the starting material
Why

This is RU 3.0. Where Mengzi says virtue is innate (just cultivate the sprouts), Xunzi says human nature tends toward disorder. But unlike Han Feizi, who will abandon ritual for law, Xunzi insists that deliberate effort (wei 偽) through learning and ritual can produce something genuinely better than raw nature. The blue dye surpasses the plant it came from. The student surpasses the teacher. Goodness is constructed, not discovered.

07 Fa Han Feizi · Legalism
Han Feizi studied under Xunzi but reached a radically different conclusion. What was the pivot?
A
He rejected the idea that human nature was problematic and returned to Mengzi's optimism
B
He agreed human nature is problematic but replaced ritual (li) with law (fa) as the instrument of order
C
He combined Confucian virtue ethics with Daoist wuwei into a new synthesis
D
He abandoned philosophy entirely in favor of military strategy
Why

Xunzi and Han Feizi share the diagnosis: human nature left unchecked leads to disorder. The treatment is where they split. Xunzi prescribes ritual, learning, and accumulated moral effort. Han Feizi says that's too slow, too dependent on rare sages, and too easy to fake. His solution: clear, public laws enforced without exception, plus the "two handles" of punishment and reward. The ruler doesn't need to be virtuous. He needs to be systematic.

08 Chu Qu Yuan · Li Sao
In the "Li Sao," Qu Yuan uses elaborate imagery of fragrant plants (orchids, melilotus) and foul weeds (mugwort). What do they represent?
A
Virtuous people and corrupt courtiers, respectively. The moral and the rotten.
B
The state of Chu and the state of Qin, as rival civilizations
C
Daoist naturalism versus Confucian artifice
D
The poet's youth and old age, marking the passage of time
Why

Fragrant plants (xiangcao 香草) represent moral worth throughout the Chuci tradition. Qu Yuan adorns himself with orchids, angelica, and melilotus to signify his virtue. When the plants wither or change nature, it means the good people have been corrupted. The foul weeds are the slanderers who turned the king against him. This botanical allegory became foundational for Chinese political poetry: if you describe plants, you might be describing the court.

09 Fu Sima Xiangru · Shanglin Fu
The Shanglin fu spends hundreds of lines cataloguing the emperor's hunting park in dazzling detail, then abruptly turns to moral critique. What is this rhetorical strategy called, and why is it a paradox?
A
Straightforward satire: the excess is presented negatively from the start
B
Historical documentation: Sima Xiangru is recording the park accurately before critiquing it
C
The fu's power to dazzle undermines its own moral message. The description is so seductive that the reader enjoys the very excess being condemned.
D
There is no paradox: the moral conclusion was added later by Confucian editors, not by Sima Xiangru
Why

This is the central problem of the Han fu genre. Emperor Wu reportedly said: "If I could get a writer like this to serve me, I would not regret dying." He wasn't reacting to the moral lesson at the end. He was seduced by the magnificence of the description. The fu critiques imperial excess by first performing it so brilliantly that the critique becomes secondary. Ban Gu later complained that the genre "persuades a hundred while dissuading one." The dazzle wins.

10 Recluse Ruan Ji · Strategic Illegibility
Ruan Ji's 82 "Poems of My Heart" express overwhelming anguish but never name what he is anguished about. How did this save his life?
A
The poems were written in a private code that only his friends could decipher
B
He published them anonymously, so the Sima government never traced them to him
C
The poems praised the Sima clan while hiding criticism in obscure allusions only scholars could detect
D
The poems were so deliberately opaque that no one could prove they contained political criticism, even though everyone could feel the distress
Why

His closest friend Ji Kang chose principled clarity and was executed. Ruan Ji chose opacity. He stayed drunk for sixty days to avoid a marriage alliance with the Sima clan. He greeted people he respected with the dark of his pupils and those he despised with only the whites. His poetry communicates genuine suffering while making it impossible to prove what, specifically, the suffering is about. This is not coded protest. It is something more radical: the expression of a condition that cannot safely be named.
