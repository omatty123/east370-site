Sima Xiangru

c. 179‚Äì117 BCE ¬∑ Chengdu, Shu (Sichuan)

Life

Sima Xiangru is generally regarded as the greatest of all composers of Chinese fu rhapsodies. Born in Chengdu into a wealthy family, he received training in literature, history, and philosophy. As a youth, he took the name "Xiangru" out of admiration for Lin Xiangru, the Warring States diplomat famous for his wit and courage.

Early Career

Around 150 BCE, Sima traveled to the capital Chang'an and became a mounted military attendant to Emperor Jing ‚Äî a low-ranking position that suited neither his talents nor his literary temperament. In 149 BCE, he moved to the court of Liu Wu, Prince of Liang, joining a circle of brilliant literati. There he composed the Zixu fu (Rhapsody of Sir Fantasy), which would later catch an emperor's attention.

The Zhuo Wenjun Romance

After Prince Liu Wu's death in 143 BCE, Sima returned to Shu in poverty. His fortunes changed when the magistrate Wang Ji introduced him to Zhuo Wangsun, a wealthy iron merchant. At a banquet, Sima performed "Phoenix Seeking Phoenix" (È≥≥Ê±ÇÂá∞) on the qin ‚Äî ostensibly for the host, but truly for Zhuo's recently widowed daughter Wenjun, who listened hidden behind a screen. The two eloped that night.

Disinherited and destitute, they opened a small wine shop where Wenjun tended bar while Sima washed pots. Eventually, public shame forced her father to recognize the marriage with a massive dowry ‚Äî one of Chinese literature's most celebrated love stories.

At Emperor Wu's Court

In 137 BCE, a copy of the Zixu fu reached the sixteen-year-old Emperor Wu, who exclaimed that he regretted the author lived in a different era. Informed that Sima was very much alive, Wu summoned him to court. Thus began a relationship that would produce the Shanglin fu and secure Sima's place in literary history.

Legacy

Sima's long, imaginative compositions wove together fantastic images in an outpouring of alliterative and rhyming descriptive expressions. His language is extraordinarily rich ‚Äî "majestic and beautiful" ‚Äî though notoriously difficult, employing many rare characters that challenge even learned commentators. When word reached the court that Sima was dying in 117 BCE, Emperor Wu dispatched an official to collect his writings, but the poet had already passed away at approximately age 62.

The Shanglin Park

Imperial Hunting Preserve ¬∑ Outside Chang'an

The Physical Park

Outside his capital of Chang'an (modern Xi'an), Emperor Wu of Han maintained a vast wilderness preserve for imperial hunts and excursions. The Shanglin Park (‰∏äÊûóËãë, "Supreme Forest Garden") was an estimated 167 kilometers in circumference ‚Äî a staggering expanse containing mountains, rivers, forests, and elaborate palace complexes.

Imperial Hunts

The park served multiple functions: a game reserve stocked with exotic animals, a botanical garden, a site for military exercises, and a stage for displaying imperial magnificence to foreign envoys. The hunts themselves were spectacular affairs involving thousands of participants, elaborate chariots, trained animals, and theatrical spectacles.

The Commission

When Sima appeared before Emperor Wu, he proposed something unprecedented: rather than compose another fu about vassal lords' hunting grounds, he wished to create a grand rhapsody celebrating the excursions and hunts of the Son of Heaven himself. The emperor provided brushes and bamboo slips, and Sima produced his magnum opus ‚Äî originally titled Tianzi youlie fu (Rhapsody on the Excursions and Hunts of the Son of Heaven).

The Three Speakers

A Dialectical Framework

The Dialogic Form

The Zixu fu and Shanglin fu together form a single extended work employing three fictional speakers with deliberately satirical names. This dialogue format allows Sima to stage a debate that escalates from regional boasting to imperial magnificence ‚Äî and ultimately to moral reflection.

The Debate Unfolds

Part I (Zixu fu): Sir Fantasy, visiting Qi, is taken hunting. When asked about it, he launches into a description of the vast Yunmeng marshes in his native Chu. His rhapsodic outburst overwhelms Master No-Such, who accuses him of improper boasting.

Part II (Shanglin fu): Lord Not-Real enters to refute both speakers. The hunting parks of Chu and Qi are nothing compared to the Son of Heaven's Shanglin Park. Through over 250 lines, he catalogs the imperial domain in staggering detail ‚Äî then delivers an unexpected moral conclusion.

"The richness and beauty of the land says nothing about the moral and virtue of the king." ‚Äî Critique delivered within the Shanglin fu

Why Fictional Names?

The speakers' names ‚Äî all meaning "non-existent" or "unreal" ‚Äî signal that this is literary artifice, not historical record. The names also suggest the emptiness of mere boasting and the ultimate futility of material excess without moral substance.

The Catalog of All Things

Epideictic Rhapsody as Imperial Inventory

The Epideictic Mode

The Shanglin fu is the supreme example of epideictic fu ‚Äî display rhetoric meant to showcase verbal virtuosity while cataloging the emperor's sovereignty over all things in his realm. The park becomes a synecdoche for the empire: everything within it represents the vast dominion of Han.

The Hunt Itself

Having taken over 250 lines to describe the park, Sima details its use: as autumn turns to winter, the emperor travels from his palace to hunt. His carriage is "decorated with carved ivory and drawn by six jade dragons." The hunt is depicted with "exaggerated exuberance" ‚Äî part real observation, part fantastic imagination.

"Twisting and twining, tortuously turning, / Arch aloft, precipitously piled." ‚Äî Sima Xiangru describing the mountains, trans. Knechtges

Language and Difficulty

Sima's language is "notorious for long catalogues of animal, plant, fish, and mineral names, many of which are only vaguely identified even by the most learned commentaries." The text bristles with rare characters, alliterative compounds, and rhyming pairs that create sensual impressions of movement, sound, and color ‚Äî a "dazzling verbal description" that generations of readers have celebrated as "one of the most extraordinary displays of poetic creativity in the Chinese language."

Feng: Indirect Admonition

Critique Through Praise

The Concept of Feng (Ë´∑)

The word feng (Ë´∑) refers to "the presentation of criticism and advice by indirection, usually in the form of subtle moral reprimands addressed to the emperor." Han fu writers used elaborate descriptions to "sway" (feng) imperial opinion ‚Äî Yang Xiong later wrote that the primary purpose of his fu was to convey moral messages to rulers.

The Paradox of the Shanglin Fu

The Shanglin fu presents a paradox: it dazzles with descriptions of extravagance, yet its purpose is to critique that very extravagance. Through "concrete description of how the parks looked and how the hunting activities were implemented," Sima delivers "an indirect critique of the imperial hunts" with the moral that "a ruler has to obey propriety and moral principles of government."

The Moral Turn

After hundreds of lines celebrating imperial magnificence, the fu reaches an unexpected conclusion: the emperor becomes aware of the implications of such extravagance and sets his attention to reform ‚Äî "implementing economic and land-use policies favorable to the people and adopting proper administrative and ritual conduct for the correct governance of the empire."

Key Themes

  • Material abundance ‚â† moral virtue: The park's richness "says nothing about the moral and virtue of the king"
  • Microcosm of empire: The park symbolically represents Han's dominion over all things
  • The humiliation of boasters: Both Sir Fantasy and Master No-Such are refuted, exposing the emptiness of regional pride
  • Imperial frugality: The ultimate message advocates restraint against "sensual indulgence in hunting"
  • Display as didactic: Showing excess makes visible its moral costs

Did It Work?

Later critics debated whether ornate fu actually persuaded emperors or merely entertained them. Yang Xiong famously worried that his rhapsodies were "like the work of someone who carves insects" ‚Äî beautiful but ineffective. Yet the Shanglin fu's structure ‚Äî pleasure building to moral reflection ‚Äî established a template for court literature that persisted for centuries.

Discussion Questions

  • How does the Shanglin fu compare to the earlier fu we've read (Song Yu, Jia Yi)? What's different about its scale and purpose?
  • Can you criticize power while celebrating it? Is the moral ending sincere, or is it a fig leaf for imperial flattery?
  • Why use fictional speakers with names meaning "non-existent"? What does this frame accomplish?
  • The park is described as a "synecdoche for the empire." How does cataloging all things assert sovereignty?
  • Compare the Shanglin fu's approach to political critique with Song Yu's "Fu on the Wind." How do both use natural description to comment on social conditions?
  • Sima's language is famously difficult, full of rare characters. What's the rhetorical effect of linguistic excess?
  • Consider the relationship between writer and patron: How does Sima navigate praising Emperor Wu while also admonishing him?
