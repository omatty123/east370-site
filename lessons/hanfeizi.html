師 From Teacher to Student

The Xunzi Connection: Han Feizi was reputedly a student of Xunzi. While never mentioning Xunzi by name, the Han Feizi clearly shows familiarity with Xunzi's ideas.

The Key Pivot: Xunzi said human nature is problematic and needs ritual (禮) to shape it. Han Feizi agrees nature is problematic, but replaces ritual with law (法). Where Xunzi trusted educated rulers and moral cultivation, Han Feizi trusts only systems.

Biography: Han Feizi was from the ruling house of Hán 韓. According to the Shiji, he was a stutterer who turned to writing. He was dispatched as envoy to Qin around 234 BCE. Li Si (a fellow Xunzi student) had him imprisoned and poisoned.

法術勢 The Legalist Framework

Three Pillars:

  • 法 fǎ (law/standards): clear, public, consistently enforced rules
  • 術 shù (techniques): methods of bureaucratic control to prevent ministers from usurping power
  • 勢 shì (positional power): the ruler's position itself is the source of authority, not personal virtue

What sets Legalism apart

Unlike Confucians and Mohists: no belief in changing people's fundamental desires or interests. Unlike Daoists: doesn't seek to reduce desires through simplicity. Instead: channel self-interest through a system of rewards and punishments.

Laws must be clear, public, and consistently enforced. The ruler's position itself (勢) is the source of authority, not personal virtue.

主道 Chapter 5 — The Way of the Ruler (pp. 324-327)
道 Key Terms
Chinese Pinyin Meaning
道 dào The Way; here, the ruler's method of governing
形名 xíng míng Form and name; matching results to proposals
無為 wú wéi Nonaction; the ruler does not act personally
虛靜 xū jìng Empty and still; the ruler's proper disposition
虛 The Empty Ruler

The ruler must not reveal desires, intentions, or preferences. "A ruler must not reveal his desires, for if he does so, his ministers will carve and polish themselves accordingly."

If the ruler shows likes or dislikes, ministers will manipulate him. The ruler should be "empty and still," causing others to exhaust themselves while he remains inscrutable.

形名 Form and Name

The ruler's key technique: When ministers make proposals (名 name), hold them to their results (形 form).

  • If results match proposals → reward
  • If results don't match proposals → punish
  • This applies even when results exceed the proposal

The Keeper of Caps

A keeper who placed clothing on a sleeping ruler was punished for overstepping his role, even though the action was kind. Accountability requires staying within bounds.

蔽 Five Obstructions

The five ways ministers block the ruler:

  • Ministers blocking the ruler's access to information
  • Controlling wealth and profits
  • Exceeding their authority
  • Doing what they think is right (bypassing the ruler's judgment)
  • Cultivating their own disciples and factions

The ruler must monopolize power; ministers must not be allowed to grasp hold of it.

虛靜無為而無不為
"Empty, still, doing nothing, yet nothing left undone."
Chapter 5 — Han Feizi adapts Daoist language for authoritarian ends
"Be so great that you cannot be measured, so deep that you cannot be fathomed. Ensure the correspondence between achievements and claims, examine laws and rules, execute those who act without authorization." — Han Feizi, Chapter 5
有度 Chapter 6 — Having Standards (pp. 328-333)
強弱 No Eternal Strength

"No state is eternally strong; no state is eternally weak. When those who uphold the law are strong, the state is strong."

Historical examples: King Zhuang of Chu, Duke Huan of Qi, King Zhao of Yan, King Anxi of Wei. All rose through law, all fell when law was abandoned.

公私 Public Law vs. Private Interest

The fundamental problem: ministers pursue private gain at the state's expense. "This is like trying to put out a fire while carrying kindling on one's back."

Solution: eliminate private crookedness, attend to public laws. If promotion is based on public acclaim, factionalism results. If based on law, capable people rise.

繩墨 The Carpenter's Standard

"So long as the ink line is straight, warped wood can be cut straight; so long as the steelyard is properly hung, what is heavy will be seen to be greater than what is light."

Law is the objective standard. Like the carpenter's tools, it doesn't bend for the noble or the base. The law treats all equally: punishments for transgressions do not bypass great ministers, while rewards for goodness do not leave common people behind.

"As for what the law confers, even the wise cannot shirk from it and even the brave do not dare to dispute it. Punishments for transgressions do not bypass great ministers, while rewards for goodness do not leave the common people behind." — Han Feizi, Chapter 6
二柄 Chapter 7 — The Two Handles (pp. 333-336)
刑德 Key Terms
Chinese Pinyin Meaning
刑 xíng Punishment; killing, executing
德 dé Favor/reward (NOT moral virtue here)
二柄 èr bǐng The Two Handles of governance
刑 Punishment and Favor

"The two handles are punishment and favor. What is meant by 'punishment' and 'favor'? Killing and executing are cases of 'punishment.' Venerating and rewarding are cases of 'favor.'"

The ruler must monopolize both handles. If ministers control rewards, people follow ministers. If ministers control punishments, people fear ministers.

Historical warnings

Tian Chang seized favor and murdered Duke Jian of Qi. Zi Han seized punishments and robbed the ruler of Song.

虎 The Tiger's Claws

"The reason the tiger can subdue the dog is because of its claws and fangs. If the tiger were to cast aside its claws and fangs and allow the dog to use them, then the tiger would instead be subdued by the dog."

The ruler's power is the two handles. Lose them, and you're controlled by those who were supposed to serve you.

冠 The Keeper of Caps

Marquis Zhao of Han fell asleep; the Keeper of Caps saw he was cold and placed clothing over him.

The ruler punished both the Keeper of Caps (overstepping) and the Keeper of Clothing (negligence). The harm of intruding on others' offices was considered greater than the harm of cold.

This is the system working as designed.

明主之所導制其臣者，二柄而已矣
"The means by which the clear-sighted ruler guides and controls his ministers are the two handles and that is all."
Chapter 7 — Governance reduced to its mechanical essence
說難 Chapter 12 — The Difficulty of Persuasion (pp. 336-340)
難 The Real Difficulty

"The difficulty of persuasion is not the difficulty of gaining knowledge... The difficulty lies in understanding the heart of the person being persuaded such that you can ensure your persuasion matches it."

You must read the ruler's psychology: what they actually want vs. what they pretend to want.

危 The Minefield

Even knowing the truth, danger is everywhere:

  • If the ruler's secret affairs are divulged, you are endangered
  • If the ruler accepts your advice but a clever outsider takes credit, you are endangered
  • If your connection is not yet intimate but your discussions are brilliant, you get no credit for success but full blame for failure
  • If the ruler makes a mistake and you speak of "propriety and righteousness," you are endangered
桃 Mi Zixia and the Half-Eaten Peach

Mi Zixia was favored by the ruler of Wey. He once faked an order to use the ruler's carriage (to visit his sick mother). The ruler praised his filial piety. He gave the ruler a half-eaten peach. The ruler praised his love.

Later, when Mi Zixia's beauty faded and the ruler's love cooled, the same actions were cited as crimes. "Love had turned to hate."

The lesson: persuasion depends on the ruler's feelings toward you, not on the quality of your argument.

龍 The Inverted Scales

"The dragon is a creature that can become accustomed to being ridden. However, on the underside of its throat is a square foot of inverted scales. If someone brushes against them, the dragon will certainly kill them."

The ruler has "inverted scales" too. If you can avoid brushing against them, "that is a success."

"Therefore, if one is loved by their ruler, one's wisdom will be deemed appropriate and the two will become increasingly close. If one is hated by one's ruler, one's knowledge will be deemed inappropriate and one will be accused of crimes." — Han Feizi, Chapter 12
問 Discussion Questions
Question 1
Han Feizi was Xunzi's student. How does Legalism grow from Xunzi's premises? Where exactly does the break occur? At what point does "human nature needs shaping" become "human nature needs controlling"?
Question 2
Han Feizi adapts Daoist language (無為, 虛靜) for the ruler. Compare his "nonaction" with the Daodejing's. Is this a genuine philosophical development or a cynical appropriation?
Question 3
The Keeper of Caps is punished for a kind act. Is this justice or absurdity? What does this story reveal about the difference between Legalist and Confucian views of governance?
Question 4
"The Difficulty of Persuasion" reads like a survival manual for court life. How does this chapter complicate the image of Han Feizi as a cold theorist? What does it reveal about the human cost of autocratic systems?
Question 5
The story of Mi Zixia shows that the same action can be praised or condemned depending on the ruler's feelings. What does this imply about the gap between Han Feizi's theory (objective law) and his reality (arbitrary power)?
Question 6
Han Feizi was imprisoned and poisoned by Li Si (a fellow student of Xunzi) in the very Qin state that adopted Legalist principles. Is there something self-defeating about a system built on distrust? Does the theory contain the seeds of its own destruction?
