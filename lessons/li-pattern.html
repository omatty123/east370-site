導論 INTRODUCTION
理 Why "Pattern" and Not "Principle"

The Translation Problem

The character 理 (li) originally described the veins running through jade, the grain visible in wood, the natural markings in stone. It names a structure that is in the material, not imposed on it from outside. You see it by looking closely at what is already there.

Bryan Van Norden deliberately translates 理 as "Pattern" rather than "Principle" in Readings in Later Chinese Philosophy. The choice matters. "Principle" in Western philosophy carries rationalist baggage: an abstract rule that governs from above, like a law of logic or a Platonic Form. That is not what 理 means in either the Buddhist or Neo-Confucian tradition.

Translation note: "Pattern" captures the Chinese sense: an immanent structure visible in the grain of things. Like seeing veins in marble, the order is IN the material. Both Fazang and Zhu Xi would agree on this point, even as they disagree on almost everything else about what the Pattern contains.
傳 A Concept That Crossed Traditions

The Journey

This page traces how the concept of 理 traveled from Tang dynasty Huayan Buddhism into Song dynasty Neo-Confucianism and was fundamentally transformed in the process. The same word, the same character, came to mean something radically different.

Buddhist Li (Fazang, Tang)

For Fazang (法藏, 643–712), 理 is the relational structure of dependent arising: the Pattern by which all phenomena interpenetrate, grounded in emptiness. It has no moral content. It is not a force. It is not a substance.

Neo-Confucian Li (Zhu Xi, Song)

For Zhu Xi (朱熹, 1130–1200), 理 is the moral order inherent in all things: the reason things are as they are AND the reason they ought to be as they are. It is grounded in Heaven (天), not emptiness. It has full moral content.

The Central Transformation

Same word. Same character. Different universe. Understanding how the transformation happened, and what was gained and lost in the process, is the purpose of this page.

法藏 LI IN FAZANG
金獅子 Gold = Li, Lion = Shi pp. 86–91

The Golden Lion Metaphor

In his Essay on the Golden Lion, Fazang explains Huayan philosophy to Empress Wu Zetian using a golden lion statue. The gold of the statue represents 理 (the Pattern); the lion-shape represents 事 (shi, phenomena).

"The gold of the statue is a metaphor for the unified, underlying Pattern of relationships, while the appearance of the statue as a lion is a metaphor for our illusory perception of things as independent individuals." Tiwald & Van Norden, p. 87; trans. Bryan W. Van Norden

Gold Has No Fixed Form

Gold could be a lion, a bowl, a ring. It takes any shape. The point: 理 is the formless ground from which all appearances arise. The lion-shape is real (the gold really does appear as a lion), but it is not independently real (there is no lion apart from the gold).

空 What Li Meant for Fazang

Three Things Li Is NOT (for Fazang)

  • Li is NOT a substance. It is grounded in emptiness (kong 空). Gold has no fixed form of its own; the "Pattern" is not a thing that exists independently.
  • Li is NOT a force. It does not cause phenomena to arise. Phenomena arise through dependent arising (yuanqi 緣起): the craftsman shapes the gold, conditions produce events.
  • Li is NOT moral. It has no content about right and wrong. The Pattern of relationships among phenomena is ethically neutral. It describes how things interrelate, not what they ought to be.

What Li IS (for Fazang)

  • The relational structure of dependent arising. The pattern by which all phenomena mutually constitute each other.
  • The ground of interpenetration. Because all things are empty of self-nature, each thing reflects and contains every other.

Why This Matters

  • Neo-Confucians will change all three characteristics
  • Li will become a substance (logically prior to material force)
  • Li will become a cause (the reason things are as they are)
  • Li will become moral (containing the full ethical order)
四法界 THE FOUR DHARMADHATUS
華嚴 Four Levels of Reality

Huayan Buddhism describes four progressively deeper ways of understanding reality. Each dharmadhatu (法界, "realm of dharmas") represents a level of insight.

事法界 Shi Fajie
Realm of Phenomena
Everyday experience. We see distinct, individual things: this table, that person, this moment. The conventional level of reality.
理法界 Li Fajie
Realm of Pattern
The underlying emptiness/suchness common to all things. Beyond individual appearances, there is the unified Pattern. Like seeing only the gold, not the lion.
理事無礙法界 Li-Shi Wu Ai Fajie
Interpenetration of Pattern & Phenomena
Pattern and phenomena are non-obstructing. Each contains the other. The gold IS the lion; the lion IS the gold. This is the level Neo-Confucians adopted.
事事無礙法界 Shi-Shi Wu Ai Fajie
Interpenetration of Phenomena & Phenomena
Every phenomenon contains every other phenomenon. Indra's Net: each jewel reflects every other jewel. The highest Huayan insight. Neo-Confucians rejected this.
取捨 What They Took and What They Left

Adopted: The Li-Shi Framework

Song Neo-Confucians adopted the basic architecture of the third dharmadhatu: the idea that Pattern (li) and phenomena (shi) interpenetrate, that Pattern is present in all things, and that understanding Pattern is the goal of philosophical inquiry. This framework gave them something classical Confucianism never had: a metaphysics.

Rejected: Three Core Elements

  • The emptiness foundation. For Fazang, li is grounded in emptiness (kong 空). Neo-Confucians replaced this with a positive ground: Heaven (tian 天). Li is not empty; it is full of moral content.
  • The fourth dharmadhatu. The radical Huayan claim that every phenomenon interpenetrates every other phenomenon (Indra's Net) had no place in Neo-Confucian thought. Each thing has its own portion of li, but a horse does not contain a plum blossom.
  • The soteriological goal. Fazang's entire system aims at liberation from samsara. Neo-Confucians replaced this with moral cultivation: becoming a sage in this world, not escaping it.

The Narrowing

  • Kept: li/shi distinction, Pattern present in all things
  • Dropped: Emptiness, interpenetration of phenomena, liberation
  • Added: Moral content, qi (material force), sagehood
  • The result was a significantly narrower concept but one with immense ethical power

宗密 THE BRIDGE: ZONGMI
宗密 Buddhist Who Engaged Confucianism

A Double Position

Zongmi (宗密, 780–841) held a unique position in Chinese intellectual history. He was recognized as the fifth patriarch of the Huayan school AND held a Chan (Zen) lineage. This dual authority gave him credibility across Buddhist schools.

Engaging Chinese Philosophy

In his Inquiry into the Origin of Humanity (原人論, Yuan ren lun), Zongmi did something no major Buddhist thinker before him had attempted systematically: he ranked Confucianism and Daoism alongside Buddhist schools in a classification of teachings (panjiao 判教). He placed them lowest in his hierarchy, but the act of ranking them at all acknowledged them as serious philosophical positions worth engaging.

The True Mind

Zongmi taught that all beings possess a "true mind" (zhenxin 真心) that is originally enlightened. This concept would echo centuries later in Wang Yangming's "innate knowing" (liangzhi 良知), though the two thinkers would have understood its content very differently.

橋 Preparing the Ground

The journey from Buddhist li to Neo-Confucian li was not a straight line. It required a 200-year gap, the near-destruction of institutional Buddhism, and a Confucian intellectual renaissance.

理學 SONG NEO-CONFUCIAN LI
程頤 Cheng Yi: Li Yi Fen Shu 1033–1107

"The Pattern Is One, Its Manifestations Many"

Cheng Yi (程頤) formulated what became the foundational principle of the li-xue (理學, "Study of Pattern") school: 理一分殊 (li yi fen shu). There is one universal Pattern, but it manifests differently in each thing. Every blade of grass, every human relationship, every natural process contains its own portion of the single, unified li.

Li as Moral Order

For Cheng Yi, li is not ethically neutral. It tells you what things ought to be. The li of a ruler includes the Pattern of proper rulership. The li of a son includes the Pattern of filial piety. Understanding li means understanding both what things are and what they should be.

Gewu: Investigation of Things

How do you access li? Through gewu (格物), the investigation of things. Study individual things carefully, and you will come to understand the Pattern they embody. Read widely, observe closely, and the unified li behind all manifestations will become clear. This is an incremental, outward-facing method: look at the world to find the Pattern in it.

朱熹 Zhu Xi: Li-Qi Metaphysics 1130–1200

Li + Qi

Zhu Xi (朱熹) synthesized Cheng Yi's insights into the most comprehensive metaphysical system in Chinese philosophy. His framework pairs li (理, Pattern) with qi (氣, material force). Li is formless and immaterial; qi gives form and substance. Together they constitute all things. There has never been any qi without li, or any li without qi, but li is logically prior.

Tianli: Heaven's Pattern

Zhu Xi's concept of tianli (天理, Heaven's Pattern) represents the full moral order. This is not the amoral, relational li of Fazang. Tianli includes the principles of proper human relationships (the five relationships), the foundations of virtue, and the cosmic order that makes moral cultivation possible and necessary.

Xing Ji Li: Human Nature IS Li

Zhu Xi's most consequential claim: xing ji li (性即理), human nature IS the Pattern. Your nature is not separate from the universal moral order. When you cultivate yourself, you are not importing moral rules from outside. You are recovering the Pattern that is already your nature, but obscured by impure qi.

The Complete Reversal

  • Fazang: li is empty of content; Zhu Xi: li is full of moral content
  • Fazang: li describes how things relate; Zhu Xi: li prescribes what things ought to be
  • Fazang: the self is an illusion; Zhu Xi: human nature IS the Pattern
  • Fazang: understanding li leads to liberation; Zhu Xi: understanding li leads to sagehood

三變 Three Fundamental Shifts

1. Emptiness to Moral Content

For Fazang, li is grounded in emptiness (kong 空). It describes a relational structure without ethical content. For Zhu Xi, li is the moral order of the universe. The Pattern tells you not just how things are but how they should be. Emptiness was replaced by fullness.

2. Liberation to Cultivation

Fazang's entire philosophical system aims at liberation from samsara: escape from the cycle of birth and death. Neo-Confucians replaced this with the goal of becoming a sage (shengren 聖人) within the social world. You don't leave the world; you perfect your participation in it.

3. No-Self to Human Nature

Buddhism denies a permanent, independent self (wu wo 無我). Zhu Xi's most radical move was to claim that human nature IS li (xing ji li 性即理). Far from denying the self, he made the self the very location of the cosmic moral order. Your nature is not something to see through; it is something to cultivate.

借鑒 Why This Is Not Mere Borrowing

The Problem Buddhism Solved

Classical Confucianism (Kongzi, Mengzi, Xunzi) had a rich ethical tradition but no metaphysics. It could tell you to be virtuous but could not explain WHY the moral order exists, what grounds it, or how it relates to the structure of reality itself. Buddhism, with centuries of sophisticated metaphysical thinking, provided the conceptual tools.

Architecture vs. Building

The Neo-Confucians borrowed Buddhist architectural techniques: how to build a metaphysical system, how to think about the relationship between the formless and the formed, how to connect cosmology to ethics. But the building they constructed was Confucian through and through: it housed social ethics, political order, human relationships, and moral cultivation.

The Critics' Charge

This is why contemporary critics sometimes called the Neo-Confucians "Confucians on the outside, Buddhists on the inside" (外儒內佛, wai ru nei fo). The charge was not entirely unfair. But it also missed the point: the content of Neo-Confucian li is as far from Buddhist emptiness as a concept can travel while keeping the same name.

王陽明 WANG YANGMING
心即理 The Mind Is Li 1472–1529

The Bamboo Anecdote

As a young man, Wang Yangming (王陽明, 1472–1529) tried Zhu Xi's method of gewu (格物, investigation of things). He sat in front of bamboo for seven days, trying to "investigate" its li. He got sick. He found nothing. He concluded the method was wrong.

Xin Ji Li: The Mind IS the Pattern

Wang Yangming's alternative was radical: xin ji li (心即理), the mind itself IS li. You do not need to investigate external things to find the Pattern. Li is already complete in your mind. The moral order is not something you discover by looking outward; it is something you already possess inward.

Liangzhi: Innate Knowing

Wang Yangming called this innate moral knowledge liangzhi (良知). Everyone possesses it. The sage and the ordinary person have the same liangzhi. The difference is that the sage acts on it directly, while the ordinary person's liangzhi is obscured by selfish desires. Moral cultivation is not accumulation of knowledge but removal of what blocks the knowledge you already have.

輪迴 The Circle Closes

A Structural Parallel

Consider the parallel between Wang Yangming's liangzhi (良知, innate knowing) and Zongmi's zhenxin (真心, true mind). Both locate the essential truth inside the mind rather than in external things. Both claim this inner knowledge is innate and complete. Both say the task is to remove obstructions, not to acquire new information.

The Loop

The concept of li traveled in something like a circle:

  • Fazang (Buddhist): li is the pattern of emptiness, understood through meditation
  • Cheng Yi & Zhu Xi (Neo-Confucian): li is the moral order, understood through external investigation
  • Wang Yangming (Neo-Confucian): li is in the mind, understood through inner knowing

The endpoint (inner knowing) structurally resembles the starting point (inner realization of emptiness), even though the content of what is known is completely different.

Crypto-Buddhist?

Ming dynasty Confucians who followed Wang Yangming were frequently accused of being crypto-Buddhists. The charge had structural logic: returning li to the mind looked suspiciously like the Buddhist move Zhu Xi had worked so hard to escape. But Wang Yangming's liangzhi is moral knowledge, not awareness of emptiness. The architecture may echo Zongmi, but the building remains Confucian.

討論 DISCUSSION QUESTIONS
Translation
Question 1

Should we translate 理 the same way for Fazang and Zhu Xi? Van Norden uses "Pattern" for both. Is this helpful (showing continuity) or misleading (hiding a fundamental transformation)?

Question 2

The character 理 originally described the veins running through jade. How does this etymology shape what both traditions mean by the concept? Does the image of "veins in jade" fit Buddhist emptiness, Confucian moral order, or both?

Transformation
Question 3

Compare Fazang's golden lion metaphor with Zhu Xi's li-qi framework. Both explain how the formless (li) relates to the formed (shi/qi). What changes when you replace "gold/lion" with "li/qi"? What is gained, and what is lost?

Question 4

Zongmi ranked Confucianism below Buddhism in his classification of teachings. Song Neo-Confucians reversed this ranking, treating Buddhism as a deviation from the true Way. What does each ranking reveal about the tradition that produced it?

Legacy
Question 5

Critics called Neo-Confucians "Confucians on the outside, Buddhists on the inside" (外儒內佛). Based on the comparison table above, is this charge fair? Where exactly is the line between "borrowing a framework" and "being influenced at the core"?

Question 6

Wang Yangming's "the mind is li" (心即理) structurally resembles Zongmi's "true mind" (真心). Does the similarity of structure matter if the content is completely different? Can the same conceptual architecture carry radically different philosophical buildings?
