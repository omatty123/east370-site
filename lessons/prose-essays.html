Executive Summary

What You Need to Know

The Reading

Birch, Anthology of Chinese Literature, pp. 242–259. Seven prose essays from the mid-Tang dynasty by two champions of the guwen 古文 (Ancient Style) movement. Han Yu (Han Yü in Birch) dominates with six pieces ranging from satirical fantasy to anti-Buddhist polemic to a raw grief letter. Liu Zongyuan (Liu Tsung-yüan in Birch) closes with a parable about trees and governance.

Historical Moment

The mid-Tang, roughly 800–820 CE. The High Tang's golden age is over. The An Lushan Rebellion (755) shattered central authority. Buddhism has become the dominant intellectual and institutional force, with enormous monasteries, tax-exempt land, and political influence. The Confucian literati feel sidelined. The guwen movement is their counterattack: restore simple, direct prose as a vehicle for moral argument.

Core Themes

  • The guwen revival: ancient simplicity against ornamental parallelism
  • Anti-Buddhist polemic: Han Yu's frontal assault on imperial Buddhist patronage
  • The scholar-official's relationship to power: begging, banishment, and defiance
  • Grief, poverty, and the cost of intellectual integrity
  • Governance by non-interference (Liu Zongyuan's Daoist counterpoint)

Birch's Romanization

  • Han Yü = Han Yu 韓愈 (Pinyin)

Literary Context

The Guwen 古文 Movement

The Problem

Parallel Prose Had Won

By the Tang dynasty, the dominant literary prose style was pianwen 駢文, "parallel prose": ornamental, balanced, and decorative. Sentences came in matched pairs with strict tonal patterns. It was virtuosic and beautiful, but Han Yu and his allies saw it as hollow. Prose had become jewelry rather than argument.

The "ornamental styles (parallel prose and the like) which had been the rage" (Birch, p. 242) were the target. Guwen writers wanted prose that could do something: persuade, mourn, accuse, reform.

The Solution

Ancient Style, New Weapon

Han Yu championed a return to the "ancient style" (guwen), modeling prose on the directness of pre-Han masters. This was not antiquarianism. It was a political program: "in the hands of scholar-officials no movement could remain purely literary in scope" (Birch, p. 242). Simple prose carried Confucian moral arguments that ornate parallelism could not.

The movement prized "lucidity and artful simplicity" (Birch, p. 242) while still allowing literary skill. These are not dry tracts. "Farewell to Poverty" is fantasy. "Proclamation to the Crocodile" is absurdist theater. The style is simple; the art is not.

Complete Essay-by-Essay Analysis

Han Yu 韓愈 (768–824)

Han Yu "stood in the front line of defence of Confucian principles against what he considered the heresies and decadence of the age" (Birch, p. 242). Orphaned young, repeatedly banished, he wrote prose that ranges from bitter satire to raw grief to political confrontation.

韓 Han Yu (pp. 244–257)
Farewell to Poverty
送窮文 Sung ch'iung wen
tr. J.K. Rideout (pp. 244–246)
"This humble person does not venture to ask by what road, but has had a boat and a cart made ready and loaded with wet rice and with dry. The day is auspicious, the time favourable; you may profitably go to whichever of the four quarters you please."
Analysis: Han Yu attempts to banish the Demon of Poverty from his household. Birch notes "it is not to be thought that Han Yu ever attempted to perform the curious ceremony here described" (p. 243). The essay is a "highly sophisticated form of begging letter, intended to attract the reader's sympathy and excite his indignation against a regime which would permit the poverty of a man of obvious literary ability and upright moral character." The Demon responds by naming its five companions: Poverty through Intelligence, Poverty through Learning, Poverty through Writing, Poverty through Fate, and Poverty through Friendship. Each is a virtue that makes Han Yu unemployable. In the end, the master "hung his head in dismay, raised his hands and offered his apology: and having burnt the boat and the cart, he led the demons to the place of honour." The joke lands hard: Han Yu's excellence is itself the cause of his poverty. You cannot separate the man from the curse.
Lament for His Nephew Shih-erh Lang
祭十二郎文 Chi Shih-erh Lang wen
tr. J.K. Rideout (pp. 246–249)
"Alas, a little child I was left an orphan. When I grew up, I could not recall the faces of my father and mother. It was solely to my elder brother and his wife that I turned."
Analysis: The most emotionally devastating piece in the selection. Han Yu writes a funeral offering (祭文 jiwen) for his nephew (actually his brother's son, Shih-erh Lang, "Twelfth Young Master"), who died young and unexpectedly. The letter recounts their shared orphanhood, the brother's early death, the years of separation as Han Yu pursued government appointments. "Who could have told that the younger would be taken and the elder and the sickly spared whole?" The grief is compounded by guilt: Han Yu chose career over family, pursued "some small salary" in the capital, and now his nephew is dead. The letter's final movement, where Han Yu vows to retire and raise both their children on a farm, reads as a repudiation of everything the examination system promised. The prose is plain and direct. No parallel ornament. This is guwen doing what it was designed to do: carrying emotion too raw for decoration.
Against the God of the Wind
送風伯 Sung Feng Po
tr. J.K. Rideout (p. 250)
"Of this drought, who is the cause? I know the author: it is the God of the Wind who is to blame. The hills made the clouds to rise, the marshes sent up their vapour. The thunder whipped the chariot, the lightning shook the banner. The rain was promising, ready to fall; but the God of the Wind was angry..."
Analysis: Birch identifies this as "actually 'rhyme-prose,' and a heavily veiled satire." The "God of the Wind" is the emperor (the Sun Crow), "the object of Han Yu's lampoon," who had shown readiness to remit taxes in a bad year but then "rapaciously demanded their payment" (p. 243). The Wind God scattered the rain clouds, preventing relief. Han Yu uses the ancient form of addressing a nature deity to deliver political criticism without naming names. The closing threat ("the sentence of Heaven will fall upon you") is aimed at real power.
Memorial on the Bone of Buddha
論佛骨表 Lun Fo ku piao
tr. J.K. Rideout (pp. 250–253)
"I humbly submit that Buddhism is but one of the religious systems obtaining among the barbarian tribes, that only during the later Han dynasty did it filter into the Middle Kingdom, and that it never existed in the golden age of the past."
Analysis: Han Yu's most famous memorial, and the piece that nearly killed him. Emperor Xianzong (Hsien-tsung) had ordered Buddhist monks to escort a bone relic of the Buddha from Fengxiang (Feng-hsiang) to the Imperial Palace. Han Yu submitted this memorial to stop the procession. His arguments are relentless: (1) Buddhism is foreign and barbarian. (2) Before Buddhism, Chinese emperors lived long and ruled well. After its introduction, dynasties shortened and collapsed. (3) Emperor Wu-di of Liang, who "consecrated his life to Buddha, made no animal sacrifices in his ancestral temple, and ate but one meal a day of vegetables and fruit," was driven out by a rebel and died of starvation. (4) Confucius said of supernatural beings "I stand in awe... but keep them at a distance." (5) Even if the Buddha were alive today, Xianzong should give him "no greater reception than an interview in the Strangers' Hall, a ceremonial banquet, and the gift of a suit of clothes, after which you would have sent him under guard to the frontier." The memorial calls for the bone to be thrown into water or fire and Buddhism "destroyed root and branch for ever." Distinguished Confucians rallied behind Han Yu when the sentence provoked by the memorial was reduced from execution to banishment to Chaozhou (Ch'ao-chou) in the far south.
Proclamation to the Crocodile
祭鱷魚文 Chi o yü wen
tr. J.K. Rideout (pp. 253–255)
"The governor has received the command of the Son of Heaven to come here as an officer, he cannot but dispute with you, crocodile: and if you have understanding, do you hearken to the governor's words."
Analysis: Written during Han Yu's exile as governor of Chaozhou in the far south. A crocodile was terrorizing the local population. Han Yu had his officer Ch'in Chi throw a sheep and a pig into the creek as an offering, then addressed the crocodile in the form of an official proclamation. The essay deploys the full apparatus of bureaucratic authority: "Now a Son of Heaven has succeeded to the throne of Tang, who is godlike in his wisdom, merciful in peace and fierce in war." The crocodile is given three days to leave, then five, then seven. If it refuses, "the governor will then choose skilful officers and men, who shall take strong bows and poisoned arrows and conclude matters with you, crocodile, nor stop until they have slain you utterly." Birch records that "a violent storm which rose that same evening and lasted for several days" followed, and "it was found that the crocodile had removed to a distance of sixty li to the west; nor was Ch'ao-chou again plagued with crocodiles for several hundred years" (p. 242). The piece works on two levels: as absurdist theater (a Confucian bureaucrat negotiating with a reptile) and as a demonstration that the moral authority of the state extends to all creatures. In Han Yu's worldview, even animals should obey orders issued on the authority of the Son of Heaven.
An Enquiry Into Slander
原毀 Yüan hui
tr. J.K. Rideout (pp. 255–257)
"The demands that the great men of the past made of themselves were heavy and comprehensive. What they expected of others was light and simple. The first saved themselves from laziness; the second made men glad to do good."
Analysis: Han Yu's most purely Confucian essay. The argument is structural: ancient sages demanded everything of themselves and little of others, so people were inspired to be good. Modern officials demand nothing of themselves and everything of others, so people resent and slander. "Laziness prevents their own development; jealousy makes them dread development in others." The essay diagnoses slander not as a character flaw but as a systemic consequence of moral laziness at the top. "To test my theory I have publicly pronounced this gentleman to be good... Those who have agreed have been either his associates, or persons so far removed from him that their interests could in no way clash." When Han Yu praises someone, only the uninvolved agree. The involved attack. The conclusion is bleak: "the pursuit of truth and goodness will bring in this world the glory of fame and praise is to expect the impossible." Good work provokes slander. This is the cost of the guwen project itself.
The Other Master

Liu Zongyuan 柳宗元 (773–819)

Liu Zongyuan was Han Yu's ally in the guwen movement but a different temperament: "celebrated for his depictions of natural scenery in prose and verse," he "wrote also one of the best-loved of all the allegories" (Birch, p. 243).

柳 Liu Zongyuan (pp. 258–259)
Camel Kuo the Gardener
種樹郭橐駝傳 Chung shu Kuo T'o-t'o chuan
tr. Cyril Birch (pp. 258–259)
"I cannot make a tree live for ever or flourish. What I can do is comply with the nature of the tree so that it takes the way of its kind. When a tree is planted its roots should have room to breathe, its base should be firmed, the soil it is in should be old and the fence around it should be close. When you have it this way, then you must neither disturb it nor worry about it, but go away and not come back."
Analysis: Camel Kuo is a hunchback tree-grower in Feng-lo (Fengle), west of Chang'an. His secret: plant the tree correctly, then leave it alone. Other growers "dote too fondly and worry too anxiously. They inspect the tree every morning and cosset it every night; they cannot walk away from it without turning back for another look. The worst of them will even scrape off the bark to see if it is still living, or shake the roots to test whether they are holding fast." When asked whether his philosophy applies to government, Camel Kuo claims ignorance but then describes officials who "go to a lot of trouble issuing orders as though they were deeply concerned for the people; yet all they achieve is an increase of misfortune." The final line delivers the parable's point: "'The art I sought was of cultivating trees; the art I found was of cultivating men. Let this be passed on as a lesson to all in office!'" This is the Daoist position within the guwen movement. Where Han Yu advocates vigorous Confucian intervention (memorializing the emperor, proclaiming to crocodiles), Liu Zongyuan argues that the best governance is minimal governance. Plant right. Walk away.
Side-by-Side

Two Masters Compared

Han Yu 韓愈 Liu Zongyuan 柳宗元
Dates 768–824 (56 years) 773–819 (46 years)
Philosophy Militant Confucian. Buddhism is barbarism. The state must enforce orthodox values. Daoist-inflected Confucian. Good governance means minimal interference. Let things grow.
Relation to Power Confrontational. Memorializes the emperor, gets banished. Proclaims to crocodiles. Demands the burning of Buddhist relics. Observational. Draws political lessons from a gardener. Criticizes officials through parable rather than direct petition.
Prose Style Muscular, argumentative, sometimes furious. Ranges from satirical fantasy to raw grief. Always making a case. Gentle, scenic, allegorical. "Celebrated for his depictions of natural scenery" (Birch). The argument arrives disguised as landscape or anecdote.
Key Genre Memorials, satirical essays, funeral offerings, official proclamations Allegories, landscape prose, biographical sketches
On Buddhism "This decayed and rotten bone, this filthy and disgusting relic." Wants it destroyed root and branch. Absent from this selection. More tolerant in general.
On Governance The state should actively enforce Confucian morality. Officials must speak truth to power, even at the cost of exile. "I avoid hindering the fruiting; I have no power to bring it forward or make it more abundant." Govern by not governing.
Death Died 824. Eventually restored to honor. Became the model for all later guwen writers. Died 819 in exile at age 46. Never recalled from banishment.
For the Classroom

Discussion Questions

  1. "Farewell to Poverty" names five demons: Poverty through Intelligence, Learning, Writing, Fate, and Friendship. Each is actually a virtue. Han Yu's point is that his own excellence makes him unemployable. Is this self-pity, social criticism, or both? How does the satirical form change the argument?
  2. The "Memorial on the Bone of Buddha" argues that Buddhism is foreign, that Buddhist emperors suffered short reigns, and that the Buddha himself deserves only a brief diplomatic reception. Which of these arguments is historical, which is rhetorical, and which is xenophobic? Can you separate them?
  3. Han Yu's "Lament for His Nephew" is pure grief with no philosophical agenda. Why does Birch include it alongside the polemical essays? What does the lament reveal about the personal cost of the scholar-official's life that the political essays hide?
  4. "Proclamation to the Crocodile" treats a reptile as a bureaucratic subordinate. Is this absurd comedy, a demonstration of Confucian moral cosmology (where all creatures obey Heaven's mandate), or a displaced attack on human enemies? What happens when you read the crocodile as a metaphor for the local warlords or Buddhist monasteries Han Yu could not actually proclaim against?
  5. Liu Zongyuan's Camel Kuo argues that good governance means leaving people alone: "plant right, then walk away." How does this Daoist position sit alongside Han Yu's activist Confucianism? Could both be right? Are they arguing about the same problem from different angles, or do they disagree fundamentally?
  6. Compare Han Yu's anti-Buddhist memorial to the Confucian critiques of Buddhism you've encountered elsewhere in the course (e.g., Xunzi's arguments against superstition, or the general Confucian emphasis on ritual propriety over metaphysical speculation). Is Han Yu extending an existing tradition, or doing something new?
  7. "An Enquiry Into Slander" argues that ancient sages demanded much of themselves and little of others, while modern officials do the reverse. Is this observation specific to Tang China, or does it describe a universal pattern in how institutions decay?
