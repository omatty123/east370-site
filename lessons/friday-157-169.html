週五 157–169

Friday Readings: Letters of Withdrawal & Recluse Ideals

Yang Yiin · Hsi K'ang · Tao Ch'ien · Kung Chih-kuei
報孫會宗書
Letter to Sun Hui-tsung
Author: Yang Yiin (Yang Yun 楊惲) · Western Han · p. 159

Author & Context

The Wenxuan text notes that Yang Yun, having lost office and returned home, received an admonishing letter from Sun Huizong and replied in defense of his conduct and outlook.

What This Work Does

  • Defends a scholar-official’s dignity after political demotion.
  • Frames withdrawal as a matter of character and integrity rather than public performance.

Concepts to Teach

  • Letters as moral self-justification and reputation management.
  • Retreat vs. disgrace: what counts as “proper” withdrawal?

Importance & Influence

  • Use it to model how a literatus answers censure: the letter turns blame into a moral self-portrait.
與山巨源絕交書
Letter to Shan Tao (Letter of Severance)
Author: Hsi K'ang (Ji Kang 嵇康) · Wei-Jin · p. 162

Author & Context

The Wenxuan note says Shan Tao proposed that Ji Kang succeed him in office; Ji replied with a refusal and explained his incompatibility with political life.

Britannica identifies Ji Kang as a leading member of the Seven Sages of the Bamboo Grove and a Daoist thinker who advocated following nature rather than institutions.

What This Work Does

  • Rejects office and rebukes friends who accept court service.
  • Argues for a personal ethics rooted in naturalness and refusal of coerced duty.

Concepts to Teach

  • “Withdrawal” as moral protest, not laziness.
  • Daoist critique of court culture and ritual.

Importance & Influence

  • Helped define the ethos of the Seven Sages and the literature of principled non-participation.
桃花源記
Peach Blossom Spring
Author: Tao Ch'ien (Tao Yuanming 陶潛/陶淵明) · Eastern Jin · p. 167

Author & Context

The tale is attributed to Tao Yuanming and tells of a fisherman who discovers a hidden, idyllic community cut off from outside turmoil.

What This Work Does

  • Creates a utopian counter-world that is peaceful and self-sufficient, beyond dynastic change.
  • Suggests that perfect refuge is fragile and hard to recover once lost.

Concepts to Teach

  • Utopia as critique of political disorder.
  • Retreat as imagination: the “place” is real in story but elusive in reality.

Importance & Influence

  • The Peach Blossom Spring became a cultural symbol of an ideal world and inspired later painting traditions.
北山移文
Proclamation on North Mountain
Author: Kung Chih-kuei (Kong Zhigui 孔稚珪) · Southern Qi · p. 169

Author & Context

The piece is a parallel-prose composition preserved in the Wenxuan.

Scholarly commentary describes it as a satire of a would-be recluse who seeks reputation and office, often linked to Zhou Yong, highlighting how “reclusion” can be performative.

What This Work Does

  • Attacks hypocritical withdrawal as a strategy for career advancement.
  • Uses “North Mountain” as a moral stage for sincerity versus self-promotion.

Concepts to Teach

  • Authentic vs. performative reclusion.
  • Parallel prose as a vehicle for satire and moral critique.

Importance & Influence

  • Selected into Wenxuan, which helped make it a standard reference text for debates about reclusion.
