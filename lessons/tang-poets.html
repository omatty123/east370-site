Executive Summary

What You Need to Know

The Reading

Birch, Anthology of Chinese Literature, pp. 217–236. "Poets of a Generation: One" covers three Tang Dynasty masters. Wang Wei is represented by nature poetry and a letter to his friend Pei Di (P'ei Ti). Li Bai (Li Po in Birch) ranges from mountain mysticism to war protest to drunken celebration. Du Fu (Tu Fu in Birch) appears through his eight-poem "Autumn Meditation" sequence, written in exile.

Historical Moment

The reign of Emperor Xuanzong (Hsuan-tsung, r. 712–756), called "Ming-huang, the Illustrious." This was the golden age of shi poetry, the High Tang. All three poets lived through or near the An Lushan Rebellion (755), the cataclysm that ended the Tang's golden age and scattered the literati class.

Core Themes

  • Nature as spiritual dissolution vs. nature as backdrop for human drama
  • The tension between official service and reclusion
  • Wine as philosophical vehicle (Li Bai) vs. chrysanthemums as exile's emblem (Du Fu)
  • Friendship poetry: Wang-Pei Di exchange, Li Bai's letters, Du Fu's longing
  • War and the frontier: Li Bai's anti-war protest, Du Fu's grief for Ch'ang-an

Birch's Romanization

  • Li Po = Li Bai 李白 (Pinyin)
  • Tu Fu = Du Fu 杜甫 (Pinyin)
  • P'ei Ti = Pei Di 裴迪
  • Ch'ang-an = Chang'an 長安
  • K'uei-chou = Kuizhou 夔州
  • Hsuan-tsung = Xuanzong 玄宗

Timeline: Three Lives, One Rebellion

699
Wang Wei born in Qi County, Shanxi. Will become poet-painter.
699
Li Bai born in Suyab (modern Kyrgyzstan) or Sichuan. Same year as Wang Wei.
712
Du Fu born near Luoyang. Thirteen years younger than the other two.
712
Emperor Xuanzong ascends. Beginning of the High Tang golden age.
~720s
Wang Wei passes the jinshi exam, enters civil service. Begins painting career.
~740s
Wang Wei retreats to his Wang River estate (Wangchuan). Writes the Wang River Collection with Pei Di.
742–744
Li Bai summoned to court. Brief appointment at the Hanlin Academy. Dismissed (legend: too drunk and arrogant).
744
Li Bai and Du Fu meet. One of the most famous encounters in Chinese literary history.
755
AN LUSHAN REBELLION. The watershed. Chang'an falls. The golden age ends. All three poets' lives are shattered.
757–58
Du Fu serves briefly as "Reminder" at court. Loses favor for writing critical memorials.
759
Wang Wei dies at 60. Had been captured by rebels, forced to serve them. Pardoned but broken.
762
Li Bai dies at 63. Legend: drowned reaching for the moon's reflection while drunk.
766
Du Fu writes "Autumn Meditation" at Kuizhou, in exile on the Yangtze gorges.
770
Du Fu dies at 58. Never returned to Chang'an. Died on a boat on the Xiang River.

Vital Rundown

The Three Masters

王維
Wang Wei
699–759 (60 years)
The Poet-Painter
Philosophy
Chan (Zen) Buddhism. Sought dissolution of self in nature. His courtesy name "Mojie" (摩詰) references the Buddhist figure Vimalakirti.
Signature Move
"The closest union with the natural world that has ever been expressed." Harmony, stillness, emptiness. Painting and poetry as twin arts.
Key Location
Wang River estate (Wangchuan), south of Chang'an. Semi-retired retreat where he composed with friend Pei Di.
Poems in Reading
Letter to P'ei Ti River Wang (6 poems) Seeing Master Yuan Off To Asst. Prefect Chang
Translators
Arthur Waley, C.J. Chen & Michael Bullock, Kwock & McHugh, Cyril Birch
李白
Li Bai
699–762 (63 years)
The Banished Immortal
Philosophy
Daoist eclecticism. "Now epicurean, now Taoist in characteristic admixture." Wine as gateway to transcendence. The Dao in the cup.
Signature Move
Grandeur, spontaneity, cosmic vision. Drinks alone with the moon. Writes begging-letters with the swagger of a prince. Anti-war fury alongside drunken ecstasy.
Key Location
Everywhere. Born in Central Asia or Sichuan, wandered the empire. Brief court stint 742–744. Exiled to Yelang after the rebellion.
Poems in Reading
On the Mountain Spring Thoughts To Someone Far Away Night Mooring Han-tan Dancers To Old Friend Hsin Fighting South Four Poems on Wine Letter to Han Ching-chou
Translators
Kwock & McHugh, Arthur Waley, William Acker, A.C. Graham, J.R. Hightower
杜甫
Du Fu
712–770 (58 years)
The Poet-Sage
Philosophy
Confucian conscience. Political commitment and moral seriousness. A loyalist's grief over a broken empire. Duty to speak truth to power, even at personal cost.
Signature Move
"Encrusted with jewels of compressed esoteric allusion." Dense, allusive, emotionally devastating. War poems: "simple, direct, and immediate of impact."
Key Location
Kuizhou (K'uei-chou), on the Yangtze gorges. Wrote "Autumn Meditation" there in 766 during his second autumn of exile, far from Chang'an.

Three Poets Compared

Complete Poem-by-Poem Analysis

Every Poem in the Reading

王 Wang Wei (pp. 219–224)
Letter to the Bachelor of Arts P'ei Ti
tr. Arthur Waley (prose letter, p. 219)
...When night was far advanced, I mounted Hua-tzu's Hill and saw the moonlight tossed up and thrown down by the jostling waves of Wang River. On the wintry mountain distant lights twinkled and vanished; in some deep lane beyond the forest a dog barked at the cold, with a cry as fierce as a wolf's. The sound of villagers grinding their corn at night filled the gaps between the slow chimes of a distant bell.
Analysis: Birch calls this the preface to the Wang-Pei Di poem exchange. It is a prose poem disguised as a letter. Notice how it achieves the same dissolution as the verse: dog, bell, grinding corn. No commentary. Pure image. The invitation to Pei Di to visit in spring turns a private letter into a miniature treatise on friendship and landscape.
1. The Hill of Hua-Tzu
From "Poems of the River Wang" · tr. C.J. Chen & Michael Bullock (p. 220)
WANG: The birds fly away / into infinite space: / Over the whole mountain / returns the splendour of autumn. / Ascending and descending / Hua-tzu hill, / I feel / unbounded bewilderment and lamentation. P'EI: The sun sets, / the wind rises among the pines. / Returning home, / there is a little dew upon the grass. / The reflection of the clouds / falls into the tracks of my shoes, / The blue of the mountains / touches my clothes.
Analysis: The exchange format matters. Wang voices loss and bewilderment. Pei Di answers with quiet physical details: dew, cloud-reflection, blue touching cloth. Together they model how two minds meet the same landscape differently. Wang sees cosmic emptiness. Pei Di feels the mountain literally touching him.
2. The Deer Enclosure
tr. C.J. Chen & Michael Bullock (pp. 220–221)
WANG: On the lonely mountain / I meet no one, / I hear only the echo / of human voices. / At an angle the sun's rays / enter the depths of the wood, / And shine / upon the green moss. P'EI: At the end of day / the mountain looks cold. / But a belated wanderer / still passes on his way. / He knows nothing / of the life of the wood: / Nothing remains / but the tracks of the buck.
Analysis: The quintessential Wang Wei poem. The self disappears. What remains: sound, light, moss. Four lines, total emptiness. This is Chan Buddhism as poetry. Pei Di's response reintroduces the human figure, but that figure "knows nothing." Both poets circle the same void from different angles.
3. The Path of the Ash Trees
tr. C.J. Chen & Michael Bullock (p. 221)
WANG: On the narrow path, / sheltered by the ash trees, / In the secrecy of their shade / flourishes the green moss, / Only swept / when someone answers the gate, / Fearing that the monk from the mountain / has come to call. P'EI: To the south of the gate, / along by the ash trees, / Is the path over the hillcrest, / that leads to Lake Yi. / When the autumn comes / it rains much on the mountain; / No one picks up / the falling leaves.
Analysis: Wang Wei hints at Buddhist-recluse practice: the path is swept only for a visiting monk. The moss is valued, not removed. Pei Di's reply lets autumn rain accumulate. Both poems celebrate neglect as a form of attentiveness. The unswept leaves are the point.
4. The Pavilion of the Lake
tr. C.J. Chen & Michael Bullock (pp. 221–222)
WANG: Light the boat that carries me / to meet the gentle guest, / Who from a great distance / is coming over the lake. / Then, on the terrace, / before a cup of wine, / On every side / the lotus flowers will open. P'EI: In front of the balcony, / as the expanse of water / fills with ripples, / The solitary moon / goes wandering without pause. / From the depth of the valley / the cries of the monkeys rise; / Borne by the wind / they reach me in my room.
Analysis: Wang Wei is surprisingly social here: wine, a guest, lotus. But it is still nature doing the welcoming (the lotuses "will open" as if on cue). Pei Di turns to solitude. The moon wanders, the monkeys cry. The contrast between friendship and loneliness within the same landscape is the point of the exchange form.
5. The Stream at the House of the Luans
tr. C.J. Chen & Michael Bullock (p. 222)
WANG: Gusts of wind / in the autumn rain; / The water falls headlong, / it spills from the rocks in torrents. / The waves leap capriciously / one on the other in flight; / The startled white heron / comes down to earth again. P'EI: The voice of the stream / resounds to the farthest bay. / I walk along the shore / towards the southern ford. / Here and there on the water / ducks and egrets glide, / Always they return, impelled / to the proximity of men.
Analysis: Wang Wei captures violent motion: cascading water, leaping waves, a startled heron. Pei Di responds with calm: gliding ducks drawn toward human presence. The contrast is between nature's wildness and its domestication, wild stream vs. settled shore.
6. The Cove of the Wall of Meng
tr. C.J. Chen & Michael Bullock (p. 223)
WANG: My new house / is at the beginning of the wall of Meng, / Among old trees / and remains of decaying willows. / The other, after me, / who will he be? / Vain his grief / for this which was mine. P'EI: My new hut / is under the old wall: / Occasionally I go up / to the ancient enclosure. / There is nothing of the past now / about the old wall; / Men of today, uncaring, / come and go.
Analysis: Both poets meditate on impermanence beside ruins. Wang Wei asks who will grieve for his home after he is gone. Pei Di notes that the past has already vanished from the old wall. This is the closing poem of the sequence, and it turns the entire Wang River exchange into a meditation on transience. Everything celebrated in the previous poems will pass.
Seeing Master Yüan Off on His Mission to Kucha
tr. C.H. Kwöck & Vincent McHugh (p. 224)
City on Wei the morning rain wet on light dust Around the inn green willows fresh I summon you: Drink one more cup No old friends, my friend When you start westward for Yang Kuan
Analysis: One of the most famous farewell poems in Chinese. The spatial layout on the page mimics the scattering of departure. "No old friends, my friend" contains the entire paradox of farewell: you are about to lose the only person who matters. The destination (Kucha, over 2000 km west on the Silk Road) makes the distance feel absolute.
To the Assistant Prefect Chang
tr. Cyril Birch (p. 224)
In evening years given to quietude, The world's worries no concern of mine, For my own needs making no other plan Than to unlearn, return to long-loved woods: I loosen my robe before the breeze from pines, My lute celebrates moonlight on mountain pass. You ask what laws rule "failure" or "success" -- Songs of fishermen float to the still shore.
Analysis: The recluse ideal in miniature. "Unlearn" is the key verb. The final couplet refuses the question entirely. Compare this to Li Bai's aggressive self-promotion in the letter to Han Ching-chou: same genre (poem/letter to an official), opposite posture. Wang Wei answers the question of success with fishermen who don't care about the question.
李 Li Bai (pp. 225–234)
On the Mountain: Question and Answer
tr. Kwöck & McHugh (p. 225)
You ask me: Why do I live on this green mountain? I smile No answer My heart serene On flowing water peachblow quietly going far away This is another earth another sky No likeness to that human world below
Analysis: Birch says this is where "the unearthly beauty" begins. Compare to Wang Wei's silence: both refuse the question, but Li Bai smiles and makes it theatrical. The Daoist mountain as separate cosmos. "No likeness to that human world below" is more defiant than Wang Wei's quiet retreat. Li Bai always performs, even in solitude.
Spring Thoughts
tr. Kwöck & McHugh (p. 225)
O grass of Yen like green silk flowing Green boughs low on mulberries of Ch'in All that time! you've been thinking of home and all that time! my heart breaking In my silk bedcurtain spring wind
Analysis: A wife's lament for a husband away on frontier duty. Li Bai ventriloquizes female experience. "Yen" (north, frontier) and "Ch'in" (home region) establish the geography of separation. The "spring wind" in the empty bed is both seasonal and erotic. The spatial layout on the page enacts the distance between lovers.
To Someone Far Away
tr. Kwöck & McHugh (p. 226)
When she was here pretty darling flowers filled the hall Now she's gone pretty darling left her bed behind On her bed th'embroidered coverlet rolled up never slept in again Three years to the day still keeps the scent of her Fragrance never lost pretty darling never came back Yellow leaves falling when I think of her white dew on green moss
Analysis: A poem of absence and grief. The coverlet "rolled up / never slept in again" is devastating in its physical specificity. The scent lasting three years is sensory memory as a form of haunting. The closing image (yellow leaves, white dew, green moss) is pure Wang Wei territory, but Li Bai gets there through loss, not contemplation.
Night Mooring at Cow's Creek
tr. Kwöck & McHugh (pp. 226–227)
At Cow's Creek on Western River the night Sky still blue not a rag of cloud I go on deck to look at the bright moon thinking of the great General Hsieh of old I also can make poetry but that man's like will not be found again In the morning we make sail and go The maple leaves fall as they will
Analysis: Li Bai compares himself to the ancient poet-general Xie Tiao (Hsieh). "I also can make poetry" is Li Bai's characteristic swagger. But then the admission: "that man's like will not be found again." Even Li Bai's ego contains grief. The closing "maple leaves fall as they will" accepts transience with a shrug that is itself poetic.
Visiting Han-tan: The Dancers at the Southern Pavilion
tr. Kwöck & McHugh (p. 227)
They sang to me and drummed, the boys of Yen and Chao, Lovely girls plucked the sounding string. Their painted cheeks shone like dazzling suns; The dancers' sleeves shook out like blossoming boughs... Where is the Prince of Chao, what has he left But an old castle-moat where tadpoles breed? Those three thousand knights that sat at his board, Is there one among them whose name is still known? Let us make merry, get something in our own day To set against the pity of ages still unborn.
Analysis: Li Bai's carpe diem at its most urgent. The dancers are alive now; the Prince of Chao's three thousand knights are forgotten. The "old castle-moat where tadpoles breed" is a brutal image of glory decayed. "To set against the pity of ages still unborn" is Li Bai arguing that present pleasure is the only answer to historical oblivion.
To Yüan Tan-ch'iu
tr. Kwöck & McHugh (pp. 227–228)
My friend is lodging high in the Eastern Range, Dearly loving the beauty of valleys and hills. At green spring he lies in the empty woods, And is still asleep when the sun shines on high. A pine-tree wind dusts his sleeves and coat; A pebbly stream cleans his heart and ears. I envy you who far from strife and talk Are high-propped on a pillow of grey mist.
Analysis: Tan-ch'iu was a Daoist adept and Li Bai's friend. The poem is an idealized portrait of the recluse life. "A pebbly stream cleans his heart and ears" is the ancient trope of the recluse who washes his ears after hearing of worldly offers. Li Bai envies what Wang Wei actually lives.
To His Old Friend Hsin
tr. A.C. Graham (p. 228)
Long ago, among the flowers and willows, We sat drinking together at Ch'ang-an... We thought, you and I, that it would always be like this. How should we know the grass would stir and dust rise on the wind? Suddenly foreign horsemen were at the Hsien-ku Pass Just when the blossom at the Palace of Ch'in was opening on the sunny boughs. Now I, unhappy, am on my way to banishment at Yeh-lang...
Analysis: Before and after the An Lushan Rebellion in a single poem. "Foreign horsemen at the pass" is the exact moment the golden age ends. The blossoms were still opening when the world fell. Li Bai is now exiled. The poem's power is in the contrast: flowers and cavalry, wine and banishment, in the same breath.
Fighting South of the Ramparts
tr. Arthur Waley (pp. 228–229)
The King's armies have grown grey and old Fighting ten thousand leagues away from home. The Huns have no trade but battle and carnage; They have no fields or ploughlands... Crows and hawks peck for human guts, Carry them in their beaks and hang them on the branches of withered trees... Know therefore that the sword is a cursed thing Which the wise man uses only if he must.
Analysis: Written to an old anonymous folk-song tune (Birch includes the original at the bottom of the page). The final line quotes the Dao De Jing directly. Li Bai channels the anti-war tradition from the Warring States through the Tang frontier. The image of guts hung on withered branches is among the most violent in Chinese poetry.
Four Poems on Wine, No. 1
tr. William Acker (pp. 230–232)
Amidst the flowers / a jug of wine -- / I pour alone / lacking companionship, / So raising the cup / I invite the moon, / Then turn to my shadow / which makes three of us. ... I start a song / and the moon begins to reel, / I rise and dance / and the shadow moves grotesquely. ... After I'm drunk / let each one go his way. / Let us bind ourselves for ever / for passionless journeyings. / Let us swear to meet again / far in the Milky Way.
Analysis: The most famous Li Bai poem. Moon, shadow, self = a trinity of loneliness disguised as a party. "Passionless journeyings" (wuqing you) is a Buddhist term applied to cosmic wandering. Wine is not escape but communion with the cosmos.
Four Poems on Wine, No. 2
tr. William Acker (pp. 231–232)
If Heaven itself / did not love wine, / Then no Wine Star / would shine in the sky. / And if Earth also / did not love wine, / Earth would have no such / place as Wine Fountain. ... If wise men and sages / are already drinkers / What is the use / of seeking gods and fairies? / With three cups / I understand the great Way / With one jar / I am at one with Nature.
Analysis: Li Bai builds a cosmological argument for drinking. If heaven and earth love wine, who is he to refuse? The final couplet ("With three cups I understand the great Way") is the poem's thesis: wine as epistemology. This is Daoist spontaneity weaponized against Confucian sobriety.
Four Poems on Wine, No. 3
tr. William Acker (p. 231)
In the third month / the city of Hsien-yang -- / Thousands of flowers / at noon like brocade. / Who is able / in spring to be sad alone? / Faced with this / to drink is the shortest way / Infinite things / as well as short and long / Alike have early been / offered us by Creation. / A single cup / may rank with life and death, / The myriad things / are truly hard to fathom.
Analysis: The argument shifts from cosmology to mortality. "A single cup may rank with life and death" elevates drinking to existential philosophy. The spring brocade of flowers makes sadness feel perverse, so drink. The "myriad things" resist understanding; wine offers a shortcut that bypasses intellect.
Four Poems on Wine, No. 4
tr. A.C. Graham (pp. 232–233)
Have you not seen How the Yellow River, which flows from heaven and hurries toward the sea, never turns back?... At dawn black silk, by evening changed to snow? While there is pleasure in life, enjoy it, And never let your gold cup face the moon empty! Heaven gave me my talents, they shall be used; A thousand in gold scattered and gone will all come back again. ... I ask only to be drunk for ever and never wake!
Analysis: The grandest of the four, and the most famous. "Heaven gave me my talents, they shall be used" is Li Bai's supreme self-confidence. The Yellow River never turning back is both natural law and carpe diem argument. The closing feast scene, with its address to Master Ts'en and Friend Tan-ch'iu, makes drinking a communal ritual against oblivion.
Letter to Han Ching-chou
tr. J.R. Hightower (prose, pp. 233–234)
...I am a commoner from Lung-hsi. I have been a vagabond through Ch'u and Han. At fifteen, I was fond of swordplay, and went around visiting often the feudal lords. At thirty, I had perfected my literary style and was in touch with the Prime Minister. Although I am not a full six feet in height, still in my heart there is the courage of ten thousand men...
Analysis: Li Bai's begging-letter to a potential patron. The self-promotion is breathtaking: swordsman, literary genius, moral giant, all in one paragraph. Compare directly to Wang Wei's "To the Assistant Prefect Chang" where Wang refuses the question of success. Li Bai demands recognition. Same genre, opposite personality. Both are performing a role that Tang literary culture made available.
杜 Du Fu (pp. 235–236)
Autumn Meditation, No. 1
From "Ch'iu hsing" (秋興), written 766 at K'uei-chou (p. 235)
Gems of dew wilt and wound the maple trees in the wood: From Wu mountains, from Wu gorges, the air blows desolate. The waves between the river-banks merge in the seething sky, Clouds in the wind above the passes meet their shadows on the ground. Clustered chrysanthemums have opened twice, in tears of other days; The forlorn boat, once and for all, tethers my homeward thoughts. In the houses winter clothes speed scissors and ruler; The washing-blocks pound, faster each evening, in Pai Ti high on the hill.
Analysis: The opening of the eight-poem sequence. "Chrysanthemums have opened twice" = two autumns in exile. The "forlorn boat" is tethered just as Du Fu is tethered, unable to return to Chang'an. The closing couplet brings domestic life in: women sewing winter clothes, washing-blocks pounding. Even preparation for winter carries the weight of exile. Every line fuses landscape with political allegory.
Autumn Meditation, No. 2
"Ch'iu hsing" No. 2 (p. 235)
On the solitary walls of K'uei-chou the sunset rays slant, Each night guided by the Dipper I gaze towards the capital. It is true then that tears start when we hear the gibbon cry thrice; Useless my mission adrift on the raft which came by this eighth month.
Analysis: The Dipper points north toward Chang'an. Du Fu uses it as a compass of longing. The "gibbon cry thrice" is an old folk belief from the Yangtze gorges that three cries bring tears. The "raft which came by this eighth month" refers to the legend of a fisherman who floated out to sea on a raft and reached the Milky Way. Du Fu sees himself adrift, carried ever farther from home.
Autumn Meditation, No. 3
"Ch'iu hsing" No. 3 (p. 236)
The thousand houses, the circling mountains, are quiet in the morning light; Day by day in the house by the river I sit in the blue of the hills. Two nights gone the fisher boats once more come bobbing on the waves, Belated swallows in cooling autumn still flutter to and fro. K'uang Heng writing state papers, which earned me no credit, Liu Hsiang editing classics, my hopes elsewhere... Yet many of my school friends have risen in the world. By the Five Tombs in light cloaks they ride their sleek horses.
Analysis: Du Fu compares himself to two Han dynasty figures who served the state through writing but were not rewarded. K'uang Heng and Liu Hsiang (Kuang Heng and Liu Xiang in Pinyin) were his models for the scholar-official ideal. The bitter final couplet: his former classmates ride sleek horses at the imperial tombs, while he sits by the Yangtze. Success went to others.
Autumn Meditation, No. 4
"Ch'iu hsing" No. 4 (p. 236)
Well said, Ch'ang-an looks like a chessboard -- Won and lost for a hundred years, sad beyond all telling. The mansions of princes and nobles all have new lords, And another generation wears the caps and robes of office. Due north on the mountain passes the gongs and drums shake, To the chariots and horses campaigning in the west the winged dispatches hasten. While the fish and dragons fall asleep and the autumn river turns cold My native country, untroubled times, are always in my thoughts.
Analysis: Chang'an as chessboard = the capital won and lost by loyalists, rebels, and Tibetan invaders. "New lords" in old mansions: the political class has been replaced wholesale. The "gongs and drums" north and "winged dispatches" west show the empire fighting on multiple fronts. "My native country, untroubled times" is the heartbreak line. He mourns not just a place but a time that will not return.
Autumn Meditation, No. 5
"Ch'iu hsing" No. 5 (p. 236)
The gate of P'eng-lai Palace faces the southern hills, Dew collects on the bronze stems high in the Misty River. Fumes of the censers by the pictures in the ministry elude my sickbed pillow, The whitewashed parapets of turrets against the hills dull the mournful bugles. Look! On the wall, the moon in the ivy Already, by the shores of the isle, lights the blossoms on the reeds.
Analysis: Du Fu imagines himself back at the capital, remembering his brief time as "Reminder" at court (757-58). P'eng-lai Palace was an actual court building where he served. The "sickbed pillow" places him inside the ministry, but the imagined scene dissolves into moonlight on ivy. This is memory operating as hallucination, exile longing made vivid enough to feel present.
For the Classroom

Discussion Questions

  1. Wang Wei's "Deer Enclosure" has almost no human presence. Li Bai's "On the Mountain" is all personality. Du Fu's "Autumn Meditation" is dense with history. How does each poet's relationship to the self shape their poetry?
  2. All three poets deal with the problem of political service vs. withdrawal. Where does each stand? Is Wang Wei's retreat to Wang River the same kind of reclusion Tao Yuanming practiced? How is it different?
  3. Li Bai's "Four Poems on Wine" argues that drunkenness is a path to truth ("With three cups I understand the great Way"). Is this Daoist philosophy, or is he just making excuses? Compare to Ruan Ji's drinking in the previous unit.
  4. Du Fu's "Autumn Meditation" sequence is called "encrusted with jewels of compressed esoteric allusion." What does this mean in practice? Take one couplet and unpack the layers.
  5. The An Lushan Rebellion (755) runs through all three lives. How does each poet process catastrophe? Wang Wei was captured. Li Bai was exiled. Du Fu was broken. Which response resonates most, and why?
  6. Birch says Wang Wei "achieved perhaps the closest union with the natural world that has ever been expressed." Is this Buddhist? Daoist? Both? Neither? What separates it from simple nature description?
  7. Li Bai's letter to Han Ching-chou is an aggressive self-promotion piece ("the courage of ten thousand men"). Compare it to Wang Wei's "To the Assistant Prefect Chang" ("The world's worries no concern of mine"). Same genre (poem/letter to an official), opposite posture. What do these contrasts reveal about Tang literary culture?
