Three Poets Who Defined an Era

Wang Wei, Li Bai, and Du Fu were born within thirteen years of each other, during the golden age of Emperor Xuanzong. Together they shaped the common vessel of Chinese poetry into an erudite and exquisite perfection.

杜甫 Du Fu 712–770

Executive Summary

What You Need to Know

The Reading

Birch, Anthology of Chinese Literature, pp. 217–236. "Poets of a Generation: One" covers three Tang Dynasty masters. Wang Wei is represented by nature poetry and a letter to his friend Pei Di (P'ei Ti). Li Bai (Li Po in Birch) ranges from mountain mysticism to war protest to drunken celebration. Du Fu (Tu Fu in Birch) appears through his eight-poem "Autumn Meditation" sequence, written in exile.

Historical Moment

The reign of Emperor Xuanzong (Hsuan-tsung, r. 712–756), called "Ming-huang, the Illustrious." This was the golden age of shi poetry, the High Tang. All three poets lived through or near the An Lushan Rebellion (755), the cataclysm that ended the Tang's golden age and scattered the literati class.

Core Themes

  • Nature as spiritual dissolution vs. nature as backdrop for human drama
  • The tension between official service and reclusion
  • Wine as philosophical vehicle (Li Bai) vs. chrysanthemums as exile's emblem (Du Fu)
  • Friendship poetry: Wang-Pei Di exchange, Li Bai's letters, Du Fu's longing
  • War and the frontier: Li Bai's anti-war protest, Du Fu's grief for Ch'ang-an

Birch's Romanization

  • Li Po = Li Bai 李白 (Pinyin)
  • Tu Fu = Du Fu 杜甫 (Pinyin)
  • P'ei Ti = Pei Di 裴迪
  • Ch'ang-an = Chang'an 長安
  • K'uei-chou = Kuizhou 夔州
  • Hsuan-tsung = Xuanzong 玄宗
Historical Context

Timeline: Three Lives, One Rebellion

699
Wang Wei born in Qi County, Shanxi. Will become poet-painter.
699
Li Bai born in Suyab (modern Kyrgyzstan) or Sichuan. Same year as Wang Wei.
712
Du Fu born near Luoyang. Thirteen years younger than the other two.
712
Emperor Xuanzong ascends. Beginning of the High Tang golden age.
~720s
Wang Wei passes the jinshi exam, enters civil service. Begins painting career.
~740s
Wang Wei retreats to his Wang River estate (Wangchuan). Writes the Wang River Collection with Pei Di.
742–744
Li Bai summoned to court. Brief appointment at the Hanlin Academy. Dismissed (legend: too drunk and arrogant).
744
Li Bai and Du Fu meet. One of the most famous encounters in Chinese literary history.
755
AN LUSHAN REBELLION. The watershed. Chang'an falls. The golden age ends. All three poets' lives are shattered.
757–58
Du Fu serves briefly as "Reminder" at court. Loses favor for writing critical memorials.
759
Wang Wei dies at 60. Had been captured by rebels, forced to serve them. Pardoned but broken.
762
Li Bai dies at 63. Legend: drowned reaching for the moon's reflection while drunk.
766
Du Fu writes "Autumn Meditation" at Kuizhou, in exile on the Yangtze gorges.
770
Du Fu dies at 58. Never returned to Chang'an. Died on a boat on the Xiang River.

Vital Rundown

The Three Masters

王維
Wang Wei
699–759 (60 years)
The Poet-Painter
Philosophy
Chan (Zen) Buddhism. Sought dissolution of self in nature. His courtesy name "Mojie" (摩詰) references the Buddhist figure Vimalakirti.
Signature Move
"The closest union with the natural world that has ever been expressed." Harmony, stillness, emptiness. Painting and poetry as twin arts.
Key Location
Wang River estate (Wangchuan), south of Chang'an. Semi-retired retreat where he composed with friend Pei Di.
Poems in Reading
Letter to P'ei Ti River Wang (6 poems) Seeing Master Yuan Off To Asst. Prefect Chang
Translators
Arthur Waley, C.J. Chen & Michael Bullock, Kwock & McHugh, Cyril Birch
李白
Li Bai
699–762 (63 years)
The Banished Immortal
Philosophy
Daoist eclecticism. "Now epicurean, now Taoist in characteristic admixture." Wine as gateway to transcendence. The Dao in the cup.
Signature Move
Grandeur, spontaneity, cosmic vision. Drinks alone with the moon. Writes begging-letters with the swagger of a prince. Anti-war fury alongside drunken ecstasy.
Key Location
Everywhere. Born in Central Asia or Sichuan, wandered the empire. Brief court stint 742–744. Exiled to Yelang after the rebellion.
Poems in Reading
On the Mountain Spring Thoughts To Someone Far Away Night Mooring Han-tan Dancers To Old Friend Hsin Fighting South Four Poems on Wine Letter to Han Ching-chou
Translators
Kwock & McHugh, Arthur Waley, William Acker, A.C. Graham, J.R. Hightower
杜甫
Du Fu
712–770 (58 years)
The Poet-Sage
Philosophy
Confucian conscience. Political commitment and moral seriousness. A loyalist's grief over a broken empire. Duty to speak truth to power, even at personal cost.
Signature Move
"Encrusted with jewels of compressed esoteric allusion." Dense, allusive, emotionally devastating. War poems: "simple, direct, and immediate of impact."
Key Location
Kuizhou (K'uei-chou), on the Yangtze gorges. Wrote "Autumn Meditation" there in 766 during his second autumn of exile, far from Chang'an.
Poems in Reading

Translators
Not named in the excerpt for the "Autumn Meditation" sequence
Side-by-Side

Three Poets Compared

Wang Wei 王維 Li Bai 李白 Du Fu 杜甫
Core Identity Poet-Painter. Buddhist recluse. Banished Immortal. Daoist wanderer. Poet-Sage. Confucian conscience.
Relation to Nature Dissolves into it. Subject and landscape merge. Performs in front of it. Moon, mountain, river as cosmic audience. Reads history through it. Autumn, decay, seasons as political metaphor.
Relation to State Semi-retired. Served but retreated to Wang River. Wanted glory, got exile. Brief court stint, mostly outside. Devoted but punished. Lost favor for honesty. Died in exile.
Key Genre Nature quatrains. Landscape letter-poems. Drinking songs. Frontier ballads. Begging-letters. Regulated verse (lushi). Dense allusive sequences.
Tone Still. Luminous. Empty. The silence between sounds. Soaring. Defiant. Ecstatic. The shout across mountains. Heavy. Grieving. Dense. The weight of an empire's collapse.
Friendship Pei Di: quiet exchange of poems at Wang River. Yuan Tan-ch'iu, Master Ts'en: drinking companions. Memories of friends who've risen while he's stuck in exile.
On Wine "Before a cup of wine, on every side the lotus flowers will open." "With three cups I understand the great Way. With one jar I am at one with Nature." Absent. Chrysanthemums and tears, not cups.
Death Died 759, age 60. Captured by rebels, pardoned. Died 762, age 63. Legend: drowned reaching for moon. Died 770, age 58. On a boat. Never went home.

Selected Poems from the Reading

Key Poems to Know

王 Wang Wei
The Deer Enclosure
From "Poems of the River Wang" · tr. C.J. Chen & Michael Bullock
On the lonely mountain I meet no one, I hear only the echo of human voices. At an angle the sun's rays enter the depths of the wood, And shine upon the green moss.
Why it matters: The quintessential Wang Wei poem. The self disappears. What remains: sound, light, moss. Four lines, total emptiness. This is Chan Buddhism as poetry.
To the Assistant Prefect Chang
tr. Cyril Birch
In evening years given to quietude, The world's worries no concern of mine, For my own needs making no other plan Than to unlearn, return to long-loved woods: I loosen my robe before the breeze from pines, My lute celebrates moonlight on mountain pass. You ask what laws rule "failure" or "success" -- Songs of fishermen float to the still shore.
Why it matters: The recluse ideal in miniature. "Unlearn" is the key verb. The final couplet refuses the question entirely. Compare this to Li Bai's aggressive self-promotion in the letter to Han Ching-chou.
Letter to the Bachelor of Arts P'ei Ti
tr. Arthur Waley (prose letter)
...When night was far advanced, I mounted Hua-tzu's Hill and saw the moonlight tossed up and thrown down by the jostling waves of Wang River. On the wintry mountain distant lights twinkled and vanished; in some deep lane beyond the forest a dog barked at the cold, with a cry as fierce as a wolf's. The sound of villagers grinding their corn at night filled the gaps between the slow chimes of a distant bell.
Why it matters: Birch calls this the preface to the Wang-Pei Di poem exchange. It's a prose poem disguised as a letter. Notice how it achieves the same dissolution as the verse, but in prose. The dog, the bell, the grinding corn. No commentary. Pure image.
李 Li Bai
On the Mountain: Question and Answer
tr. Kwock & McHugh
You ask me: Why do I live on this green mountain? I smile No answer My heart serene On flowing water peachblow quietly going far away This is another earth another sky No likeness to that human world below
Why it matters: Birch says this is where "the unearthly beauty" begins. Compare to Wang Wei's silence: both refuse the question, but Li Bai smiles and makes it theatrical. The Daoist mountain as separate cosmos.
Four Poems on Wine, No. 1 (excerpt)
tr. William Acker
Amidst the flowers a jug of wine -- I pour alone lacking companionship, So raising the cup I invite the moon, Then turn to my shadow which makes three of us. ... I start a song and the moon begins to reel, I rise and dance and the shadow moves grotesquely. While I'm still conscious let's rejoice with one another, After I'm drunk let each one go his way.
Why it matters: The most famous Li Bai poem. Moon, shadow, self = a trinity of loneliness disguised as a party. Wine is not escape but communion with the cosmos. "Let us bind ourselves for ever / for passionless journeyings."
Fighting South of the Ramparts
tr. Arthur Waley
The King's armies have grown grey and old Fighting ten thousand leagues away from home. The Huns have no trade but battle and carnage; They have no fields or ploughlands... Crows and hawks peck for human guts, Carry them in their beaks and hang them on the branches of withered trees... Know therefore that the sword is a cursed thing Which the wise man uses only if he must.
Why it matters: Li Bai wrote this to an old anonymous folk-song tune. The final line quotes the Dao De Jing. This is not the drunken Li Bai. This is the political Li Bai, channeling anti-war tradition from the Warring States through the Tang frontier.
To His Old Friend Hsin
tr. A.C. Graham
Long ago, among the flowers and willows, We sat drinking together at Ch'ang-an. The Five Barons and Seven Grandees were of our company... We thought, you and I, that it would always be like this. How should we know the grass would stir and dust rise on the wind? Suddenly foreign horsemen were at the Hsien-ku Pass Just when the blossom at the Palace of Ch'in was opening on the sunny boughs. Now I, unhappy, am on my way to banishment at Yeh-lang...
Why it matters: Before and after the An Lushan Rebellion in a single poem. The "foreign horsemen at the pass" is the exact moment the golden age ends. Li Bai is now exiled. The cherry blossoms were still opening when the world fell.
杜 Du Fu
Autumn Meditation, No. 1
From "Ch'iu hsing" (秋興), written 766 at K'uei-chou
Gems of dew wilt and wound the maple trees in the wood: From Wu mountains, from Wu gorges, the air blows desolate. The waves between the river-banks merge in the seething sky, Clouds in the wind above the passes meet their shadows on the ground. Clustered chrysanthemums have opened twice, in tears of other days; The forlorn boat, once and for all, tethers my homeward thoughts. In the houses winter clothes speed scissors and ruler; The washing-blocks pound, faster each evening, in Pai Ti high on the hill.
Why it matters: The opening poem of the eight-poem sequence. "Chrysanthemums have opened twice" = two autumns in exile. The "forlorn boat" is tethered just as Du Fu is tethered, unable to return to Chang'an. Every line carries double meaning: landscape and political allegory fused.
Autumn Meditation, No. 4
"Ch'iu hsing" No. 4
Well said, Ch'ang-an looks like a chessboard -- Won and lost for a hundred years, sad beyond all telling. The mansions of princes and nobles all have new lords, And another generation wears the caps and robes of office. Due north on the mountain passes the gongs and drums shake, To the chariots and horses campaigning in the west the winged dispatches hasten. While the fish and dragons fall asleep and the autumn river turns cold My native country, untroubled times, are always in my thoughts.
Why it matters: Chang'an as chessboard = the capital has been won and lost by loyalists, rebels, and Tibetan invaders in turn. "My native country, untroubled times" is the heartbreak line. He mourns not just a place but a time.
Autumn Meditation, No. 2
"Ch'iu hsing" No. 2
On the solitary walls of K'uei-chou the sunset rays slant, Each night guided by the Dipper I gaze towards the capital. It is true then that tears start when we hear the gibbon cry thrice; Useless my mission adrift on the raft which came by this eighth month.
Why it matters: The Dipper points north, toward Chang'an. Du Fu uses it as a compass of longing. The "gibbon cry thrice" is an old folk belief that three cries bring tears. The raft adrift = a legend about floating to the Milky Way, used here as an image of exile without return.
For the Classroom

Discussion Questions

  1. Wang Wei's "Deer Enclosure" has almost no human presence. Li Bai's "On the Mountain" is all personality. Du Fu's "Autumn Meditation" is dense with history. How does each poet's relationship to the self shape their poetry?
  2. All three poets deal with the problem of political service vs. withdrawal. Where does each stand? Is Wang Wei's retreat to Wang River the same kind of reclusion Tao Yuanming practiced? How is it different?
  3. Li Bai's "Four Poems on Wine" argues that drunkenness is a path to truth ("With three cups I understand the great Way"). Is this Daoist philosophy, or is he just making excuses? Compare to Ruan Ji's drinking in the previous unit.
  4. Du Fu's "Autumn Meditation" sequence is called "encrusted with jewels of compressed esoteric allusion." What does this mean in practice? Take one couplet and unpack the layers.
  5. The An Lushan Rebellion (755) runs through all three lives. How does each poet process catastrophe? Wang Wei was captured. Li Bai was exiled. Du Fu was broken. Which response resonates most, and why?
  6. Birch says Wang Wei "achieved perhaps the closest union with the natural world that has ever been expressed." Is this Buddhist? Daoist? Both? Neither? What separates it from simple nature description?
  7. Li Bai's letter to Han Ching-chou is an aggressive self-promotion piece ("the courage of ten thousand men"). Compare it to Wang Wei's "To the Assistant Prefect Chang" ("The world's worries no concern of mine"). Same genre (poem/letter to an official), opposite posture. What do these contrasts reveal about Tang literary culture?
