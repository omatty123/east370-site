Executive Summary

The Chu Ci (楚辭, "Songs of Chu") stands as one of the two foundational anthologies of Chinese poetry, second only to the Shijing (Book of Songs). This guide accompanies David Hawkes' translation, the preeminent Western scholarly rendering of these ancient verses.

Historical Context

The Kingdom of Chu (楚) was centered in the Yangtze River valley in southern China, encompassing modern Hubei, Hunan, and parts of surrounding provinces. The Chu Ci represents a distinct southern Chinese literary tradition infused with shamanistic religious practices, contrasting sharply with the northern Zhou culture reflected in the Shijing.

The Primary Author

Qu Yuan (屈原, c. 340-278 BCE) is celebrated as China's first named poet. A minister of Chu whose unwavering loyalty to his king led to exile, he ultimately drowned himself in the Miluo River in protest and despair after learning that Qin forces had captured the capital. His death is commemorated annually at the Dragon Boat Festival (端午節 Duanwu Jie).

Literary Characteristics

  • Rich botanical and floral imagery - orchids, angelica, cassia, lotus - symbolizing moral virtue and purity
  • Shamanistic spirit-journeys and encounters with divine beings
  • Allegory of the loyal minister and inconstant ruler cast as unrequited love
  • Longer, more elaborate verse forms than the Shijing, with the characteristic exclamatory particle 兮 (xi)

The Kingdom of Chu

The rivers and lakes of the Yangtze valley - sacred to Chu's shamanistic traditions - form the geographic and spiritual landscape of the Chu Ci.

Biographies

Qu Yuan

屈原 Qu Yuan
c. 340-278 BCE
China's First Named Poet - Patriot of Chu - Father of Chinese Romanticism

Qu Yuan was born into the royal house of Chu at Zigui (modern Yichang, Hubei Province). His noble lineage granted him access to the highest echelons of Chu's court, where he rose to serve as Left Minister (左徒 zuotu) under King Huai.

In this role, Qu Yuan wielded considerable influence over both domestic governance and foreign affairs. He championed three interconnected policies: the appointment of capable ministers based on merit rather than birth, the clarification and reform of Chu's laws, and - most consequentially - an alliance with the eastern state of Qi to counterbalance the rising threat of Qin in the west.

"Long did I sigh and wipe away my tears, / To see my people bowed by griefs and fears."

- Li Sao (tr. Yang Hsien-yi)

Qu Yuan's downfall came through court intrigue. The Qin strategist Zhang Yi, seeking to isolate Chu diplomatically, bribed the corrupt minister Jin Shang and the king's favorite concubine Zheng Xiu. They slandered Qu Yuan before King Huai, accusing him of arrogance and disloyalty. The king, swayed by these whispers, exiled his most capable counselor.

In 278 BCE, the Qin general Bai Qi captured Ying, the Chu capital. Upon hearing this news, Qu Yuan composed his final poem, the Lament for Ying, then walked into the Miluo River clasping a stone, ending his life in ritual suicide.

Legacy

  • Established the Sao (騷) poetic tradition, distinct from the northern Shijing style
  • First Chinese poet known by name and individual biography
  • China's Tianwen (天問 "Heavenly Questions") space program is named after his cosmological poem
  • Dragon Boat Festival commemorates his death annually

King Huai of Chu

楚懷王 Chu Huai Wang
r. 328-299 BCE
Personal name: Xiong Huai 熊槐 - Son of King Wei - Patron and Exiler of Qu Yuan

King Huai succeeded his father King Wei in 328 BCE, inheriting a Chu state that had become one of the most powerful of the Seven Warring States. His reign would witness both the zenith of Chu's territorial extent and the beginning of its catastrophic decline.

The pivotal disaster came in 313 BCE. The Qin strategist Zhang Yi arrived at the Chu court with a promise: if Chu would break its alliance with Qi, Qin would cede 600 li of territory. King Huai, seduced by this offer, dismissed Qu Yuan's warnings and severed ties with Qi. When he sent envoys to claim the promised land, Zhang Yi claimed he had offered only 6 li - a deliberate humiliation.

In 299 BCE, King Huai accepted an invitation to negotiate with King Zhao of Qin. It was a trap. The Chu king was seized and held hostage, dying in Qin captivity in 296 BCE.

Historical Assessment

  • His reign is viewed as the turning point in Chu's decline
  • Became a cautionary figure: the ruler who ignores loyal advisors
  • His relationship with Qu Yuan exemplifies the classical trope of the faithful minister and inconstant lord
