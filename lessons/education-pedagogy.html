教 The Central Question
問 Can People Be Taught to Be Good?

Every school of Warring States philosophy is, at bottom, a theory of education. The political chaos of the era forced each thinker to answer the same question: how do you produce good people?

Their answers split along a fault line that still structures debates about education today. The Confucians said learning transforms character. The Daoists said learning deforms it. The Mohists said learning needs objective standards. The Legalists said learning is irrelevant.

What follows traces that fault line through each thinker's voice, then lets a Tang dynasty gardener have the last word.

學 Two Models of Learning

The Cultivation Model (Confucian)

Humans have moral potential that needs nurturing. Education draws out what is already there (Mengzi) or shapes raw material into something better (Xunzi). The teacher matters.

The Subtraction Model (Daoist)

Humans are naturally whole. Education adds unnecessary complexity, carving the "uncarved block" into artificial shapes. The best teacher does nothing.

孔子 Kongzi: The Teacher as Model
學 Learning as Pleasure Full page →

Kongzi opens the Analects not with a doctrine but with an invitation. Learning is presented as something intrinsically enjoyable, not a burden imposed from outside. The student who studies and practices what he has learned experiences genuine pleasure.

Kongzi is clear that he is not a prodigy. He claims no innate knowledge. His method is love of the past and relentless effort. This matters pedagogically: if even the Master had to work at it, then learning is available to everyone willing to put in the time.

"Is it not a pleasure to study and practice what you have learned? Is it not a joy to have friends come from afar?" Analects 1.1

The Teacher's Method

Kongzi refuses to spoon-feed. He will not open a door for someone who is not already struggling to get through it. He expects the student to arrive at the threshold of understanding first, then he provides the final push. This is a pedagogy of readiness, not force.

"I do not open up the truth to one who is not eager to get knowledge, nor do I help one who is not anxious to explain himself." Analects 7.8
禮 Ritual as Curriculum

For Kongzi, education is not primarily about acquiring information. It is about forming character through the practice of ritual propriety (li 禮). You learn to be good by doing good things, repeatedly, until they become second nature.

This is education as habituation. The Six Arts (ritual, music, archery, chariot-driving, calligraphy, mathematics) train the whole person, not just the intellect. Music and ritual work on the emotions directly, shaping feeling before it becomes thought.

"I am not one who was born with knowledge. I am one who loves the past and is diligent in seeking it." Analects 7.20

Universal Access

The most radical claim: education should be available to everyone, regardless of social origin. In an aristocratic society where knowledge was a class privilege, this was genuinely subversive.

"In teaching, there should be no class distinction." Analects 15.39 (有教無類)
學而不思則罔，思而不學則殆
"Learning without thinking is useless; thinking without learning is dangerous."
Analects 2.15
孟子 Mengzi: Nurturing What Is Already There
四端 Sprouts, Not Seeds Full page →

Mengzi's theory of the Four Sprouts (si duan 四端) reframes education entirely. If human nature is already good, the teacher's job is not to implant virtue but to nurture what is already growing. Compassion, disdain for shame, deference, and moral discernment are present from birth. They need cultivation, not creation.

The sprout metaphor is precise: a sprout is real but fragile. It can grow into a tree or wither and die. Education is the process of providing the right conditions for growth.

"What people can do without studying is 'best capability'; what they know without pondering is 'best knowledge.' Infants love their parents; as they grow, they respect their elders." Mengzi 7A15
牛山 Ox Mountain and the Pulled Seedlings

The Ox Mountain parable (6A8) is Mengzi's most devastating image of failed education. The mountain was once forested. Axes stripped the trees. Cattle grazed the new shoots. Now people see the barren slope and conclude it was always barren. The same happens to human moral nature under constant assault from a corrupt environment.

The pulled seedlings (2A2) warn against the opposite error: forcing growth. A farmer of Song, anxious to see his crops grow faster, pulled them up by the roots. They all died. Moral development cannot be rushed. The teacher who pushes too hard destroys what they are trying to cultivate.

"There must be activity, but do not correct it; let the mind not forget, but do not help it to grow. Do not be like the man from Song." Mengzi 2A2

Pedagogical Implication

The good teacher creates conditions for growth without interfering with the process itself. Provide the right environment, remove obstacles, but do not pull on the shoots.

荀子 Xunzi: Learning as Transformation
勸學 Encouraging Learning Full page →

Xunzi opens his text with "Encouraging Learning" (Quan xue 勸學), placing education at the very foundation of his philosophy. Where Mengzi sees moral sprouts to nurture, Xunzi sees crooked wood that needs straightening. Human nature is problematic (xing e 性惡). Without deliberate effort (wei 偽), people follow their desires toward conflict.

This makes education not optional but essential. The process of learning is not drawing out what is inside (Mengzi) but adding what is missing. Ritual, music, and the teachings of the sages are the tools that reshape raw human nature into something civilized.

"Learning should never cease. Blue comes from the indigo plant but is bluer than the plant itself. Ice is made of water but is colder than water ever is." Xunzi, "Encouraging Learning"
偽 Deliberate Effort

The character 偽 (wei) is Xunzi's key concept: it means deliberate, conscious effort applied to self-transformation. The word contains 人 (person) and 為 (to act). It is what distinguishes the educated person from the raw one.

The crooked wood analogy drives the point home: wood does not become straight by itself. It must be steamed and pressed against a frame. Once straightened, it stays straight even after the frame is removed. So too with human nature: once shaped by ritual and learning, the shape holds.

"A piece of wood as straight as an ink-line may be bent into a circle as true as a compass. Even after it dries it will not straighten out again, for the bending has made it that way. Similarly, wood that is warped can be steamed and forced straight." Xunzi, "Encouraging Learning"

The Xunzi-Mengzi Debate

Where Mengzi says "don't pull up seedlings," Xunzi says there are no seedlings to pull. The gardener must plant before anything will grow. This is the central pedagogical disagreement in the Confucian tradition: is education cultivation or construction?

道家 The Daoist Critique: Against Learning
老子 Laozi: Unlearn Everything Full page →

The Daodejing inverts the entire Confucian educational project. Where Kongzi says study the ancients, Laozi says abandon sageness. Where Mengzi says cultivate your sprouts, Laozi says return to the uncarved block. The Confucian virtues are not solutions. They are symptoms of the disease they claim to treat.

"When the great Dao is abandoned, benevolence and righteousness appear. When wisdom and cleverness emerge, there is great hypocrisy." Daodejing, Chapter 18

Chapter 48 makes the anti-pedagogical stance explicit: the pursuit of the Dao requires doing less, not more. This is not anti-intellectualism for its own sake. It is the claim that accumulated knowledge creates a screen between the person and reality.

"In pursuit of learning, one knows more every day. In pursuit of the Way, one does less every day. One does less and less until one does nothing. One does nothing, yet nothing is left undone." Daodejing, Chapter 48
莊子 Zhuangzi: Mastery Beyond Teaching Full page →

Zhuangzi does not reject learning outright. He rejects the idea that what matters most can be taught in words. Cook Ding's mastery of butchery transcends anything his teacher could have told him. He meets the ox "with his spirit, not his eyes." The knife finds the joints by itself. This is learning that has passed beyond conscious knowledge into embodied practice.

"What I love is the Way, which goes beyond skill. When I first began cutting up oxen, all I could see was the ox itself. After three years I no longer saw the whole ox. And now I meet it with my spirit and don't look with my eyes." Zhuangzi, Chapter 3 (Cook Ding)

The fish-trap metaphor in Chapter 26 goes further: words and teachings are tools for arriving at understanding. Once you arrive, the tools are useless. Holding onto the teachings after grasping the meaning is like carrying the trap after catching the fish.

"Words exist because of meaning; once you've gotten the meaning, you can forget the words. Where can I find a man who has forgotten words so I can have a word with him?" Zhuangzi, Chapter 26
墨子 Mozi: Standards Over Intuition
法 The Craftsman's Model Full page →

Mozi's critique of Confucian pedagogy is methodological. Confucians educate through immersion in ritual and personal example: the student observes the teacher, absorbs the tradition, develops moral intuition. Mozi objects: intuition varies from person to person. What you need is a compass and square. What you need is an explicit, objective standard (fa 法).

A carpenter does not rely on his "feeling" for straightness. He uses an ink-line. Ethics needs the same precision. The Three Standards (san biao 三表) provide it: historical precedent, empirical evidence, and practical results.

"A blind person can say 'what is bright is white and what is dark is black,' and even the keen-sighted would agree. But mix black and white and ask a blind person to pick them out, and he cannot. It is not that he does not know white from black; he cannot apply his knowledge." Mozi, "Condemning Fatalism"

Meritocracy as Pedagogy

Mozi's doctrine of "Promoting the Worthy" (shang xian 尚賢) is itself a pedagogical claim: competence should determine who teaches and who leads, not birth or social standing. Even farmers and artisans can rise to office if they demonstrate ability. This parallels Kongzi's "no class distinction" in teaching, but grounds it in outcomes rather than moral formation.

非樂 Against the Arts as Education

Where Kongzi sees music and ritual as essential tools of moral education, Mozi sees waste. Court ceremonies with bells and drums produce nothing useful. The resources spent on elaborate performances could feed the hungry. Education should be practical, oriented toward measurable benefit.

This is a genuine clash of educational philosophies. For Kongzi, the arts shape the inner life. For Mozi, the inner life is irrelevant if people are starving. The debate anticipates modern arguments about "soft skills" versus vocational training, liberal arts versus STEM.

"If people regarded others' states as they regard their own, who would attack others' states? ... If people regarded others' families as they regard their own, who would bring disorder to others' families?" Mozi, "Inclusive Care"
韓非子 Han Feizi: Education Is Irrelevant
法 Systems Replace Teachers Full page →

Han Feizi, reputedly Xunzi's student, takes his teacher's premises to their logical extreme. Xunzi said human nature is problematic and needs shaping. Han Feizi agrees. But where Xunzi trusted ritual and education to do the shaping, Han Feizi trusts only law (fa 法), technique (shu 術), and positional power (shi 勢).

The pivot is a loss of faith in teachers. Moral education depends on finding exceptional teachers, and exceptional people are rare. Laws work regardless of who enforces them. A good system does not require good people.

"A ruler must not reveal his desires, for if he does so, his ministers will carve and polish themselves accordingly." Han Feizi, Chapter 5

The Irony

Han Feizi himself was killed by Li Si, a fellow student of Xunzi, in the Qin court that adopted Legalist principles. The system that replaced moral education with bureaucratic control consumed the very thinker who designed it. There is something pedagogical in that outcome, even if Han Feizi would not have appreciated the lesson.

五蠹 Against Confucian Teachers

In "The Five Vermin" (wu du 五蠹), Han Feizi identifies Confucian scholars as parasites on the state. They "use literature to subvert the law." They promote personal virtue over institutional discipline. Their students become people who argue cleverly but produce nothing.

The Legalist state does not need educators producing morally cultivated individuals. It needs a system of rewards and punishments that channels self-interest toward socially useful behavior. Character is irrelevant; compliance is everything.

"As for what the law confers, even the wise cannot shirk from it and even the brave do not dare to dispute it. Punishments for transgressions do not bypass great ministers, while rewards for goodness do not leave the common people behind." Han Feizi, Chapter 6
種樹 The Literary Voice: Camel Kuo the Gardener
柳宗元 Liu Zongyuan: "Camel Kuo the Gardener" Birch p. 258

Liu Zongyuan (773-819 CE), one of the "Eight Great Prose Masters of Tang and Song," wrote this short fictional biography (zhuan 傳) that reads like a philosophical parable wearing the clothes of literary prose. It synthesizes the pedagogical debates of the masters into a single image: a hunchbacked gardener who grows the best trees in the capital.

Guo Tuotuo, nicknamed "Camel" for his hunched back, is famous because his trees flourish while other gardeners' trees wither and die. When asked his secret, he insists there is none. He simply plants trees according to their nature: roots spread out, soil packed firm, then he leaves them alone.

What Other Gardeners Do Wrong

Other gardeners cannot leave their trees alone. They over-water, over-prune, dig up the roots to check whether they've taken hold, scratch the bark to see if the tree is alive. "They love them too much and worry about them too much." Their anxiety produces the very failure they fear.

The Political Application

Asked whether his method can be applied to governing people, Camel Kuo deflects, then admits the parallel. Officials who constantly interfere with the people, issuing decree after decree "for their benefit," are like gardeners who pull up seedlings. Drums and gongs summon people morning and night. The harvest suffers because the farmers spend their time answering bureaucratic demands.

教 The Gardener and the Masters

Liu Zongyuan's essay is not a simple restatement of Daoist wu wei. It draws on the entire tradition, and in doing so, it becomes the most complete literary treatment of pedagogy in the Birch anthology.

Echoing Mengzi

The pulled seedlings of Mengzi 2A2 reappear as the gardeners who dig up roots. The pedagogical lesson is identical: forcing growth kills what you are trying to cultivate. The good teacher, like the good gardener, creates conditions and steps back.

Echoing Laozi

The best ruler in the Daodejing is one "whose existence the people barely know." Camel Kuo embodies this principle at the level of craft: his mastery is invisible. He plants the tree correctly, then does nothing. His inaction is not laziness. It is the product of deep understanding.

Echoing Zhuangzi

Like Cook Ding, Camel Kuo has passed beyond technique into effortless mastery. He does not consult a manual on arboriculture. He meets each tree on its own terms, with a kind of embodied knowledge that cannot be reduced to rules.

Against Xunzi

Xunzi's steamed and pressed wood, forced straight against a frame, stands in direct contrast. Where Xunzi says you must actively reshape nature, Liu Zongyuan's gardener says you must understand nature and align yourself with it. The frame produces a straight board; the gardener produces a living tree.

Against Han Feizi

The bureaucratic meddling that Camel Kuo criticizes is precisely the Legalist system of surveillance and control. Constant inspection, constant regulation, constant interference. The Legalist state is the gardener who scratches the bark every morning to check whether the tree is alive.

"Follow the nature of the tree to realize its potential."

問 Discussion Questions
Question 1

Mengzi says moral sprouts are innate. Xunzi says human nature is problematic. Both are Confucians. Can you construct an educational philosophy that takes both positions seriously, or are they irreconcilable?

Question 2

Laozi's Chapter 48 says the pursuit of the Way requires doing less every day. Is there a version of this that makes sense in an actual classroom? What would "teaching by subtraction" look like in practice?

Question 3

Liu Zongyuan's gardener says over-intervention kills growth. But some students do need active intervention. Where is the line between nurturing and neglect? Between shaping and forcing?

Question 4

Mozi wants objective standards for evaluating doctrines. Confucians rely on cultivated moral intuition. In your own education, which approach has been more effective? Which do you trust more?

Question 5

Han Feizi says good systems don't need good people. Modern institutions (standardized testing, rubrics, compliance training) echo this logic. Does education still need a personal relationship between teacher and student?

Question 6

Zhuangzi's Cook Ding learned his art through years of practice, not from a book. Can the most important things you know actually be taught in words, or did you learn them through experience? What does this imply about the limits of formal education?
