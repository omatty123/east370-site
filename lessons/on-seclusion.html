論隱逸

On Seclusion: Two Letters, a Dream, and a Satire

Birch, pp. 157–169 · Period of Division (220–589 CE)

The Problem of Withdrawal

Between the fall of the Han dynasty (220 CE) and the reunification under the Sui (589 CE), China fragmented into competing kingdoms. For the educated elite, this raised an urgent question: what does a scholar-official owe a state that may be illegitimate, unstable, or morally compromised?

The four texts in this section offer competing answers. Each engages differently with the two dominant philosophical frameworks—Confucianism and Daoism—and each reveals that "seclusion" (yinyi 隱逸) is never merely personal. It is always a political act, a philosophical statement, and a claim about what constitutes a meaningful life.

Key Terms

隱逸 yǐnyì
Reclusion, withdrawal from public life
無為 wúwéi
"Non-action" or effortless action; acting in accord with natural spontaneity
自然 zìrán
"Self-so"; naturalness, spontaneity
性 xìng
Inborn nature; what one is by constitution
報孫會宗書
Letter to Sun Huizong
Yang Yun 楊惲 · Western Han (1st c. BCE) · pp. 159–161 · Trans. Burton Watson

Historical Context

Yang Yun was the grandson of the historian Sima Qian (author of the Shiji). He rose to prominence under Emperor Xuan (r. 74–48 BCE), was enfeoffed as a marquis, and played a key role in exposing a plot by the Huo family to overthrow the emperor. But court politics turned against him: he was accused of treasonable speech, stripped of his title, and banished to the countryside.

His friend Sun Huizong wrote to rebuke him: a gentleman in disgrace should practice "humble and abject seclusion," not throw parties and speculate in grain markets. This letter is Yang Yun's response.

The Philosophical Argument

Confucian Framework Political Ethics

Yang Yun does not reject Confucianism—he deploys it. His key move is to invoke a distinction from the Han Confucian thinker Dong Zhongshu (c. 179–104 BCE):

"To strive with all one's might for benevolence and righteousness, fearful always lest one fail to educate the people—this is the ambition of a statesman. To strive with all one's might for goods and profit, fearful always of poverty and want—this is the business of ordinary people."
— p. 161

Yang's logic: Sun Huizong is a statesman pursuing benevolence (rén 仁); Yang is now an ordinary person pursuing livelihood. They follow different dao. He cites Confucius (Analects 15.40): "Those who do not follow the same road cannot lay plans for each other" (道不同，不相為謀).

This is a Confucian defense of withdrawal—not by rejecting Confucian values, but by arguing that the obligations of the gentleman (junzi 君子) no longer apply once one has been demoted to commoner status. It's a clever rhetorical move: he accepts the framework but exits its jurisdiction.

The Life He Defends

Yang describes his retirement in vivid terms: farming, feasting on holidays, making music with his wife (she plays the lute; he sings in the Qin style), drinking until his ears burn. Then he sings:

"I sowed the southern hill / But I could not keep back the weeds. / I planted an acre of beans / But they fell off the vine, leaving bare stems. / Man's life should be spent in joy; / Why wait in vain for wealth and honor?"
— p. 160

This echoes folk and Daoist traditions of "enjoying the moment" (及時行樂). But Yang frames it as the natural consequence of his changed status, not as philosophical Daoism. He's not a sage hiding from the world—he's a commoner living like one.

The Sting in the Tail

Yang admits to grain speculation—buying cheap, selling dear for 10% profit—and calls it "a vile and merchantly thing." He's not claiming moral purity. The letter's tone mixes self-deprecation with defiance: he knows he's fallen, but he refuses to perform shame. The implicit argument: Sun's standards don't apply to me anymore, so stop judging me by them.

與山巨源絕交書
Letter to Shan Tao (Letter Breaking Off Relations)
Xi Kang 嵇康 · Wei-Jin (3rd c. CE) · pp. 162–166 · Trans. J.R. Hightower

Historical Context

Xi Kang (223–262 CE) was one of the Seven Sages of the Bamboo Grove (竹林七賢)—a group of scholars who gathered to drink, debate philosophy, and refuse participation in the corrupt politics of their time. The Sima family was consolidating power, soon to overthrow the Wei dynasty and establish the Jin. Praising "virtuous usurpers" like the Zhou founders was regime propaganda; criticizing them was dissent.

When Xi Kang's friend Shan Tao left his government post and recommended Xi Kang as his replacement, Xi Kang responded with this letter—a refusal so thorough that it ends their friendship. Xi Kang was later executed, in part for his nonconformity.

The Philosophical Argument
Daoist Anti-Confucian

Unlike Yang Yun, Xi Kang explicitly rejects the Confucian framework. He names his philosophical masters:

"Laozi and Zhuang Zhou are my masters: they held mean positions. I would hardly criticize them. And Confucius, out of his love for all, was ready to hold a coachman's whip."
— p. 162

Note the backhanded treatment of Confucius: praised only for his willingness to take a lowly job—not for his teachings on governance. Xi Kang then attacks the Confucian culture-heroes directly:

"I am always finding fault with Tang and Wu Wang, or running down the Duke of Zhou and Confucius. If I did not stop this in society, it is clear that the religion of the times would not put up with me."
— p. 165

Tang and Wu Wang founded the Shang and Zhou dynasties by overthrowing their predecessors—exactly what the Sima family was about to do. Criticizing these "sage kings" was a political statement as much as a philosophical one.

The Seven Things He Cannot Stand

Xi Kang lists seven aspects of official life incompatible with his nature (xìng 性):

  1. Lying late abed while heralds wait at his door
  2. Walking and singing freely while subordinates restrict his movement
  3. Bowing and kowtowing in formal clothes while infested with lice ("I commonly go half a month without washing my face")
  4. Writing letters and handling business—he hates paperwork
  5. Attending funerals—rituals he finds unbearable
  6. Serving with vulgar people and their "double-dealings"
  7. Having his mind bound with a thousand worldly cares

This list deliberately mixes bodily discomforts (lice, sleepiness) with philosophical objections (hypocrisy, mental bondage). For Xi Kang, there is no separation: his Daoism is about naturalness (zìrán 自然), and his bodily habits are as much "his nature" as his ideas. To force him into ritual propriety would be to violate his very being.

The Wild Deer

"In this I am like the wild deer, which captured young and reared in captivity will be docile and obedient. But if it be caught when full-grown, it will stare wildly and butt against its bonds, dashing into boiling water or fire to escape. You may dress it up with a golden bridle and feed it delicacies, and it will but long the more for its native woods and yearn for rich pasture."
— p. 163

This is the letter's central image. Xi Kang claims he was "spoiled" as a child—never disciplined into Confucian ritual behavior—so his nature is now untameable. Office would be a cage; he would destroy himself trying to escape. The claim is that his rejection of service is not a choice but a necessity of his constitution.

Wuwei as Program

"Of late I have been studying the techniques of prolonging one's life, casting out all ideas of fame and glory, eliminating tastes, and letting my mind wander in stillness: what is most worthwhile to me is Inaction."
— p. 165

Wuwei (無為) is the Daoist term for "non-action" or "effortless action"—acting in accordance with natural spontaneity rather than imposing one's will. For Xi Kang, retirement isn't just personal preference; it's a philosophical program derived from the Laozi and Zhuangzi. To enter office would be to betray his deepest commitments.

桃花源記
Peach Blossom Spring
Tao Qian 陶潛 (Tao Yuanming 陶淵明) · Eastern Jin (c. 421 CE) · pp. 167–168 · Trans. Cyril Birch

The Story

During the Taiyuan reign period (376–396 CE), a fisherman from Wuling follows a stream and loses track of distance. He comes upon a grove of blossoming peach trees—"no tree of any other kind"—and follows the stream to its source. He finds a narrow cave, squeezes through, and emerges into a hidden valley: fertile fields, pleasant ponds, mulberry and willow trees, paths connecting farms, people working contentedly.

The villagers explain: their ancestors fled here during the Qin dynasty (221–206 BCE) to escape the turmoil. They've been isolated ever since. They don't even know the Han dynasty existed, let alone the Wei or Jin. The fisherman stays several days, then leaves—ignoring their request to tell no one. He marks his route, reports to the prefect, who sends officers. They fail. A learned hermit tries. He falls ill and dies. "Since that time there have been no further 'seekers of the ford.'"

Philosophical Resonances

Daoist Primitivism Confucian Allusion Utopian Critique

Daoist Primitivism: The Peach Blossom Spring echoes Laozi chapter 80, which imagines an ideal community: "Let there be a small state with few people... Let them find their food sweet, their clothes beautiful, their homes comfortable, their customs delightful. Though a neighboring state be within sight... the people will grow old and die without ever having been there." Tao Qian's hidden village realizes this vision: small, self-sufficient, cut off from history.

Confucian Allusion: The final phrase—"seekers of the ford" (問津者)—alludes to Analects 18.6. Confucius sends his disciple Zilu to ask two recluses (Changju and Jieni) where to cross a river. They mock Confucius for trying to reform a disordered world: "The whole world is inundated by the same flood. Who can reverse its flow?" Confucius responds: "I cannot associate with birds and beasts. If I cannot associate with people, who can I associate with?" Tao Qian's ending—"no further seekers of the ford"—suggests that the search for utopia, like the search for the crossing, has been abandoned.

Why Can't It Be Found Again?

The story insists the place is real—the fisherman stayed there, ate their food—but unreachable. Several interpretations:

  • Betrayal destroys utopia: The villagers said "Do not speak of us to the people outside." The fisherman told the prefect anyway. His betrayal makes return impossible.
  • Utopia is by definition unreachable: The moment you seek it deliberately, it disappears. This matches the Daoist paradox that seeking the Way (Dao) through effort defeats the purpose.
  • The state destroys what it cannot control: The prefect sends officers—agents of government—and they fail. The hermit (a private seeker) also fails. Perhaps the Peach Blossom Spring can only be stumbled upon, never reached by intention.

The Political Edge

The villagers fled the Qin—the dynasty synonymous with tyranny, book-burning, and forced labor. Tao Qian wrote during the Eastern Jin, a period of weak central government, warlord conflict, and northern invasion. The fantasy of a community that escaped all dynastic history is pointed: it imagines life without the state. The Peach Blossom Spring isn't just a pleasant fable—it's a critique of the entire political order.

北山移文
Proclamation on North Mountain
Kong Zhigui 孔稚珪 · Southern Qi (late 5th c. CE) · pp. 169–173 · Trans. J.R. Hightower

The Satire

This is a satirical piece in ornate parallel prose (pianwen 駢文). The conceit: the spirits of Bell Mountain (Zhongshan 鍾山) issue a formal proclamation denouncing a certain Zhou Yong 周顒, who posed as a recluse—studying Daoist and Buddhist texts, living in a hermitage called "Grass Hut" (Caotang 草堂)—but immediately accepted office when the court summoned him. The mountains themselves bar his return.

The Accusation

Authentic vs. Performative Reclusion as Career

By the fifth century, "reclusion" had become a recognized path to fame. Emperors sought out famous hermits to demonstrate their own virtue; being summoned from retirement was prestigious. Kong Zhigui's satire attacks this system:

"At the present time there is Zhou Tzu [Zhou Yong], / An outstanding man among the vulgar, / Cultured and a scholar, / Philosopher and scribe. / But he needs must / Imitate Yan He's retirement, / Copy Nan-guo's meditation, / Occupy the Grass Hut by imposture, / Usurp a hermit's cap on North Mountain..."
— p. 170

The names Yan He and Nan-guo refer to genuine recluses from the Zhuangzi and Liezi. Zhou Yong is accused of imitating them—performing reclusion for career advancement rather than inner conviction.

Nature Rejects the Hypocrite

"He burned his castalian garments and tore his lotus clothes / He raised a worldly face and carried on in a vulgar manner. / Wind-driven clouds grieved as they carried their anger / Rock-rimmed springs sobbed as they trickled their disappointment / Forests and crags appeared to lack something / Grass and trees seemed to have suffered loss."
— p. 171

The landscape itself mourns the betrayal. Mountains, springs, forests—the very environment of reclusion—reject the man who used them as a prop. The proclamation concludes by ordering the mountains to "bar our mountain windows, / close our cloud-passes" against Zhou Yong's return.

What the Satire Assumes

The piece only works because readers share certain assumptions:

  • There is such a thing as authentic reclusion (the genuine sages invoked throughout)
  • Withdrawal should be motivated by inner conviction, not career strategy
  • The tension between reclusion and office is a real dilemma, not a pose

Kong Zhigui isn't attacking reclusion—he's defending it against those who would cheapen it. The piece presupposes the Xi Kang position (reclusion as authentic Daoist practice) and uses it to condemn fakers. This tells us that by the late fifth century, the discourse of withdrawal had become sophisticated enough to distinguish genuine from performative versions—and that the distinction mattered.

Discussion Questions

  1. Yang Yun claims he's now an "ordinary person," not a statesman. Is this a genuine philosophical move or a rhetorical dodge? Can you simply opt out of the Confucian framework by changing your social position?
  2. Xi Kang mixes bodily complaints (lice, sleepiness) with philosophical objections. Why? What does this tell us about his understanding of "nature" (xìng 性) and the relationship between body and ethics?
  3. The Peach Blossom Spring cannot be found again. Is this because the fisherman betrayed the villagers? Because utopia is inherently unreachable? Because the state destroys what it cannot control? What does your answer reveal about Tao Qian's view of politics?
  4. Kong Zhigui's satire assumes we can distinguish "real" from "fake" recluses. What are the criteria? How would you actually tell the difference?
  5. All four texts assume that withdrawal requires justification. Why? What does this reveal about the default expectations for educated men in early medieval China—and about the power of those expectations?
  6. Compare the relationship to Confucianism across the four texts. Yang Yun uses Confucian categories; Xi Kang rejects them; Tao Qian alludes to them ironically; Kong Zhigui satirizes their misuse. Is there a coherent "philosophy of seclusion" here, or four incompatible positions?
