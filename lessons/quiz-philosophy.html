Question 1 Kongzi
In Analects 4.15, Zengzi explains that the "one thread" (一以貫之) unifying Kongzi's Way consists of:
Zengzi glosses the Master's "one thread" as zhong 忠 (doing one's best for others) and shu 恕 (sympathetic understanding, or not imposing on others what you would not want yourself). This pair captures both the active and restraining sides of ren.
Question 2 Mengzi
Mengzi uses the parable of Ox Mountain (Niu Shan 牛山) to illustrate that:
The mountain was once lush, then stripped bare by axes and grazing cattle. People see it barren and assume it was always that way. Mengzi's point: human goodness is like the trees. It keeps trying to grow back (the "night qi" nourishes new sprouts), but constant assault makes people conclude the goodness was never there. The barren surface is not the nature.
Question 3 Xunzi
"Blue comes from indigo, yet is bluer than indigo" (青出於藍而勝於藍). Xunzi uses this metaphor to argue that:
This appears in Chapter 1, "Exhortation to Learning." The dye is extracted from indigo but surpasses its source. Xunzi's point is about accumulation (積): sustained effort in learning produces results that go beyond the raw material. Learning transforms, and the student can exceed the teacher.
Question 4 Laozi
Chapter 18 of the Daodejing argues that when people praise ren 仁 and yi 義, this actually shows that:
Laozi's logic: "When the great Dao declined, humaneness and rightness appeared." You only need to name a virtue when it has already disappeared from practice. A truly good society would not need the concept of "goodness" because it would simply be how people live. Confucian virtue-talk is a symptom of the Dao's decline, not evidence of moral progress.
Question 5 Zhuangzi
The story of Cook Ding (庖丁) butchering an ox illustrates:
Cook Ding says he no longer sees with his eyes or knows with his mind: "I go at it by spirit." His blade has lasted nineteen years without sharpening because he follows the natural structure rather than forcing. This is Zhuangzi's vision of de 德 as embodied mastery: skill so deep it operates without deliberation, finding the spaces that are already there.
Question 6 Mozi
Mozi's "Three Standards" (san biao 三標) for evaluating any claim are:
The Three Standards are Mozi's test for any doctrine: (1) Does it have a root in the practice of the ancient sage-kings? (2) Does it have a source that ordinary people can verify with their own ears and eyes? (3) Does it have use, meaning practical benefit for the state and people? If a claim fails any of these, it should be rejected. This makes Mozi arguably the most empirical of the classical thinkers.
Question 7 Han Feizi
The story of Mi Zixia and the half-eaten peach illustrates Han Feizi's point that:
When the Duke loved Mi Zixia, sharing a half-eaten peach was praised as an act of devotion ("He forgot his own appetite to share with me!"). When the Duke's love faded, the same story was cited as evidence of disrespect ("He dared feed me his leftovers!"). Han Feizi's lesson: morality is not objective. What counts as virtue or crime depends entirely on the ruler's feelings. This is why systems (fa 法) must replace personal judgment.
Question 8 Cross-tradition
Which thinker adopted the Daoist concept of wu wei (無為) but repurposed it for a radically different political vision?
Han Feizi borrows wu wei from Daoism but gives it a completely different meaning. For Laozi, the ruler does not act because action disturbs the natural order. For Han Feizi, the ruler does not act personally because the system of law (fa), techniques (shu), and positional power (shi) does the work. The ruler stays "empty and still" (虛靜) not out of spiritual wisdom but to remain inscrutable and prevent ministers from manipulating him.
Question 9 Mengzi vs. Xunzi
Mengzi and Xunzi disagree about whether human nature is good or problematic. However, they agree that:
Despite their famous disagreement about human nature, both Mengzi and Xunzi insist that moral development requires effort over time. Mengzi says the sprouts must be cultivated (like watering seedlings, not pulling them). Xunzi says nature must be reshaped through deliberate effort (wei 偽) and ritual. Neither believes goodness happens automatically. The disagreement is about the starting material, not about whether cultivation is necessary.
Question 10 Mozi vs. Kongzi
Mozi's most direct critique of Confucianism targeted:
Mozi's doctrines of Moderation in Burial (節葬) and Against Music (非樂) are directly aimed at Confucian ritual culture. The Confucians emphasized elaborate mourning rites (three-year mourning, expensive funerals) and court music as expressions of li 禮. Mozi argued these drain the state's resources, keep people from productive labor, and serve only the elite. His test: does it benefit the people? If not, eliminate it.
