Book One
梁惠王

King Hui of Liang

Key Terms

Chinese Pinyin Meaning
仁 ren Benevolence; the cardinal Confucian virtue
義 yi Righteousness; doing what is proper
利 li Profit; what Mengzi warns against prioritizing
王道 wang dao "Kingly Way"; rule through virtue

Book 1 Synopsis

1A1 — Profit vs. Benevolence: King Hui asks how to "profit" his state. Mengzi: "Why say profit? Let there be benevolence and righteousness." If everyone seeks profit, the state tears apart.

1A7 — The Ox and Bell: King Xuan spares an ox from slaughter. Mengzi shows he already has the "heart that cannot bear suffering"—he simply needs to extend it from animals to people.

1B8 — On Regicide: Asked about killing tyrant Zhou: "I have heard of executing a fellow named Zhou, not killing a ruler." A tyrant is no king.

何必曰利
"Why must you speak of profit?"
Mengzi's opening challenge to King Hui (1A1)
Book Two
公孫丑

Gongsun Chou

Key Terms

Chinese Pinyin Meaning
浩然之氣 hao ran zhi qi "Flood-like qi"; energy of the righteous
四端 si duan The Four Sprouts of virtue
惻隱之心 ce yin zhi xin Heart of compassion; sprout of benevolence
揠苗助長 ya miao zhu zhang "Pulling up seedlings"; forcing growth

Book 2 Synopsis

2A2 — Flood-like Qi: Mengzi cultivates vast energy through righteous action. But this cannot be forced—a farmer of Song pulled seedlings to help them grow and killed them all. Moral cultivation requires patience.

2A6 — The Four Sprouts: All humans have innate moral sprouts: compassion (to benevolence), disdain (to righteousness), deference (to propriety), right/wrong (to wisdom). As natural as four limbs.

揠苗助長
"Pulling up seedlings to help them grow"
Still used today to warn against forcing development (2A2)

The Four Sprouts (四端)

Compassion -> Benevolence (仁)

惻隱之心 — The heart that cannot bear others' suffering becomes benevolence when cultivated.

Disdain -> Righteousness (義)

羞惡之心 — Disdain for what is shameful develops into righteousness.

Deference -> Propriety (禮)

辭讓之心 — The instinct to yield becomes proper ritual conduct.

Discernment -> Wisdom (智)

是非之心 — The sense of right and wrong develops into wisdom.

Book Three
滕文公

Duke Wen of Teng

Key Terms

Chinese Pinyin Meaning
井田 jing tian "Well-field" land system
勞心者 lao xin zhe "Those who labor with minds"
勞力者 lao li zhe "Those who labor with strength"

Book 3 Synopsis

3A3 — Well-Field System: Eight families farm outer plots; jointly cultivate center for the lord. Benevolent government provides material welfare as foundation for moral education.

3A4 — Debate with Xu Xing: Agriculturalist says rulers should farm alongside subjects. Mengzi: "Does he weave his cloth? Make his pots?" Division of labor is natural. "Those who labor with minds govern; those with strength are governed."

"Those who labor with their minds govern others; those who labor with their strength are governed by others. Those governed feed others; those who govern are fed by others." — Mengzi 3A4
Book Six
告子

Gaozi

Key Terms

Chinese Pinyin Meaning
性 xing Human nature
性善 xing shan "Human nature is good"
牛山 niu shan Ox Mountain; famous parable
夜氣 ye qi "Night qi"; restorative moral energy

Book 6 Synopsis

6A1-4 — Debates with Gaozi: Gaozi: nature is like water—no direction, flows where channeled. Mengzi: water has no east/west preference, but certainly flows downward! Nature tends toward good. Evil is like forcing water uphill.

6A8 — Ox Mountain: Trees were once beautiful; hatchets stripped them, grazing prevented regrowth. Seeing the barren mountain, people assume it was always so. "Night qi" (moral renewal in rest) shows goodness constantly resurfaces, only to be cut down daily.

人性之善也，猶水之就下也
"Human nature tends toward goodness as water tends downward."
Mengzi's definitive statement on human nature (6A2)
Book Seven
盡心

Jin Xin (Fully Applying the Heart)

Key Terms

Chinese Pinyin Meaning
盡心 jin xin Fully applying the heart
良知 liang zhi Innate moral knowing
良能 liang neng Innate moral ability

Book 7 Synopsis

7A1 — Understanding Nature: "To fully apply one's heart is to understand one's nature. To understand nature is to understand Heaven." Introspection reveals moral nature reveals cosmic order.

7A15 — Innate Knowledge: What people can do without studying is "best capability"; what they know without pondering is "best knowledge." Infants love parents; children respect elders. Extend these to the world.

7A26 — Against Extremes: Yang Zhu (egoism) and Mozi (impartial love) are opposites. Zimo tried the middle. But "holding the middle without discretion is holding one extreme." Virtue requires judgment.

盡其心者，知其性也
"To fully apply one's heart is to understand one's nature."
The path from self-cultivation to cosmic understanding (7A1)

Major Themes Across the Text

Theme Key Passages Core Argument
Human Nature is Good 2A6, 6A1-8 Innate moral "sprouts" develop into virtue when nurtured. Evil results from neglect, not depravity.
Benevolent Government 1A1, 1A3, 1A7 Rulers prioritize people's welfare over profit. Material security enables moral cultivation.
Extension of Virtue 1A7, 2A6, 7A15 Extend innate compassion outward: family to community, humans to animals, near to far.
Right to Revolution 1B8 A tyrant loses the Mandate of Heaven—becomes a "fellow" whom subjects may remove.
Division of Labor 3A4 Society divides into those who govern and those governed. Specialization is natural.

Discussion Questions

Question 1

Mengzi claims human nature is good. What is his evidence? How does he respond to counter-examples like cruel tyrants?

Question 2

In 1A7, Mengzi distinguishes "cannot" from "will not." How does the ox-and-bell story illustrate this?

Question 3

What is the relationship between material welfare and moral cultivation? Can people be virtuous while hungry?

Question 4

Compare the "sprout" metaphor with the "Ox Mountain" parable. What do these suggest about moral development?

Question 5

Mengzi's doctrine that a tyrant is merely a "fellow" was controversial. What are the implications? How might it be misused?

Question 6

In his debate with Xu Xing (3A4), Mengzi defends division of labor. Is his argument convincing?
