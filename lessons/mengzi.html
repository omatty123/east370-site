Book One
梁惠王

King Hui of Liang

Passages 1A1, 1A3, 1A7, 1B8

Key Terms

Chinese Pinyin Meaning
仁 rén Benevolence, humaneness; the cardinal Confucian virtue
義 yì Righteousness, moral rightness; doing what is proper
利 lì Profit, benefit, advantage; what Mengzi warns against prioritizing
王道 wáng dào "The Kingly Way"; rule through virtue rather than force
不忍人之心 bù rěn rén zhī xīn "A heart that cannot bear others' suffering"; innate compassion

Synopsis

1A1 — Profit vs. Benevolence: The book opens with King Hui asking Mengzi how to "profit" his state. Mengzi's famous reply: "Why must Your Majesty say 'profit'? Let there be benevolence and righteousness, and that is all." If everyone seeks profit, the state will be torn apart by competing interests. This establishes the core Mencian teaching that ethics must come before expediency.

1A7 — The Ox and the Bell: King Xuan of Qi spared an ox from being slaughtered for a bell-consecration ceremony. Mengzi uses this incident to show the king already possesses the "heart that cannot bear" seeing others suffer—he simply needs to extend this compassion from animals to his people. The famous analogy: failing to care for the people is not "cannot" (不能) but "will not" (不為).

1B8 — On Regicide: When asked about the killing of the tyrant Zhou of Shang, Mengzi shockingly declares: "I have heard of the execution of a 'fellow' named Zhou, but I have not heard of the killing of a ruler." A king who abandons benevolence and righteousness is no longer a king—he becomes a mere "fellow" (一夫) whom it is righteous to remove.

何必曰利
hé bì yuē lì
"Why must you speak of profit?"
Mengzi's opening challenge to King Hui (1A1)

Major Themes

1

Ethics Over Expediency

Mengzi argues that pursuing profit (利) leads to social disintegration, while cultivating benevolence (仁) and righteousness (義) naturally produces prosperity.

2

Extension of Compassion

The moral task is not acquiring new virtues but extending our innate compassion from near to far, from family to strangers, from animals to all people.

3

Conditional Sovereignty

Legitimacy depends on virtue. A ruler who tyrannizes loses the Mandate of Heaven and may rightfully be deposed—a radical political doctrine.

Book Two
公孫丑

Gongsun Chou

Passages 2A2, 2A6, 2B13

Key Terms

Chinese Pinyin Meaning
浩然之氣 hào rán zhī qì "Flood-like qi"; the vital energy that fills one who acts righteously
四端 sì duān The "Four Sprouts/Beginnings" of virtue innate in all humans
惻隱之心 cè yǐn zhī xīn Heart of compassion; feeling of alarm and sympathy; sprout of benevolence (仁)
揠苗助長 yà miáo zhù zhǎng "Pulling up seedlings to help them grow"; forcing moral development

Synopsis

2A2 — Flood-like Qi and the Song Farmer: Mengzi describes cultivating "flood-like qi" (浩然之氣)—a vast, vital energy that fills one who consistently acts righteously. But this cannot be forced. The famous parable: a farmer of Song pulled on his seedlings to help them grow faster, only to kill them all. Moral cultivation requires steady, patient effort—not anxious striving.

2A6 — The Four Sprouts: Mengzi's most systematic statement on human nature. All humans possess four innate moral "sprouts": compassion (leading to benevolence), disdain for shameful conduct (to righteousness), deference (to ritual propriety), and the sense of right and wrong (to wisdom). These are as natural to humans as four limbs. The moral task is nurturing these sprouts to full growth.

揠苗助長
yà miáo zhù zhǎng
"Pulling up seedlings to help them grow"
Still used today to warn against forcing development (2A2)

The Four Sprouts (四端)

仁

Compassion → Benevolence

惻隱之心 (cè yǐn zhī xīn) — The heart that cannot bear to see others suffer becomes the virtue of benevolence when cultivated.

義

Disdain → Righteousness

羞惡之心 (xiū wù zhī xīn) — The heart of disdain for what is shameful develops into the virtue of righteousness.

禮

Deference → Ritual Propriety

辭讓之心 (cí ràng zhī xīn) — The instinct to defer and yield to others becomes proper ritual conduct.

智

Discernment → Wisdom

是非之心 (shì fēi zhī xīn) — The innate sense of right and wrong develops into moral wisdom.

Book Three
滕文公

Duke Wen of Teng

Passages 3A3, 3A4, 3A5

Key Terms

Chinese Pinyin Meaning
井田 jǐng tián "Well-field" system; idealized land distribution in nine squares
勞心者 láo xīn zhě "Those who labor with their minds"; the ruling/scholarly class
勞力者 láo lì zhě "Those who labor with their strength"; farmers and workers
農家 nóng jiā The "Agriculturalist" school; Xu Xing's philosophy

Synopsis

3A3 — The Well-Field System: Mengzi outlines the idealized "well-field" (井田) land system where eight families farm outer plots while jointly cultivating a central plot for the lord. This represents benevolent government providing for the people's material welfare as a foundation for moral education.

3A4 — Debate with Xu Xing: The Agriculturalist Xu Xing argues that rulers should farm alongside their subjects. Mengzi's devastating response: "Does Xu Xing weave his own cloth? Make his own pots? Forge his own tools?" Division of labor is natural and necessary. "Those who labor with their minds govern others; those who labor with their strength are governed by others." Society requires specialization.

3A5 — Debate with the Mohist Yi Zhi: The Mohist Yi Zhi, who had converted to Confucianism, gave his parents an elaborate burial despite Mohist teachings against such extravagance. When confronted about this inconsistency, Mengzi defends him: ancient people, seeing wild animals devouring their parents' corpses, wept and buried them—not from convention but from genuine feeling. Ritual emerges from authentic human emotion, not arbitrary rules.

"Those who labor with their minds govern others; those who labor with their strength are governed by others. Those who are governed by others feed others; those who govern others are fed by others. This is a principle universally recognized." — Mengzi 3A4
Book Four
離婁

Li Lou

Passages 4A17, 4A27, 4B12, 4B19, 4B28

Key Terms

Chinese Pinyin Meaning
權 quán Discretion; "weighing" circumstances to determine proper action
赤子之心 chì zǐ zhī xīn "Heart of a child"; innate moral innocence great people preserve
君子 jūn zǐ Gentleman; morally cultivated person; exemplary person

Synopsis

4A17 — Discretion (Drowning Sister-in-Law): Chunyu Kun challenges Mengzi: ritual says men and women should not touch when handing things to each other—but what if your sister-in-law is drowning? Mengzi replies: "To not pull your sister-in-law out when she is drowning is to be a beast." Following ritual is proper, but saving her with your hand is discretion (權). Virtue requires contextual judgment, not rigid rule-following.

4A27 — The Core of Virtue: The core of benevolence is serving one's parents; the core of righteousness is obeying one's elder brother. If one delights in these, "they grow... without realizing it one's feet begin to step in time to them and one's hands dance according to their rhythms."

4B12 — Heart of a Child: "Great people are those who do not lose the hearts of their children." The sage preserves the innate moral innocence that ordinary people abandon through worldly corruption.

4B19 — Human Distinctiveness: "That by which humans differ from birds and beasts is slight. The people abandon it. The gentleman preserves it." What makes us human is our moral potential—easily lost if not cultivated.

權
quán
"Discretion" — weighing circumstances to find the right action
The virtue of contextual moral judgment (4A17)
Book Five
萬章

Wan Zhang

Passages 5A2, 5A9

Key Terms

Chinese Pinyin Meaning
舜 Shùn Sage-king Shun; paradigm of filial piety despite wicked family
人倫 rén lún Human relations; the five relationships defining ethical life

Synopsis

5A2 — Shun's Marriage: Wan Zhang asks: the Odes say one must inform parents before marriage, yet sage Shun married without informing his. How? Mengzi explains: Shun's parents were wicked and would have prevented it. "For a man and woman to dwell together in one home is the greatest of human relations." To inform them would mean abandoning this greatest relation, causing resentment. The sage navigates impossible circumstances with discretion.

5A9 — Wisdom of Boli Xi: Did the sage Boli Xi really sell himself to a cattle herder to meet Duke Mu? Mengzi says no—this story was "fabricated by those obsessed with taking office." Boli Xi knew when a ruler could not be advised, when to leave, and when a good ruler arose. "To sell oneself so as to accomplish things for one's lord—even a villager who cared for himself would not do this."

Book Six
告子

Gaozi

Passages 6A1–4, 6A6, 6A8

Key Terms

Chinese Pinyin Meaning
性 xìng Human nature; innate tendencies and capacities
性善 xìng shàn "Human nature is good"; Mengzi's core thesis
牛山 niú shān Ox Mountain; setting of famous parable on human nature
夜氣 yè qì "Night qi"; the restorative energy of rest that renews moral sense
良心 liáng xīn "Original/good heart"; innate moral conscience

Synopsis

6A1–4 — Debates with Gaozi: Gaozi argues human nature is like water—it has no inherent direction (toward good or evil) and simply flows where channeled. Mengzi counters: water indeed has no preference for east or west, but it certainly flows downward! Human nature similarly tends toward goodness. Evil is like forcing water uphill—possible, but against its nature.

6A6 — Universal Moral Taste: Just as mouths agree on tastes and ears on sounds, minds agree on what is right and wrong. The sages simply discovered first what our minds already approve. Morality is not arbitrary convention but reflects our shared human nature.

6A8 — The Ox Mountain Parable: The trees of Ox Mountain were once beautiful, but hatchets stripped them bare, and grazing prevented regrowth. Seeing the barren mountain, people assume it was always so. Similarly, when we see people behaving badly, we assume humans are naturally evil. But the "night qi" (夜氣)—the moral renewal that comes with rest—shows that goodness constantly tries to resurface, only to be cut down again by daily circumstances.

人性之善也，猶水之就下也
rén xìng zhī shàn yě, yóu shuǐ zhī jiù xià yě
"Human nature tends toward goodness as water tends downward."
Mengzi's definitive statement on human nature (6A2)
Book Seven
盡心

Jin Xin (Fully Applying the Heart)

Passages 7A1, 7A15, 7A26, 7B31

Key Terms

Chinese Pinyin Meaning
盡心 jìn xīn "Fully applying the heart"; complete moral engagement
良知 liáng zhī "Best knowledge"; innate moral knowing without deliberation
良能 liáng néng "Best capability"; innate moral ability without learning
命 mìng Fate; mandate; what is beyond human control

Synopsis

7A1 — Understanding Nature and Heaven: "To fully apply one's heart is to understand one's nature. If one understands one's nature, then one understands Heaven." This opening passage of Book 7 lays out the path of self-cultivation: introspection leads to understanding our moral nature, which reveals our connection to the cosmic order.

7A15 — Innate Knowledge and Ability: "What people are capable of without studying is their best capability. What they know without pondering is their best knowledge." Infants naturally love their parents; children naturally respect elders. These innate moral responses are the foundation—we need only "extend these to the world."

7A26 — Against Extremes: Yang Zhu was "for ourselves" (egoism); Mozi was for "impartial caring" (universal love). Zimo tried holding to the middle. But "holding to the middle without discretion is the same as holding to one extreme." True virtue requires judgment, not formulaic positions.

7B31 — Extension of Moral Reactions: "People all have things they will not bear. To extend this reaction to that which they will bear is benevolence." The task of moral cultivation is extending our natural reactions from paradigmatic cases to new situations—filling out our innate moral dispositions.

盡其心者，知其性也
jìn qí xīn zhě, zhī qí xìng yě
"To fully apply one's heart is to understand one's nature."
The path from self-cultivation to cosmic understanding (7A1)

Major Themes Across the Text

Theme Key Passages Core Argument
Human Nature is Good 2A6, 6A1–8 All humans possess innate moral "sprouts" that naturally develop into virtue when properly nurtured. Evil results from neglect or bad environment, not inherent depravity.
Benevolent Government 1A1, 1A3, 1A7, 3A3 Rulers must prioritize the people's welfare over profit. The "Kingly Way" (王道) rules through virtue, not force. Material security enables moral cultivation.
Extension of Virtue 1A7, 2A6, 7A15 Moral growth means extending our innate compassion outward: from family to community, from humans to animals, from near to far.
Moral Cultivation 2A2, 6A8 Virtue requires steady cultivation like growing plants—forcing development (揠苗助長) kills it. The goal is nurturing innate goodness, not acquiring foreign virtues.
Right to Revolution 1B8, 5A5 Sovereignty depends on virtue. A tyrannical ruler loses the Mandate of Heaven and becomes a mere "fellow" (一夫) whom subjects may rightfully remove.
Division of Labor 3A4 Society naturally divides into those who govern and those who are governed. This specialization is necessary and legitimate when rulers fulfill their responsibilities.

Discussion Questions

Question 1

Mengzi famously claims that human nature is good. What is his evidence for this? How does he respond to counter-examples like cruel tyrants or ordinary people doing evil?

Question 2

In 1A7, Mengzi distinguishes between "cannot" (不能) and "will not" (不為). What is the significance of this distinction for his moral philosophy? How does the ox-and-bell story illustrate it?

Question 3

What is Mengzi's view on the relationship between material welfare and moral cultivation? Can people be virtuous while hungry? What role should government play?

Question 4

Compare Mengzi's "sprout" (端) metaphor with the "Ox Mountain" (牛山) parable. What do these different images suggest about the nature of moral development and the causes of moral failure?

Question 5

Mengzi's doctrine that a tyrannical king is no longer a "king" but merely a "fellow" (一夫) was controversial. What are the implications of this view? How might it be used—or misused?

Question 6

In his debate with Xu Xing (3A4), Mengzi defends social hierarchy and division of labor. Is his argument convincing? What assumptions does it rest on?
